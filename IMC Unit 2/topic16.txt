What are the advantages of <b>indirect</b> (collective) investing over direct investing?	(1) Lower cost through pooling; (2) professional fund management expertise; (3) greater diversification; (4) specialisation possible; (5) access to foreign investments. Also three transformations: <b>size</b> (small sums → large investments), <b>maturity</b> (short-term savings → long-term investments), <b>risk</b> (diversification & analysis).	Topic16 16.1.1 DirectIndirect
What are the <b>disadvantages</b> of collective investment vehicles?	(1) Individual cannot choose specific investments held; (2) fund manager performance varies widely across funds and over time; (3) additional fees/charges reduce returns; (4) possible underperformance vs benchmark.	Topic16 16.1.1 DirectIndirect
Why does <b>direct investment</b> form only a small proportion of lending?	Mismatch between savers (short-term, liquid, low-risk) and borrowers (long-term, illiquid, higher-risk). Also higher transaction costs (search, monitoring, due diligence) and <b>asymmetric information</b> — the borrower knows more about risk/return than the saver.	Topic16 16.1.1 DirectIndirect
Name the three types of <b>financial intermediary</b>.	(1) <b>Banks</b> (depository institutions) — lend directly; (2) <b>Contractual savings</b> — pension and life funds; (3) <b>Investment intermediaries</b> — unit trusts, hedge funds.	Topic16 16.1.1 DirectIndirect
Name the <b>four main types</b> of collective investment vehicle in the UK.	(1) <b>OEICs</b> (open-ended investment companies / ICVCs); (2) <b>Unit trusts</b>; (3) <b>Investment trusts</b>; (4) <b>Life assurance-based</b> schemes.	Topic16 16.1.2 CollectiveTypes
What is the key difference between <b>open-ended</b> and <b>closed-ended</b> funds?	<b>Open-ended</b> (unit trusts, OEICs): issue new units/shares when investors subscribe and cancel them on redemption — fund size varies.<br><b>Closed-ended</b> (investment trusts): fixed number of shares; new investors buy from existing shareholders on the stock exchange.	Topic16 16.1.2 CollectiveTypes
What are the <b>three distinct roles</b> in a unit trust?	(1) <b>Unit trust manager</b> — defines terms, markets the trust, receives payments, appoints trustees; (2) <b>Trustee</b> (usually a bank/insurance co.) — holds legal title to underlying assets, safeguards investors; (3) <b>Investment manager</b> — buys/sells securities, provides valuations. Manager and investment manager may be the same firm.	Topic16 16.1.2 UnitTrusts
Explain <b>dual pricing</b> vs <b>single pricing</b> for unit trusts.	<b>Dual</b>: investors buy at a higher <b>offer</b> price and sell at a lower <b>bid</b> price; spread can be up to 9% (commission, dealing charges, stamp duty, VAT).<br><b>Single</b>: one mid-market price; dealing costs recouped via a <b>dilution levy</b> or <b>swing pricing</b> to protect existing holders from the costs of net inflows/outflows.	Topic16 16.1.2 UnitTrusts
What is an <b>OEIC</b> and how does it combine features of unit trusts and investment trusts?	An OEIC (or ICVC) is <b>open-ended</b> like a unit trust (units vary daily) and its price reflects NAV, but it has a <b>company structure</b> like an investment trust. Assets held by a <b>depository</b> (not a trustee), which must be an FCA-authorised firm independent of the OEIC. OEICs can use dual or single pricing.	Topic16 16.1.2 OEICs
What is a <b>regulated</b> collective investment scheme in the UK? What distinguishes it from unregulated?	Regulated = (a) an ICVC/OEIC, (b) an authorised unit trust, or (c) a recognised overseas scheme. <b>Unregulated</b> schemes cannot be marketed to <b>retail investors</b>. All are regulated by the <b>FCA</b>.	Topic16 16.1.2 Regulation
Name the three categories of <b>authorised</b> collective investment scheme.	(1) <b>UCITS</b> scheme (meets UCITS Directives); (2) <b>Qualified Investor Scheme</b> (QIS) — professional investors only; (3) <b>Non-UCITS Retail Scheme</b> (NURS).	Topic16 16.1.2 Regulation
State the key <b>UCITS investment and borrowing limits</b>.	Max <b>5%</b> in transferable securities of one issuer (can rise to 10% for up to 4 holdings = 40% total). Borrowing limited to <b>10% of NAV</b>. Up to 100% in other CIS but max 20% in any one scheme. No more than 35% in one government issuer (unless 6+ issuers, with no single issue >30%). Overconcentration limits: max 10% of non-voting shares, 10% of debt, 10% of money-market instruments, 25% of units in any one CIS per issuer.	Topic16 16.1.2 UCITS
What is an <b>ETF</b> and how does it differ from an index tracker unit trust?	An ETF is a fund traded on a stock exchange whose assets mirror an underlying index, sector, commodity, or other benchmark. Unlike a unit trust tracker (priced once a day), an ETF trades <b>continuously</b> throughout the day at market prices close to NAV. ETFs disclose holdings daily; unit trusts disclose with a time lag.	Topic16 16.1.3 ETFs
How do <b>Authorised Participants (APs)</b> keep ETF prices close to NAV?	APs create shares by delivering a <b>creation basket</b> of securities to the ETF manager in exchange for ETF shares (typically blocks of 50,000 = 'creation units'). They redeem by delivering ETF shares for a <b>redemption basket</b>. This arbitrage mechanism keeps the ETF price close to NAV. Factors affecting the spread: cost of arbitrage, price volatility, and liquidity of underlying securities.	Topic16 16.1.3 ETFs
What is a <b>synthetic ETF</b> and what are its risks?	A synthetic ETF does <b>not</b> hold the underlying assets. Instead, an investment bank uses <b>derivative contracts</b> (swaps) to deliver the index return, with a basket of collateral held by a third party. Risks: <b>counterparty risk</b> (provider default), collateral may be illiquid or unrelated to the index, potential systemic risk if large-scale redemptions occur in stressed markets ('adverse feedback loops'). Criticised by the IMF and Financial Stability Board.	Topic16 16.1.3 SyntheticETFs
List the <b>advantages</b> of ETFs.	(1) Instant diversification in a single transaction across many asset classes; (2) tradeable like shares — can be sold short and bought on margin; (3) <b>intraday liquidity</b> at market prices (vs once-a-day for unit trusts); (4) <b>low cost</b> — average index ETF expense ratio ~0.16% vs ~0.66% for actively managed mutual funds; (5) high <b>transparency</b> (daily disclosure); (6) no stamp duty (unlike unit trusts).	Topic16 16.1.4 ETFs
List the <b>disadvantages</b> of ETFs.	(1) May expose investors to unfamiliar asset classes with poorly understood risk/return drivers; (2) leveraged ETFs not well understood; (3) costs include bid–offer spreads, broker commissions, and possible premia/discounts to NAV; (4) potential systemic liquidity risk — if many investors exit fixed-income ETFs simultaneously, underlying bond liquidity may be insufficient.	Topic16 16.1.4 ETFs
What is an <b>exchange-traded commodity (ETC)</b> and how does it relate to an <b>ETN</b>?	An ETC is an exchange-traded note (ETN) tracking individual commodities or commodity indices. An ETN is a senior, unsubordinated <b>debt instrument</b> issued by a single bank. The bank agrees to pay the benchmark return minus fees. Investors bear <b>credit risk of the issuing bank</b>. ETCs can be physically backed by the commodity or track futures indices. Both ETCs and ETNs are typically collateralised with government bonds and/or blue-chip stocks in a ring-fenced account.	Topic16 16.1.3 ETCs
What are <b>smart beta</b> / style ETFs?	Rule-based (systematic) portfolios offered as ETFs, using financial criteria other than market-cap weighting — e.g. value, small-cap, low volatility. 'Fundamental indices' combine metrics into a single ranking. They represent a shift from stock-picking toward macro-economic and thematic strategies.	Topic16 16.1.3 SmartBeta
Describe the key features of an <b>investment trust company (ITC)</b>.	Despite the name, ITCs are <b>limited liability companies</b> listed on the London Stock Exchange, regulated by the Companies Act 2006 (not trust law). They are <b>closed-ended</b>: fixed shares, no new shares on purchase (unless secondary issue). Board of directors sets strategy; management executes. Max <b>15%</b> in any one company. Can <b>borrow/gear</b> at fixed interest rates, amplifying gains and losses. Fee ~1.2% of fund value (AIC weighted average). Shares can trade at a <b>premium or discount</b> to NAV. ITCs may have <b>split capital</b> structures with different share classes (capital shares, income shares, zero-dividend preference shares).	Topic16 16.1.2 InvestmentTrusts
What is the <b>stewardship</b> debate around passive ETF providers?	Since passive ETFs track indices (no stock selection), should providers still engage as responsible shareholders? The consensus is that 'passive' should refer to <b>investment style</b>, not <b>engagement style</b> — large passive holders should still exercise stewardship on governance, remuneration, and climate risk, given their significant collective ownership.	Topic16 16.1.3 Stewardship
What four services do <b>private client stockbrokers</b> provide?	(1) <b>Execution-only</b> — trade execution with no advice; (2) <b>Advisory dealing</b> — execution plus advice on proposed transactions; (3) <b>Portfolio advisory</b> — holistic advice on portfolio construction, but client makes final decisions; (4) <b>Discretionary</b> — broker has full authority to buy/sell on client's behalf without prior approval.	Topic16 16.1.5 PrivateClient
Name two benchmark index series for <b>private client funds</b>.	(1) <b>ARC Private Client Indices</b> — four risk categories: Cautious (0–40% equity risk), Balanced Asset (40–60%), Steady Growth (60–80%), Equity Risk (80–110%).<br>(2) <b>MSCI PIMFA Private Investor Index Series</b> — five composites: Conservative, Balanced, Income, Growth, Global Growth. Weightings set by PIMFA committee reflecting industry strategy views.	Topic16 16.1.5 PrivateClient
What is a <b>structured product</b>? Name the three main types.	An investment with a well-defined risk/return objective linked to a specific underlying asset (e.g. FTSE 100) over a fixed calendar period (typically 3–6 years). Usually created via derivatives; investors typically forgo dividends.<br>(1) <b>Structured deposit</b> — fixed-term deposit, interest linked to an index, capital guaranteed at maturity, deposit insurance applies if issuer is deposit-taker.<br>(2) <b>Structured capital protected</b> — similar but <b>no</b> deposit insurance guarantee if counterparty defaults.<br>(3) <b>Structured capital-at-risk</b> — capital reduced if underlying breaches specified <b>barrier levels</b>.	Topic16 16.1.5 StructuredProducts
How are structured products typically <b>constructed</b> to provide capital guarantees?	The guaranteed capital sum is invested in a <b>zero-coupon bond</b> maturing at the end of the investment term. The remainder is split between management charges and a <b>call option</b> on the underlying index (strike = initial index level). If the index falls, the zero-coupon bond returns the capital and the option expires worthless. If it rises, the investor receives the bond proceeds plus the option profit.	Topic16 16.1.5 StructuredProducts
What are <b>barrier levels</b> in structured products?	Key index levels below which losses are triggered. Can apply at <b>maturity date only</b> (end of term) or <b>throughout the life</b> of the product (full term). E.g. if barrier = 60% of initial index and the index falls below this, capital returned may be reduced.	Topic16 16.1.5 StructuredProducts
What is a <b>wrap account</b> / platform?	Software that simplifies administration, management and reporting of an investor's holdings and tax wrappers. Provides a single view of portfolio valuation, asset allocation, dealing history, adviser charges, and performance vs benchmark. 'Open-architecture' platforms offer access across OEICs, ETFs, investment trusts, shares, bonds, and cash. Since April 2016, adviser, fund, and platform charging must be fully <b>unbundled</b> (separately itemised). A 'fund supermarket' is an electronic platform for purchasing/selling collective funds online, including ISAs.	Topic16 16.1.5 Platforms
What is <b>NS&I</b>?	National Savings & Investments — a state-owned savings bank and agency of HM Treasury. Aims to attract funds from individual savers to help fund government. Products: savings certificates, fixed-interest certificates, income bonds, premium bonds, ISAs, inflation index-linked savings, guaranteed equity bonds. All carry a <b>100% HM Treasury guarantee</b>, and many have tax-free elements.	Topic16 16.1.6 NSandI
Distinguish <b>life assurance</b> (long-term) from <b>general insurance</b> (short-term) business.	<b>General insurance</b>: short-term contracts, backed by more <b>liquid</b> assets.<br><b>Life assurance</b> (long-term): mix of life insurance and long-term savings. Purchasers pay a premium for a lump sum at death (<b>whole life</b>) or at the end of a specified period (<b>endowment</b>). Payouts can be fixed or depend on investment returns (<b>'with profits'</b> — basic sum assured + investment bonus). Premiums invested in long-term assets: UK/overseas equities, corporate bonds, property.	Topic16 16.1.6 LifeAssurance
Compare <b>defined contribution (DC)</b> vs <b>defined benefit (DB)</b> pensions.	<b>DC</b>: contributions fixed; benefits depend on <b>investment performance</b> and annuity rates at retirement. Investment risk borne by the <b>individual</b>. If no annuity purchased (flexible drawdown), faces <b>longevity risk</b> — possibility of running out of money.<br><b>DB</b>: employer promises a pension related to <b>final salary and years of service</b>. Investment risk borne by the <b>employer</b>. DB liabilities are <b>inversely related</b> to interest rates (low rates → higher present value of liabilities → scheme may become unviable). DC scheme involves <b>no liability</b> for the employer.	Topic16 16.1.7 Pensions
What is <b>longevity risk</b> in the context of DC pensions?	The risk that a retiree drawing down from a DC pot (rather than buying an annuity) will <b>outlive their savings</b>. Converting the pot into a regular <b>annuity</b> removes this risk but is irreversible and depends on annuity rates at the time of purchase.	Topic16 16.1.7 Pensions
How do DB pension liabilities affect <b>investment analysis</b> of the sponsoring company?	DB liabilities appear on the company's balance sheet. Their valuation is <b>inversely related to interest rates</b> (assets used to buy income streams). In a low-rate environment, liabilities rise, potentially making the scheme unviable. Investment analysts factor this into their assessment of the company's prospects.	Topic16 16.1.7 Pensions
Describe <b>execution-only</b> investment platforms.	Trading services limited to executing buy/sell orders with <b>no advice</b>. Available across stocks, bonds, mutual funds, ETFs, FX. Mostly internet/telephone-based. Use <b>nominee accounts</b> (investments registered in brokerage name; client remains beneficial owner). Low-cost — suited to experienced traders and day traders. Risk: potential over-trading.	Topic16 16.1.8 ExecutionOnly
What is the <b>ongoing charges figure (OCF)</b> and what does it exclude?	The OCF (formerly TER) is the industry standard measure of running costs. It includes the <b>AMC</b> and administrative charges (register, unit pricing, safekeeping). It <b>excludes</b>: performance fees, and <b>trading costs</b> (broker commissions, stamp duty, bid–offer spreads). Thus a low-OCF fund with high turnover may actually be expensive. Under <b>MiFID II</b>, total costs must be disclosed in <b>pounds and pence</b> (not just percentages) at least once per year.	Topic16 16.1.5 FundCharges
What are the three categories of charge under <b>MiFID II's total cost of investing (TCI)</b>?	(1) <b>Ongoing charges</b> — all recurring charges (management, custody, service costs);<br>(2) <b>Transaction costs</b> — costs of buying/selling investments (broker commissions, stamp duty); includes 'implicit' costs like market impact, execution delay, and opportunity cost;<br>(3) <b>One-off charges</b> — initial/exit fees plus incidental costs (e.g. performance fees).	Topic16 16.1.5 FundCharges
What are <b>implicit transaction costs</b> and why can they be negative?	Costs arising from the market's response to a trade: <b>market impact</b> (large trade moves the price), <b>delay</b> (price changes between order and execution), and <b>opportunity cost</b> (partial fill misses favourable moves). A 'negative' transaction cost occurs when slippage works in the investor's favour (e.g. price improves between decision and execution, e.g. sell decision at 195p, executed at 199p = −4p cost).	Topic16 16.1.5 FundCharges
Define <b>portfolio liquidity</b> and explain why it matters.	Liquidity = the ability to <b>access an investment's value without negatively affecting its price</b>. It matters for: (1) meeting client redemption requests; (2) meeting collateral/margin calls; (3) having 'dry powder' to buy cheap assets in market stress. There is a trade-off: holding liquid buffers vs maximising risk-adjusted returns. Illiquid assets may offer a <b>liquidity premium</b> to compensate.	Topic16 16.1.9 Liquidity
What is a <b>gate</b> in the context of fund liquidity?	A contractual provision allowing a fund to <b>slow the pace of redemptions</b> to prevent forced selling of illiquid assets. Commonly used by hedge funds and property funds. A well-designed gate should not encourage early redemption or provide additional benefits to those who redeem. Badly designed gates can accelerate fund dissolution.	Topic16 16.1.9 Liquidity
How can <b>derivatives</b> help manage portfolio liquidity?	Derivatives can provide the <b>same exposure as a physical security</b> (e.g. Treasury bond future instead of physical bond) and may offer <b>better liquidity</b> than the underlying, especially in corporate credit (a CDS index vs individual bonds). This frees up cash for liquidity buffers while maintaining market exposure. In stressed markets, derivative liquidity may actually improve due to increased hedging demand.	Topic16 16.1.9 Liquidity
How should investors <b>minimise liquidity risk</b> in portfolio construction?	Two key aspects: (1) Maintain sufficient <b>liquid assets</b> (cash, bills, investment-grade government bonds); (2) Ensure investment strategies, fund constraints, and asset class characteristics are <b>aligned with portfolio objectives</b>, using safeguards such as restricting liquidity, investment gating, period lockups, and constraining investor access to capital.	Topic16 16.1.9 Liquidity
