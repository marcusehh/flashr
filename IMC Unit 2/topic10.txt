What are the fundamental precepts (purposes) of accounting?	Record an organisation's transactions so its dealings can be planned/reported. Accounts are prepared to: (1) summarise transactions for management to run the business; (2) report to owners/investors/trade payables; (3) enable other parties to make analysis/assessments.	IMC::Topic10::10.1.1
Name the 4 bodies/sources governing UK accounting practice and disclosure.	(1) Companies Acts (legal requirements for all cos); (2) FRC (issues/reviews UK standards = FRS); (3) LSE listing rules (extra disclosures for quoted cos); (4) IASB (issues IFRS — mandatory for all UK-listed consolidated accounts).	IMC::Topic10::10.1.1
What records must companies keep, and what does 'true and fair view' mean legally?	Adequate accounting records so financial statements (income statement + balance sheet) can be produced, enabling a 'true and fair view'. Note: 'true and fair view' is NOT precisely defined in any Companies Act.	IMC::Topic10::10.1.1
What documents must large companies provide shareholders annually?	Comprehensive income statement; balance sheet; director's report; auditor's report; cash flow statement; statement of changes in equity. Filed with the Registrar of Companies (Companies House). Listed cos add interim/half-yearly reports + disclosures of 20%+ holdings.	IMC::Topic10::10.1.1
State the Companies Act 2006 thresholds for a SMALL company (2 or more of).	Turnover < £15m; balance sheet total < £7.5m; average employees < 50.	IMC::Topic10::10.1.2
State the thresholds for a MEDIUM-SIZED company (2 or more of).	Turnover < £54m; balance sheet total < £27m; average employees < 250.	IMC::Topic10::10.1.2
State the thresholds for a MICRO-entity (FRS 105).	Turnover ≤ £1m; balance sheet total ≤ £500,000; average employees ≤ 10. (Reporting thresholds, not audit thresholds.)	IMC::Topic10::10.1.2
Why does the small/medium definition matter for reporting?	Small/medium companies may be exempt from delivering their full annual statements to the Registrar, and smaller entities can use reduced-disclosure standards (FRS 102 small entities, FRS 105).	IMC::Topic10::10.1.2
What are the two legal categories of organisation?	(1) Bodies sole — sole traders/partnerships, no legal duty to produce accounts. (2) Bodies corporate — all other forms, recognised by law as a legal entity separate from owners, so must prepare accounts.	IMC::Topic10::10.1.3
What does it mean that a company is a 'separate legal entity'?	A company (a body corporate) is a legal entity formed by registration under the Companies Acts, recognised by law as separate and distinct from its owners — which is why accounts must be prepared.	IMC::Topic10::10.1.3
Distinguish a plc from a private company.	plc: minimum issued share capital £50,000 (25%+ paid up), registered as public, may offer shares to the general public, name ends 'plc'. Private company: any other company, cannot offer shares publicly, name ends 'limited'.	IMC::Topic10::10.1.3
What is a close company?	Broadly, a company under the control of ≤5 persons (with associates), or under the control of its directors (defined in the Income & Corporation Taxes Act 1988).	IMC::Topic10::10.1.3
Who sets UK accounting standards, and what is the IASB?	FRC sets UK policy and issues Financial Reporting Standards (FRS). The IASB is an independent, privately funded standard-setter (took over from the IASC in 2001) that issues IFRS.	IMC::Topic10::10.1.4
What is the narrow vs broad meaning of IFRS?	Narrow: the new numbered series of IASB pronouncements (distinct from older IAS). Broad: the entire body of IASB pronouncements, including the IAS approved by the previous committee.	IMC::Topic10::10.1.4
When must IFRS be used rather than UK GAAP?	From 1 Jan 2021, UK-listed (and AIM) companies must use UK-adopted IFRS for consolidated/group accounts; listed cos may use IFRS for individual accounts. Other UK cos and LLPs are permitted (not required) to use IFRS.	IMC::Topic10::10.1.4
What is UK GAAP and its main standard? How does FRS 102 differ in terminology?	UK GAAP = standards for companies not using IFRS; main standard = FRS 102 (effective periods on/after 1 Jan 2015). Supported by FRS 105 (micro-entities) and FRS 101 (reduced disclosure for IFRS-group members). FRS 102 uses IFRS language: receivables/payables, property/plant/equipment.	IMC::Topic10::10.1.4
What is the auditor's role, and when must a report be qualified?	Report to shareholders whether accounts are properly prepared (Companies Acts/standards) and give a true and fair view. Must QUALIFY if these conditions are not met — e.g. scope limitation, disagreement, or fundamental uncertainty. Only small companies are exempt from audit.	IMC::Topic10::10.1.5
What does IAS 700 (extended audit report) require auditors to do?	Provide an overview of audit scope (addressing risk/materiality); describe the risks with greatest effect on audit strategy and resource allocation; explain how materiality was applied in planning/performing the audit.	IMC::Topic10::10.1.5
Under the UK Audit Regulations, state the audit firm rotation rule for public interest entities.	Audit firms must rotate after 10 years — extendable by +10 years where public tenders are carried out, or +14 years where more than one audit firm is appointed.	IMC::Topic10::10.1.5
Under the UK Audit Regulations, state the non-audit services restrictions.	Certain non-audit services prohibited (tax, valuation, internal audit, share underwriting). Where permitted non-audit services run 3+ years, total non-audit fees capped at 70% of the average audit fees over the previous 3 years.	IMC::Topic10::10.1.5
What is the purpose of the balance sheet?	A statement of financial position at a single point in time (so quickly out of date), showing the value/categories of assets controlled and the claims against them — i.e. resources and how they were financed.	IMC::Topic10::10.2.1
State the accounting identity underlying the balance sheet.	Shareholders' funds + non-current liabilities + current liabilities = non-current assets + current assets.	IMC::Topic10::10.2.1
Define an asset (IASB) and distinguish non-current from current assets.	Asset = a resource controlled as a result of past events from which future economic benefits are expected. Non-current: held >1 period (tangible: PP&E, vehicles; intangible: patents, trademarks). Current: short-term, converted to cash/sold (inventories, receivables, cash).	IMC::Topic10::10.2.2
How are liabilities classified, and what are shareholders' funds?	Current = due within one year (trade payables, overdrafts). Non-current = loan stock, debentures, long-term loans unless due within a year. Shareholders' funds = subscribed capital + retained earnings — residual claims, paid only when the company ceases trading.	IMC::Topic10::10.2.2
Distinguish capital (capitalised) expenditure from revenue expenditure.	Capital expenditure creates future benefits (buying/improving a non-current asset, life >1yr) → capitalised on the balance sheet and depreciated. Revenue expenditure = short-life items/maintenance/repairs not extending life → expensed now. E.g. install aircon = capitalise; repair it = expense.	IMC::Topic10::10.2.3
What two models value non-current assets, and how is net book value found?	Cost model (cost − accumulated depreciation − impairment) or revaluation model (fair value − subsequent depreciation/impairment); must apply to a whole asset class. NBV = original cost − accumulated depreciation (≠ market value).	IMC::Topic10::10.2.4
When is impairment testing required?	Only where there is an indication of impairment — EXCEPT goodwill and indefinite-life intangibles, which must be tested at least annually regardless. Impairment loss = recoverable amount (fair value less selling costs, or value in use) below carrying amount.	IMC::Topic10::10.2.4
State the straight-line depreciation formula.	Annual depreciation = (original cost − expected residual value) ÷ expected useful life. Spreads the charge evenly; most common UK method.	IMC::Topic10::10.2.5
State the reducing balance depreciation formula.	Depreciation rate = 1 − ⁿ√(expected residual value ÷ original cost), where n = economic life in years. Applies a constant % to the prior period's book value (higher charges in early years).	IMC::Topic10::10.2.5
Worked: £20,000 machine, £2,000 residual, 4-year life — straight-line annual charge and reducing-balance rate?	Straight-line = (20,000 − 2,000) ÷ 4 = £4,500/yr. Reducing balance rate = 1 − ⁴√(2,000 ÷ 20,000) = 1 − 0.5623 = 43.77%.	IMC::Topic10::10.2.5
How is profit/loss on disposal of a non-current asset calculated?	Profit/loss = sale proceeds − net book value at disposal. Reflected in the year's results; disclosed separately if material.	IMC::Topic10::10.2.6
On what principle are inventories valued, and what are the 3 categories of inventory?	At the LOWER of cost and net realisable value (NRV = sale proceeds − costs still to incur: finishing, marketing, selling). Categories: raw materials, work in progress, finished goods. As sold, cost transfers to income statement as COGS.	IMC::Topic10::10.2.7
What are the 3 inventory valuation methods, and which is banned?	FIFO (oldest sold first — common UK); LIFO (newest sold first — NOT permitted under FRS 102/IFRS); weighted average (sold drawn proportionately). Valuation is a problem of ALLOCATING cost between income statement (COGS) and balance sheet.	IMC::Topic10::10.2.7
In RISING prices, how do FIFO vs LIFO affect COGS and profit?	LIFO charges latest (highest) costs → higher COGS, LOWER profit, lower closing inventory value. FIFO charges older (lower) costs → lower COGS, HIGHER profit, higher closing inventory value.	IMC::Topic10::10.2.8
What are the two risks and the tax advantage of debt finance?	Risks: (1) interest must be paid when due (dividends can be deferred); (2) most borrowing must be repaid by a set date (share capital usually isn't). Advantage: interest is allowable for corporation tax, unlike dividends (paid from taxed income).	IMC::Topic10::10.2.9
Distinguish a fixed from a floating charge; how do unsecured loan stockholders rank?	Fixed charge: on specific identifiable assets. Floating charge: general charge over the company's assets. Unsecured loan stockholders rank alongside unsecured trade payables, BELOW debenture holders and preferential payables (e.g. tax).	IMC::Topic10::10.2.9
What is convertible loan capital, and why is its interest rate lower?	Loan capital (usually unsecured) giving the owner the option to convert to shares. The market treats it as deferred equity, so its rate is lower than non-convertible loan capital. Ranks above ordinary shares but below unsecured non-convertible loan stock in liquidation.	IMC::Topic10::10.2.9
List the main current liabilities; distinguish accruals from deferred income.	Loan capital/bank loans <1yr, payments on account, trade payables, bills of exchange + commercial paper, accruals & deferred income, tax/social security. Accruals = owed for services already received but unpaid. Deferred income = received/due but not yet earned (e.g. a deposit).	IMC::Topic10::10.2.9
What is a provision, and what are its three types?	An amount charged against profit (an expense) to record a reduction in an asset's value even if the exact fall is uncertain. Types: (1) doubtful debts; (2) depreciation; (3) unrealised profit on stock. E.g. doubtful-debt provision reduces receivables on the balance sheet and is an expense.	IMC::Topic10::10.2.10
What is a contingent liability and how is it treated (FRS 102 / IAS 37)?	A potential liability not in existence at the balance sheet date, not sufficiently predictable to warrant a provision — disclosed by NOTE only. Examples: legal claims, warranties/guarantees, guarantees to banks, discounted bills of exchange.	IMC::Topic10::10.2.11
What is IAS 19 and its basic principle for DB pension costs?	IAS 19 = standard for defined benefit pension costs; brings the scheme's surplus/deficit directly onto the sponsor's balance sheet. Cost recognised in the period the employee earns the benefit (not when paid). Liability if net deficit; asset if net surplus.	IMC::Topic10::10.2.12
Under IAS 19, how are pension assets/liabilities measured, and where do gains/losses go?	Assets at market value; liabilities by projected unit method discounted at an AA corporate bond rate. Actuarial gains/losses → other comprehensive income (OCI). Only service cost + net interest → profit or loss.	IMC::Topic10::10.2.12
What are post-balance sheet events; distinguish adjusting from non-adjusting (IAS 10 / FRS 102)?	Events between balance sheet date and approval by directors. Adjusting = evidence of conditions existing at the balance sheet date (e.g. debtor insolvency) → ADJUST accounts. Non-adjusting = arise after (e.g. major acquisition) → disclose only. Dividends declared after the date = non-adjusting, NOT recognised as a liability.	IMC::Topic10::10.2.13
Distinguish authorised, issued, paid-up and called-up share capital.	Authorised = max legally issuable (set in Memorandum). Issued = sold to investors. Called-up = portion the company has requested payment for. Paid-up = actually paid (par value + share premium).	IMC::Topic10::10.2.14
Rank the four main share types for distribution in liquidation.	(1) Preference shares; (2) ordinary shares; (3) deferred shares; (4) warrants to subscribe for shares.	IMC::Topic10::10.2.14
Explain a rights issue and its balance sheet effect.	New shares offered to existing holders in proportion to holdings, at a discount (lowest price = nominal value). RAISES new capital; issued share capital rises by nominal value, excess goes to share premium account. Price normally falls, but no dilution for those taking up rights.	IMC::Topic10::10.2.15
Explain a scrip (bonus/capitalisation) issue and its balance sheet effect.	A free issue of new shares by capitalising reserves — a book-keeping transaction with NO new capital raised. Share price falls proportionally. Ordinary share capital rises and reserves fall by the same amount.	IMC::Topic10::10.2.15
Explain a stock split.	Increases the number of shares; price is adjusted so before-and-after market capitalisation is unchanged, no dilution (e.g. 1,000 @ £5 → 2,000 @ £2.50). Common reason: lower a high share price to make it more affordable.	IMC::Topic10::10.2.15
Explain a share repurchase (buyback) and its signalling role.	Company buys its own shares (held 'in treasury' since 2003 — resold, used for employee schemes, or cancelled). Value effect neutral: net assets fall but fewer shares counterbalance. Can signal management believes shares undervalued; markets respond positively.	IMC::Topic10::10.2.15
How can reserves ARISE and be REDUCED?	Arise: retained profits, premium on share issue, repaying loan capital below nominal, acquiring assets below book value, upward revaluations. Reduce: writing off goodwill, downward revaluations, losses, issue/redemption expenses.	IMC::Topic10::10.2.16
Into what three parts are reserves subdivided, and what are the first two?	Three parts: share premium account, revaluation reserve, other reserves. Share premium = excess over nominal value on issue; non-distributable except on liquidation, but can fund a scrip issue/issue costs. Revaluation reserve = surplus/loss on asset revaluation; transferred to retained earnings on disposal.	IMC::Topic10::10.2.16
Define a financial instrument, financial asset and financial liability; what standards apply?	Instrument = a contract giving a financial asset of one entity and a financial liability/equity of another. Financial asset: cash, another's equity, or contractual right to receive cash/favourable exchange. Financial liability: contractual obligation to deliver cash/unfavourable exchange. Standards: IFRS 9, IAS 32 (presentation), IFRS 7 (disclosure); FRS 102 ss.11–12.	IMC::Topic10::10.3.1
Under IAS 32, on what basis is an instrument classified as liability or equity? Apply to redeemable preference shares.	By the SUBSTANCE of the contract, not legal form, decided at initial recognition. Equity only if no obligation to deliver cash. Mandatorily redeemable preference shares = LIABILITY (obligation to pay cash) → dividends treated as interest expense. Normal preference shares = equity.	IMC::Topic10::10.3.1
Under FRS 102, how are financial instruments classified and initially measured? (listed vs unlisted example)	Classified basic (s.11: cash, commercial paper, bonds) or other (s.12: ABS, options, warrants, hedges). Initially at transaction price incl. costs — EXCEPT if at fair value through P&L (e.g. listed equity), costs excluded. Listed: exclude transaction costs, then carry at FVPL. Unlisted: include transaction costs (no reliable fair value later).	IMC::Topic10::10.3.1
When are financial assets/liabilities recognised and derecognised?	Recognised when the entity becomes a party to the contractual provisions (not when settled) — so derivatives are recognised even if nothing was paid. Derecognised when the rights to cash flows expire, OR the asset is transferred and the risks/rewards of ownership pass.	IMC::Topic10::10.3.1
Under IFRS 9, what are the 3 measurement classifications, the default, and the volatility implication?	(1) Fair value through P&L (FVPL — the default); (2) amortised cost; (3) fair value through OCI (FVOCI). Amortised cost/FVOCI only if restrictive criteria met. Vs IAS 39, IFRS 9 is simpler but gives MORE P&L volatility (non-trading default shifted from FVOCI to FVPL).	IMC::Topic10::10.3.1
Under IFRS 9, how are loans/receivables measured by business model, and what is the equity FVOCI election?	Held to collect cash flows = amortised cost; held to collect AND sell = FVOCI; otherwise FVPL. Equity election: irrevocable choice at recognition to take only dividends to P&L with fair value changes in OCI (no recycling) = FVOCI.	IMC::Topic10::10.3.1
Under IFRS 7, what must be disclosed, and distinguish fair value vs cash flow hedges.	Describe financial risk management objectives/policies incl. hedging. For each hedge disclose: description, hedging instruments + fair values, nature of risks hedged, (cash flow) expected timing. Fair value hedge = hedges changes in fair value of a recognised asset/liability. Cash flow hedge = hedges variability of cash flows.	IMC::Topic10::10.3.1
What does the income statement show, and how can expenses be classified?	Performance over a period (usually 1 year; interims ~6 months). Expenses by NATURE (raw materials, depreciation, salaries) or by FUNCTION (COGS, selling, admin).	IMC::Topic10::10.4.1
What is retained income and its formula?	Retained income R = s − c − t − d, where s = sales revenue recognised, c = matched costs, t = corporation tax on surplus of s over c, d = dividends paid/proposed.	IMC::Topic10::10.4.1
State the IFRS 15 five-step revenue recognition model.	IFRS 15 (replaced IAS 18): (1) identify the contract; (2) identify performance obligations; (3) determine transaction price; (4) allocate price to obligations; (5) recognise revenue when/as each obligation is satisfied.	IMC::Topic10::10.4.2
What is the key revenue timing principle under IFRS 15, and the telecoms effect?	Revenue recognised on delivery/satisfaction of the obligation, NOT on order or payment (e.g. sold Jan, paid Feb, delivered Mar → recognise in March). Telecoms recognise revenue EARLIER than IAS 18 because the price is allocated to performance obligations (handset gets allocated revenue).	IMC::Topic10::10.4.2
Name the two income statement formats and how operating profit is determined.	Format 1 = by function (cost of sales, distribution, admin — most retailers). Format 2 = by nature (more detailed operating costs). Operating profit = revenue − COGS − operating expenses − depreciation − amortisation (≈ EBIT; equals EBIT if no non-operating revenue).	IMC::Topic10::10.4.3
Define net income, and give the two EBITDA formulas.	Net income (bottom line) = operating profit − interest − tax ± one-offs/secondary income. EBITDA = net income + interest + taxes + depreciation + amortisation; OR = operating profit + depreciation + amortisation.	IMC::Topic10::10.4.3
What are APMs / non-statutory profit measures, and why are they scrutinised?	Non-IFRS/non-GAAP measures (EBITDA, EBIT, adjusted earnings, free cash flow, debt ratio, ROCE). FRC/regulators scrutinise them because they can mislead — concerns: absent/unclear definitions or reconciliations, unclear reasons for exclusions, items repeatedly called 'non-recurring'.	IMC::Topic10::10.4.3
What are the statement of comprehensive income and the statement of changes in equity?	Comprehensive income (IAS 1) = net income + other changes in net assets not in the income statement (asset revaluations, gains/losses on sales, FX translation); single or two-statement format. Statement of changes in equity summarises movements in share capital, share premium, retained earnings, revaluation surplus and unrealised gains.	IMC::Topic10::10.4.4
What is the purpose of the cash flow statement, and who is exempt?	A summary of cash movements during the year (cash, not accruals — like a summarised bank statement). Exempt: small companies, and subsidiaries where 90%+ of voting rights are held within the group.	IMC::Topic10::10.5.1
What are the three cash flow statement headings, with examples?	(1) Operating activities (net cash from operations; reconciliation to operating profit required). (2) Investing: buy/sell PP&E, securities, acquisitions. (3) Financing: issue/repay debt & equity, pay dividends.	IMC::Topic10::10.5.2
State the adjustments to reconcile operating profit to net cash flow from operations.	Inventories ↑ deduct / ↓ add. Trade receivables ↑ deduct / ↓ add. Trade payables ↑ add / ↓ deduct. Depreciation: add back. Increase in provisions: add. (Depreciation & provisions are non-cash, so added back.)	IMC::Topic10::10.5.3
Worked: operating profit £100k; inventories +£40k; payables +£20k; receivables +£10k; depreciation £10k — net cash flow from operations?	£100k − 40k (inventory rise) + 20k (payables rise) − 10k (receivables rise) + 10k (depreciation) = £80,000.	IMC::Topic10::10.5.3
Distinguish subsidiary, associate and investment.	Subsidiary: >50% voting rights (control) — consolidated. Associate: 20–50%, significant influence — equity method. Investment: <20%, no significant influence — marked to market, dividends recognised when received. Control can exist below 50% (IAS 27).	IMC::Topic10::10.6.1
When is significant influence presumed, and how is it evidenced?	Presumed at 20%+ of voting power (unless clearly shown otherwise); below 20%, presumed none. Evidence: board representation, participation in policy/dividend decisions, material transactions, interchange of managerial personnel, provision of essential technical information.	IMC::Topic10::10.6.2
What is the purpose of group accounts?	When a company is a parent at year end, consolidated accounts (balance sheet, income statement, cash flow) are prepared in addition to the parent's, reporting a group under single direction. Small companies are exempt (Companies Act 2006).	IMC::Topic10::10.6.2
What is a minority (non-controlling) interest and how is it represented?	The proportion of a partially owned subsidiary owned outside the group. Shown in the consolidated balance sheet WITHIN equity but separate from the parent's equity; minority interests in group profit/loss also shown separately.	IMC::Topic10::10.6.3
What is goodwill, how is it calculated, and what is negative goodwill?	Goodwill = an intangible arising on acquisition = cost of combination − acquirer's share of net fair value of identifiable assets/liabilities (represents brands, loyalty, patents). Negative goodwill (net assets > price paid) is recognised immediately as a profit.	IMC::Topic10::10.6.4
How is goodwill treated after acquisition under IAS (IFRS 3) vs FRS 102?	IAS/IFRS 3: NOT amortised; impairment-tested at least annually; written down if recoverable < carrying value. FRS 102: finite life, AMORTISED systematically; if life not reliably estimable, must not exceed 10 years.	IMC::Topic10::10.6.5
How are other intangibles treated, and what conditions allow recognising internally generated intangibles?	Acquired intangibles (trademarks, patents) recognised separately from goodwill if identifiable and reliably measurable; carried at cost − amortisation − impairment. Internally generated (from development): recognise only if identifiable, future benefits probable, and the company has the intent + ability to complete.	IMC::Topic10::10.6.5
What are the uses of accounting ratios, and the three categories?	Enable comparison across companies regardless of size, between companies (same sector), and across periods (trends). Categories: profitability (ROCE, ROE), liquidity (current, quick), gearing (extent debt finances the business / solvency).	IMC::Topic10::10.7.1
Define ROCE (with formula) and its drawbacks.	ROCE = operating profit ÷ capital employed (= share capital + reserves + long-term debt = total assets − current liabilities). Drawbacks: 'capital' has many interpretations; operating profit is subject to accounting distortions.	IMC::Topic10::10.7.2
Define ROE (with formula), and why can it be artificially boosted?	ROE = net income ÷ shareholders' equity (profit per unit of reinvested equity; also a measure of efficiency/management). Boosted artificially by write-downs and share buybacks (lower equity), and by high debt (lower equity as % of total assets).	IMC::Topic10::10.7.2
Break ROCE into its two strands.	ROCE = (operating profit ÷ sales) × (sales ÷ capital employed) = profit margin × capital turnover. Profit margin ~8–10% for manufacturing, lower for high-volume retail. Sales ÷ capital employed = sales volume per unit of capital (rise may signal improvement OR overtrading/under-reinvestment).	IMC::Topic10::10.7.3
Define asset turnover.	Asset turnover = sales ÷ total assets. Measures efficiency of asset use; generally higher is better; differs by industry, so compare only within the same industry.	IMC::Topic10::10.7.3
Define operational gearing (with formula) and its effect on profit.	Sensitivity of operating profit to a 1% change in sales. = (sales − variable costs) ÷ operating profit, OR (operating profit + fixed costs) ÷ operating profit. Higher operational gearing = larger % profit gain when sales rise, but larger % fall when sales drop (high fixed-cost base).	IMC::Topic10::10.7.4
Give the gearing ratio formulas, and define solvency.	Gearing = debt ÷ capital employed (capital employed = debt + equity), or debt ÷ equity (analysts'); both as %. Solvent = assets exceed liabilities (positive shareholders' funds); low gearing = predominantly equity-financed.	IMC::Topic10::10.7.4
Define the current and quick ratios, with prudent levels.	Current ratio = current assets ÷ current liabilities (prudent 1.5–2.0; very high not necessarily good). Quick ratio (acid test) = (current assets − inventory) ÷ current liabilities; <1.0 indicates inability to settle short-term liabilities at short notice.	IMC::Topic10::10.7.4
State the effect of a rights issue and a scrip issue on ROE, ROCE and gearing.	Rights issue: shareholders' equity rises → LOWER ROE & ROCE, and LOWER gearing. Scrip/bonus issue: no change to total equity → ROE, ROCE and gearing all UNCHANGED.	IMC::Topic10::10.7.5
State the effect of a stock split and a share repurchase on ROE, ROCE and gearing.	Stock split: market cap unchanged → NO effect on ROE, ROCE, gearing. Share repurchase: shareholders' equity falls → HIGHER ROE & ROCE, and INCREASED gearing.	IMC::Topic10::10.7.5
What is accounting manipulation, and give common methods.	Manipulating accounting info (usually legal but misleading) flatters ratios — always consider it when interpreting ratios. Methods: recording sales early (curbed by IFRS 15); delaying/capitalising expenses; under-costing inventory; over/understating asset values; acquisition accounting creating provisions to flatter future profits.	IMC::Topic10::10.7.5
