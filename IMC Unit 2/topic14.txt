What are <b>alternative investments</b> and why are they held?	Assets other than cash, equities, bonds and money markets — including commodities, property, hedge funds, private equity, private credit and crypto. Held for <b>diversification</b> (low/negative correlation with traditional assets) and as a potential <b>inflation hedge</b>.	Topic14 14.1.1 AltInvestments
What are the main <b>risks/costs</b> of alternative asset classes?	<b>Reduced liquidity</b> (lock-in periods up to 5+ years), substantial transaction costs, infrequent valuations, and reliance on appraisal-based pricing rather than market prices.	Topic14 14.1.1 AltInvestments
Distinguish <b>alternative investments</b> from <b>alternative investment products</b>.	<b>Alternative investments</b> = the asset classes themselves (commodities, property, private equity etc.).<br><b>Alternative investment products</b> = the vehicles/strategies used to access them (e.g. hedge funds running absolute return strategies, derivative contracts).	Topic14 14.1.1 AltInvestments
What are the four main categories of <b>commodity markets</b>?	(1) <b>Energy</b> (oil, gas, derivatives); (2) <b>precious metals</b> (gold, silver, platinum); (3) <b>base/industrial metals</b> (iron, copper, aluminium, zinc, lead, nickel, tin); (4) <b>agricultural/soft commodities</b> (grain, sugar, cocoa, coffee, orange juice, pork bellies).	Topic14 14.2.1 Commodities
What distinguishes <b>hard</b> from <b>soft</b> commodities?	<b>Hard</b> = mined or extracted (metals, energy).<br><b>Soft</b> = agricultural or livestock products.	Topic14 14.2.1 Commodities
List the main <b>ways to invest</b> in commodities.	(1) <b>Physically</b> (direct purchase, adds storage costs); (2) <b>futures contracts</b> (leveraged, margin-based); (3) <b>shares</b> in commodity companies (less volatile but impure exposure); (4) <b>ETFs/ETCs</b> (track a commodity or index via futures or physical holding); (5) <b>CTAs</b> (managed futures via a hedge fund vehicle).	Topic14 14.2.2 Commodities
What is a <b>commodity trading adviser (CTA)</b>?	A collective investment vehicle (often a hedge fund) that invests in futures — commodity futures plus often other assets like interest rate futures or stock indices. Uses algorithms or discretionary management. A key feature is widespread use of <b>derivatives</b>, especially futures.	Topic14 14.2.2 Commodities
Why might an ETF or ETC <b>not</b> replicate spot commodity returns?	Because most commodity ETFs hold <b>futures</b>, not physical assets. Rolling expiring contracts into new ones creates a <b>roll yield</b> that can be positive or negative depending on the futures curve shape, causing returns to diverge from spot prices.	Topic14 14.2.2 Commodities
What is <b>roll yield</b>, and how do <b>contango</b> and <b>backwardation</b> affect it?	<b>Roll yield</b> = the gain/loss from rolling expiring futures into new contracts.<br><b>Contango</b> (upward-sloping futures curve): sell low, buy high → <b>negative</b> roll yield.<br><b>Backwardation</b> (downward-sloping): sell high, buy low → <b>positive</b> roll yield.	Topic14 14.2.3 Commodities
Name the major <b>exchanges</b> for energy derivatives and state where oil reference prices are published.	<b>NYMEX</b> (part of CME Group) — Light Sweet Crude Oil.<br><b>ICE Futures Europe</b> (London) — Brent Crude Oil.<br>Reference rates for oil products published by <b>Platts</b>.	Topic14 14.2.3 Commodities
What is <b>basis risk</b> in commodity futures?	The differential between the futures price and the equivalent cash/spot market price. It changes continually, reflecting storage costs, transport, interest rates, market sentiment and supply/demand, making hedging imperfect in practice.	Topic14 14.2.3 Commodities
Describe the main features of <b>energy options</b> and <b>OTC energy derivatives</b>.	Energy options are on the underlying futures and are <b>cash-settled</b>; most are closed out before expiry (only ~2% physically delivered).<br><b>OTC</b> derivatives offer bespoke terms (different periods/strikes); counterparties are typically large oil companies and banks; each bears the other's <b>credit risk</b>. Caps and collars are popular OTC products.	Topic14 14.2.3 Commodities
What are <b>carbon emissions</b> derivatives?	Allowances to emit CO₂ can be traded on climate exchanges as futures and options. Prices quoted in <b>euros per tonne of CO₂</b>. Other greenhouse gases are quoted as CO₂-equivalent multiples. Driven by regulatory frameworks, so somewhat different from standard commodity trading.	Topic14 14.2.3 Commodities
What three features must be considered when investing in <b>physical precious metals</b>?	(i) Cost of <b>storage and insurance</b>; (ii) <b>absence of a yield</b>; (iii) potential for <b>quality issues</b>.	Topic14 14.2.4 PreciousMetals
Compare the investment characteristics of <b>gold, silver and platinum</b>.	<b>Gold</b>: limited industrial use; price driven by sentiment, financial worries, inflation fears and geopolitical stress; new supply small vs existing stocks.<br><b>Silver</b>: dual role (store of value + industrial metal) → more volatile than gold; price affected by industrial innovations.<br><b>Platinum</b>: rarer, often pricier; mainly industrial (automotive catalysts, jewellery); supply concentrated in South Africa and Russia → supply uncertainty.	Topic14 14.2.4 PreciousMetals
Name the four main <b>commodity derivative indices</b> and one key difference of each.	(1) <b>Refinitiv/CoreCommodity CRB</b> (now FTSE/CoreCommodity CRB): 19 categories, weighted arithmetic average, energy 33%, trades on ICE.<br>(2) <b>S&P DJCI</b>: weights based on 5-year moving average of world production values → overweights commodities that have risen; rebalanced annually. 2025 weights: energy ~40%.<br>(3) <b>Bloomberg BCOM</b>: uses 2:1 ratio of liquidity to production data; no single commodity &gt;15%, no sector &gt;33%; rebalanced annually. 2025: energy 30%.<br>(4) <b>Rogers International (RICI)</b>: 38 commodities on 9 exchanges in 4 currencies; aims to reflect global consumption patterns; reviewed by expert committee.	Topic14 14.2.5 CommodityIndices
How can commodity exposure be viewed as a <b>hedge against inflation</b> and event risk?	Commodity futures are <b>positively correlated with inflation</b> at all horizons (futures prices embed expected commodity price trends). They are <b>negatively correlated with equities and bonds</b> over long horizons. They tend to do well when stocks/bonds do poorly, e.g. during wars, floods and macro-shocks. However, correlations may change over time.	Topic14 14.2.6 InflationHedge
What is a <b>cryptocurrency</b> and how does it differ from fiat currency? (list key differences)	A digitally encrypted, decentralised currency built on a <b>blockchain</b>, not linked to any government or central authority.<br>Key differences from fiat: (1) no government backing; (2) no financial intermediaries needed for transfers; (3) supply may be capped (e.g. Bitcoin 21m); (4) only digital transfers (no physical form); (5) stored in digital wallets not bank accounts; (6) transactions can be anonymous; (7) lower transfer costs; (8) subdivided into tiny units (1 Satoshi = 0.00000001 BTC); (9) cross-border with no restrictions.	Topic14 14.2.7 Crypto
What is <b>blockchain</b> (DLT) and why is it important for crypto?	<b>Distributed ledger technology</b>: an immutable digital ledger duplicated across a network. New blocks added via a <b>consensus mechanism</b>. Fully peer-to-peer — no central intermediary. Enables near real-time settlement, fraud resistance, and transparent records. Uses beyond crypto include medical records, anti-money laundering, supply chain, and real estate.	Topic14 14.2.7 Crypto
Briefly describe <b>Bitcoin, Ethereum and Ripple</b>.	<b>Bitcoin</b>: first (2009), largest; created by Satoshi Nakamoto; capped at 21m coins; mined via proof-of-work; public/decentralised.<br><b>Ethereum (Ether)</b>: ~20% of Bitcoin's size; platform for smart contracts and decentralised applications; important for DeFi.<br><b>Ripple (XRP)</b>: global payments network used by major banks; owned by a private company; faster and more energy-efficient than Bitcoin.	Topic14 14.2.7 Crypto
What are <b>stablecoins</b> and <b>central bank digital currencies (CBDCs)</b>?	<b>Stablecoins</b>: pegged to fiat currencies or other assets to reduce volatility; useful for converting between fiat and crypto.<br><b>CBDCs</b>: official digital currencies created by central banks using DLT, aiming to maintain monetary control in a world of private crypto. Being explored by the Fed, BoE, ECB, PBoC and others.	Topic14 14.2.7 Crypto
What drives <b>cryptocurrency returns</b> and what is their portfolio role?	Returns are mainly driven by <b>crypto-specific factors</b> — momentum and investor attention — not by stocks, currencies or macro factors. This suggests crypto behaves differently from conventional assets, so adding it to a portfolio may offer <b>diversification benefits</b>. However, extreme volatility, lack of established legal frameworks, and regulatory uncertainty are key risks.	Topic14 14.2.7 Crypto
Distinguish <b>freehold</b> from <b>leasehold</b> property ownership.	<b>Freehold</b>: owning the property and land in perpetuity.<br><b>Leasehold</b>: owning the property (not the land) for a contractually limited period (e.g. 999 or 130 years) before it reverts to the freeholder. Break clauses may be present.	Topic14 14.3.1 Property
Why is property considered an <b>attractive asset class</b>? (list reasons)	(1) <b>Lower volatility</b> than equities/bonds; (2) <b>low long-run correlation</b> with equities/bonds → risk diversifier; (3) <b>high income return</b> (e.g. 5.6% p.a. UK property to Dec 2015); (4) combines features of both equities (capital growth) and bonds (income); (5) <b>partial inflation hedge</b> (UK commercial leases track inflation); (6) residual value; (7) well-established, transparent market.	Topic14 14.3.1 Property
What is <b>autocorrelation</b> ('return smoothing') in property and why does it matter?	Property returns are correlated with their own past values because of <b>infrequent valuations</b> and illiquidity. This <b>understates true volatility</b> — the actual standard deviation of property returns may be 1.3–1.5× (or up to 3×) the reported figure. This can also make measured correlations with other assets misleadingly low, potentially causing <b>over-allocation</b> to property in portfolio construction.	Topic14 14.3.1 Property
State the <b>de-smoothing formula</b> for property returns.	$$\text{True return}_t = \frac{\text{Valuation return}_t - k \times \text{Valuation return}_{t-1}}{1 - k}$$<br>where <i>k</i> (0 to 1) = de-smoothing coefficient representing how much current valuations are influenced by previous valuations.	Topic14 14.3.1 Property
Distinguish <b>commercial</b> from <b>residential</b> property markets.	<b>Commercial</b>: long-term leases (10+ years); tenant responsible for repairs; return dominated by <b>rental income</b>; higher cost; secure/stable cash flow; diversification benefits.<br><b>Residential</b>: short renewable leases; landlord responsible for repairs; return historically dominated by <b>capital value increases</b>.	Topic14 14.3.2 Property
Name the three main <b>commercial property sectors</b> and the main property performance index.	Sectors: <b>retail</b> (shops, shopping centres, warehouses), <b>office</b> (Central London, regional), <b>industrial</b> (standard industrial, distribution warehouses).<br>Index: <b>MSCI UK Monthly Property Index</b> (replaced MSCI IPD) — measures unlevered total returns from one valuation to the next; based on <b>portfolio valuations</b> (not transaction data); value-weighted; reported in nominal terms.	Topic14 14.3.3 PropertyIndices
What data does the MSCI property index use and what does it exclude?	Uses: total return, income return, capital growth, rental value growth — all from <b>portfolio valuations</b> of directly held properties. Excludes: effects of debt, fund management fees, taxation and cash.	Topic14 14.3.3 PropertyIndices
Name the <b>residential</b> property price indices and how property portfolio performance measurement differs from other assets.	<b>Nationwide</b> and <b>Halifax</b> House Price Indices. Portfolio performance must account for <b>transaction fees</b> on buying/selling and possible <b>performance fees</b>.	Topic14 14.3.3 PropertyIndices
Who are the main <b>investors in UK commercial property</b>?	Overseas investors (~30%), UK-domiciled unlisted & collective schemes (~16%), UK REITs & listed property companies (~15%), UK private property companies (~10%), UK insurance funds (~8%), UK pension funds (~8%), UK estates & charities (~4%), UK private investors (~3%), UK other (~7%).	Topic14 14.3.4 PropertyInvestors
How does the <b>commercial property market</b> operate (buying/selling)?	Generally a <b>private market</b> — agents match buyers and sellers for a fee; property auction markets also exist. It is <b>illiquid</b>: transactions take months; costs include agent fees, valuation fees, surveys, environmental tests, legal fees. Valuers use <b>RICS</b> (Royal Institution of Chartered Surveyors) standards (the 'Red Book') combining financial info with market data.	Topic14 14.3.5 PropertyMarket
Name the <b>three main property valuation approaches</b>.	(1) <b>Cost approach</b>: replacement cost of land + building (market value may differ from construction cost).<br>(2) <b>Sales comparison</b>: relative valuation against similar transacted properties (like P/E ratios for equities); <b>hedonic price variation</b> uses regression on property characteristics (location, rooms, transport etc.).<br>(3) <b>Income approach</b>: discounting perpetual NOI at the market capitalisation (cap) rate.	Topic14 14.3.6 PropertyValuation
State the <b>market cap rate</b> formula and explain its use.	$$\text{Market cap rate} = \frac{\text{Benchmark NOI}}{\text{Benchmark transaction price}}$$<br>NOI = gross potential income less expenses (insurance, repairs, vacancy). The cap rate reflects recent transaction yields for comparable properties. If inflation raises NOI and the cap rate adjusts accordingly, the property valuation may remain unchanged. Can be supplemented by a <b>discounted after-tax cash flow (DCF)</b> approach — for an investment to be worthwhile, NPV should be positive or IRR should exceed the investor's required rate of return.	Topic14 14.3.6 PropertyValuation
What are the ESG considerations in <b>property valuation and investment</b>?	<b>Environmental</b>: pollution, resource scarcity, climate change, regulatory compliance.<br><b>Social</b>: health & safety, labour conditions, customer satisfaction.<br><b>Governance</b>: anti-bribery, board quality, transparency.<br>'Green' buildings can: reduce operating expenses, support higher rents, reduce vacancy, improve occupant wellbeing. Non-compliant buildings face <b>'brown discounting'</b>. The Paris Agreement (2015) has driven growing mandatory ESG disclosure and net-zero targets (2050). UNEP FI's <b>Responsible Property Investment (RPI)</b> report (2012) formalises integration of ESG into property investment decisions.	Topic14 14.3.7 ESG
List the main routes to <b>indirect property investment</b>.	(1) <b>UK-authorised property unit trusts</b> (up to 100% in property; can also invest up to 20% in unregulated CIS).<br>(2) <b>Unauthorised property unit trusts</b> (for tax-exempt/offshore investors).<br>(3) <b>Unit-linked life and pension funds</b>.<br>(4) <b>UK life bonds</b> (insurance policies; deferred tax on withdrawals).<br>(5) <b>REITs</b>.<br>(6) <b>Real estate derivatives</b>.<br>(7) <b>Listed property companies</b> (higher volatility, less tax-efficient than REITs).<br>(8) <b>Investment trusts</b> (LSE-listed, exempt from CGT, derived from dwelling houses).<br>(9) <b>UK limited partnerships</b> (high risk, illiquid, offer tax transparency and leverage).	Topic14 14.3.8 IndirectProperty
What are the conditions to qualify as a <b>UK REIT</b>?	(1) UK-based, <b>closed-end</b>, traded on a recognised stock exchange.<br>(2) No single investor owns &gt;10% without losing tax benefits.<br>(3) Property-letting business (tax-exempt) must be ≥<b>75%</b> of assets and total income.<br>(4) Must own at least <b>3 properties</b>, no single property &gt;40% of total assets.<br>(5) Must distribute at least <b>90%</b> of net taxable profits.<br>(6) Must withhold <b>basic rate tax</b> on distributions.<br>(7) Subject to an <b>interest cover test</b> (not highly geared).<br>Advantage: eliminates 'double taxation' at corporate and investor level.	Topic14 14.3.8 REIT
Name three types of <b>real estate derivatives</b>.	(1) <b>Property income certificates</b>: bond with embedded property swap; pays quarterly return based on MSCI all-property total return plus a maturity payment reflecting index movement.<br>(2) <b>MSCI property index swaps</b>: OTC; bank pays SONIA + margin in exchange for property returns (MSCI annual index).<br>(3) <b>MSCI property futures</b>: traded on Eurex; annual contracts based on MSCI UK monthly indices. Stamp duty is <b>not payable</b> on property derivatives (provided no rights/intent in the land).	Topic14 14.3.8 RealEstateDerivatives
What are <b>real assets</b>, and what are the three main components in investment portfolios?	Tangible assets with intrinsic value due to their physical properties: (1) <b>real estate</b>; (2) <b>infrastructure</b>; (3) <b>natural resources</b>.<br>They are typically less liquid than financial assets, have low correlation with equities/bonds (~0.7 with US equities, 0–0.3 with US bonds), offer <b>inflation protection</b> (inelastic demand for their products, e.g. water, energy), and are accessed directly or via listed securities.	Topic14 14.3.9 RealAssets
Explain the features and objectives of <b>hedge funds</b>.	Collective investment vehicles using skill-based strategies including <b>leverage, short selling, and derivatives</b>. Seek <b>positive absolute returns</b> regardless of market direction (formerly 'absolute return funds'). Structured as partnerships: <b>general partner</b> (manager, unlimited liability) and <b>limited partners</b> (investors, liability limited to investment). Key services from <b>prime brokers</b> (execution, clearing, settlement, leverage, custody). Separate fund administrator and custodian bank. FCA regulates managers but not the funds themselves.	Topic14 14.4.1 HedgeFunds
List the key <b>differences between hedge funds and conventional funds</b>.	(1) <b>Management style</b>: more active; use leverage, derivatives, short selling.<br>(2) <b>Investment policy</b>: more flexible.<br>(3) <b>Return targets</b>: absolute return vs benchmark-relative.<br>(4) <b>Fees</b>: management fee (1–2% of AUM) + performance fee (up to 20–25%); conventional funds charge only management fees.<br>(5) <b>Liquidity</b>: hedge funds are less liquid with <b>lock-up periods</b> (months or longer).<br>(6) <b>Investor base</b>: high-net-worth individuals and institutions vs generally open.<br>(7) <b>Transparency</b>: less transparent.	Topic14 14.4.1 HedgeFunds
What is the <b>high-water mark</b> and <b>hurdle rate</b> in hedge fund fees?	<b>Hurdle rate</b>: minimum return (e.g. 'SONIA + 2%') before the fund earns a performance fee.<br><b>High-water mark</b>: performance fees only accrue on gains above the fund's previous peak NAV — so after a bad year, the manager must recover losses before earning performance fees again.	Topic14 14.4.4 HedgeFundFees
Describe the main <b>hedge fund strategies</b>.	(1) <b>Relative value / market neutral</b>: arbitrage between related instruments; aim for low/no correlation with traditional markets; highly quantitative.<br>(2) <b>Event-driven</b>: invest around corporate events (mergers, spin-offs, restructurings); sometimes called risk arbitrage.<br>(3) <b>Long/short equity</b>: directional bets on individual securities/sectors/countries; long on expected risers, short on expected fallers; <b>not automatically market neutral</b>; can have significant correlation with markets.<br>(4) <b>Tactical/directional trading</b>: speculate on market direction in currencies, commodities, equities, bonds; can be systematic (algorithmic) or discretionary; most volatile sector (includes LTCM, Soros' Quantum Fund).	Topic14 14.4.2 HedgeFundStrategies
What is a <b>fund of hedge funds</b> and what are its pros/cons?	A fund that invests in multiple hedge funds. <b>Pros</b>: diversification, access to hedge funds for smaller investors, access to manager expertise.<br><b>Cons</b>: <b>two layers of fees</b> (underlying fund fees + fund-of-funds manager fee).	Topic14 14.4.1 FundOfFunds
What are <b>unlisted (private) assets</b> and what types exist?	Investments not listed on an exchange: (1) <b>infrastructure</b> (roads, power grids, airports); (2) <b>property</b>; (3) <b>private equity</b>; (4) <b>private credit</b> (non-investment grade debt, infrastructure debt, real estate debt, corporate loans).<br>Benefits: less volatile, higher returns than listed assets, low correlation aiding diversification, less exposed to short-term market volatility, offer an <b>illiquidity premium</b>. Drawbacks: illiquid, infrequent valuations, high barriers to entry, large transaction costs, require specialist expertise.	Topic14 14.4.5 UnlistedAssets
How is <b>infrastructure investment</b> characterised?	Provides capital for essential services (transport, communication, utilities). <b>Higher gearing</b> than property (30%–90%, due to predictable revenues in regulated sectors). Tenures of 3–30 years. Relatively stable, income-generating, inflation-linked (often inelastic demand). Long investment horizons suit sovereign wealth funds and endowments.	Topic14 14.4.5 Infrastructure
What is <b>private credit</b>?	A generic term for non-bank debt: non-investment grade (junk) debt, corporate loans, infrastructure debt, real estate debt. Regarded as <b>relatively illiquid</b>. Offers diversification, potentially higher returns than public credit, and lower correlation with listed assets.	Topic14 14.4.5 PrivateCredit
What is <b>private equity</b> and what are its main forms?	Equity capital not quoted on a public exchange. Structured as <b>partnerships</b> (GP manages, LPs invest). Main forms:<br>(1) <b>Venture capital</b>: young companies, high risk/return, little revenue.<br>(2) <b>Growth capital</b>: minority stakes in mature companies to expand/restructure; no change of control.<br>(3) <b>Buy-outs</b> (MBOs/LBOs): taking mature companies private; direct influence; cost restructuring, non-core asset sales. Also <b>special situation funds</b> for distressed companies.	Topic14 14.4.5 PrivateEquity
Describe the <b>private equity fund lifecycle</b> and fee structure.	Investors commit capital upfront for ~<b>10 years</b>; drawn down as needed. First ~5 years = <b>'investing' period</b>; next ~5 years = <b>'harvesting' period</b>. Extensions possible; secondary market exists for LP interests. Structure is <b>tax transparent</b> (no tax leakage). Fees: management fee ~<b>1–2% of committed capital</b> + <b>~20% carried interest</b> on returns above a hurdle rate. Carried interest only distributed after successful exit.	Topic14 14.4.4 PEFees
Name the three types of <b>private equity investments</b>.	(1) <b>Primary</b>: newly constructed closed-end PE partnerships; LPs commit to a new fund; capital called as opportunities arise.<br>(2) <b>Secondary</b>: buying existing LP interests on the secondary market; shorter investment periods, accelerated returns, reduced blind-pool risk.<br>(3) <b>Co-investments</b>: direct investment by an LP alongside the PE fund in a specific company; often offered on a <b>no-fee, no-carry</b> basis; allows LPs to increase exposure to attractive deals.	Topic14 14.4.5 PEInvestmentTypes
What is the <b>J-curve</b> in private equity, and how is PE performance measured?	<b>J-curve</b>: cash outflows dominate the early years; returns typically turn positive after ~7 years, creating a J-shaped return profile.<br>Performance measures (because time-weighted returns are hard to calculate):<br>(1) <b>IRR</b> — captures timing of cash flows; particularly appropriate for PE.<br>(2) <b>TVPI</b> (Total Value to Paid-In) = (current value of remaining investments + total distributions) ÷ total capital paid in.<br>(3) <b>DPI</b> (Distributed to Paid-In) = cumulative distributions ÷ capital paid in. DPI &gt; 1 means the investor has received more than invested (net of fees/carry).<br>NAV = residual value of all investments remaining in the portfolio.	Topic14 14.4.6 PEPerformance
Why do private equity returns include a <b>liquidity premium</b>?	PE investments are <b>illiquid and long-term</b>; investors demand a premium to compensate. Historically PE has returned more than public markets (e.g. nearly 26% in year to June 2014 per Preqin), though the premium varies. Returns are further enhanced by <b>leverage</b>, but this requires careful risk management.	Topic14 14.4.6 PEReturns
