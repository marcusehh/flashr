What are the two components of <b>total return</b> on a portfolio?	(1) <b>Income</b> (dividends or coupons); (2) <b>capital value changes</b> (gains or losses).	Topic17 17.1.1 TotalReturn
State the <b>holding period return</b> formula (with income received at end of period).	$$R_p = \frac{(MV_E - MV_B) + I}{MV_B} \times 100\%$$<br>where MV_B = beginning market value, MV_E = ending market value (incl. reinvested income), I = income received at end of period.<br>Can be split into capital return (MV_E − MV_B)/MV_B and income return I/MV_B.	Topic17 17.1.1 TotalReturn
How is the <b>reinvestment return</b> on income calculated?	Terminal value of reinvested income: <b>TV = D × (1 + r)<sup>n</sup></b>.<br>Reinvestment return = TV − D.<br>E.g. £100 coupon reinvested 2 years at 5.5%: TV = £100 × 1.055² = £111.30, reinvestment return = £11.30.	Topic17 17.1.2 TotalReturn
How are returns <b>decomposed and attributed</b> for equities?	Total excess return vs benchmark = <b>asset allocation effect</b> + <b>stock selection effect</b> + <b>interaction effect</b>.<br>Asset allocation = intentional divergence of sector weights from benchmark. Stock selection = picking securities that differ from benchmark constituents. Interaction = combined impact of both decisions.	Topic17 17.1.3 Decomposition
How are <b>bond returns</b> decomposed?	Total return = <b>income effect</b> (coupon if yield curve unchanged) + <b>interest rate effect</b> (Treasury yield curve shift) + <b>sector/quality effect</b> (spread changes) + <b>residual</b>.<br>A consistently positive residual indicates superior bond selection skill. Yield curve shape changes are sometimes called <b>twists</b>.	Topic17 17.1.3 Decomposition
Why do client <b>deposits and withdrawals</b> complicate return measurement?	They change the portfolio's market value for reasons unrelated to the fund manager's performance, distorting simple return calculations (e.g. a £6m deposit inflates end value, overstating manager skill).	Topic17 17.2.1 MWRTWR
Define the <b>money-weighted rate of return</b> (MWRR) and state its formula.	The MWRR is the <b>internal rate of return (IRR)</b> of the portfolio:<br>$$MV_0(1+r) + \sum C_t(1+r)^{1-t} = MV_1$$<br>where MV₀ = beginning value, MV₁ = ending value, C_t = cash flow at time t, and (1 − t) = proportion of period remaining when the flow occurs. Solve for r by trial and error.	Topic17 17.2.2 MWRR
What is the main <b>limitation</b> of the MWRR for evaluating fund managers?	It places <b>greater weight on performance in periods when the account is largest</b> (hence 'money-weighted'). The timing and size of client cash flows — which the manager doesn't control — affect the result, so it may overstate or understate the manager's actual skill.	Topic17 17.2.1 MWRR
Define the <b>time-weighted rate of return</b> (TWRR) and state its formula.	TWRR gives <b>equal weight</b> to each sub-period's return, removing the distorting effect of cash flows:<br>$$R = \frac{MV_{t1}}{MV_0} \times \frac{MV_{t2}}{MV_{t1}+C_{t1}} \times \cdots \times \frac{MV_1}{MV_{tn}+C_{tn}} - 1$$<br>Requires knowing the portfolio value at <i>every</i> cash flow date.	Topic17 17.2.2 TWRR
When is <b>MWRR</b> vs <b>TWRR</b> most appropriate?	<b>TWRR</b>: preferred for evaluating <b>fund managers</b> (removes cash-flow timing effects they can't control); standard for benchmarking vs market indices; used for liquid assets (equities, fixed income).<br><b>MWRR</b>: useful for evaluating <b>personal/investor performance</b> (captures the investor's own timing decisions); used for <b>illiquid assets</b> (real estate, private equity) where frequent valuations are unavailable; useful when the investor controls cash flows (e.g. limited partners in PE).	Topic17 17.2.1 MWRTWR
What are the criteria for constructing an <b>appropriate benchmark</b>?	(1) <b>Specified in advance</b> (not chosen after the fact);<br>(2) <b>Appropriate</b> (risk-comparable to the manager's style);<br>(3) <b>Measurable</b> (can be regularly calculated);<br>(4) <b>Unambiguous and transparent</b> (clearly defined constituents and weights);<br>(5) <b>Investable</b> (manager could replicate it);<br>(6) <b>Availability of historical data</b>;<br>(7) <b>Low turnover</b> (stable composition).	Topic17 17.3.1 Benchmark
Name the key <b>types of benchmark</b> used in the investment management industry.	(1) <b>Broad market indices</b> (e.g. S&P 500, FTSE All-Share, MSCI World);<br>(2) <b>Style</b> benchmarks (e.g. small-cap, value — may be too narrow for some managers);<br>(3) <b>Absolute</b> benchmarks (a target rate of return, e.g. LIBOR + 3%; transparent but no passive investable index);<br>(4) <b>Peer group</b> comparisons (vs other managers — not a true benchmark: median unknown in advance, style may differ);<br>(5) <b>Customised</b> benchmarks (bespoke blend of indices weighted to reflect the investor's allocation/strategy).	Topic17 17.3.2 Benchmark
How is a <b>benchmark portfolio</b> constructed for performance attribution?	Create a portfolio with the <b>same initial asset mix and proportions</b> as the actual portfolio, but with each asset class earning the <b>benchmark index return</b>. Comparing actual vs benchmark portfolio decomposes performance into asset allocation and stock selection effects.	Topic17 17.3.3 Attribution
Explain the <b>Brinson-Fachler attribution model</b> using four portfolios.	<b>Portfolio I</b> (benchmark): strategic weights × benchmark returns = Σw<sub>i</sub><sup>P</sup>R<sub>i</sub><sup>P</sup>.<br><b>Portfolio II</b> (stock-selected): strategic weights × actual returns = Σw<sub>i</sub><sup>P</sup>R<sub>i</sub><sup>a</sup>.<br><b>Portfolio III</b> (timing): actual weights × benchmark returns = Σw<sub>i</sub><sup>a</sup>R<sub>i</sub><sup>P</sup>.<br><b>Portfolio IV</b> (actual): actual weights × actual returns = Σw<sub>i</sub><sup>a</sup>R<sub>i</sub><sup>a</sup>.<br><b>Timing effect</b> = R(III) − R(I); <b>Selection effect</b> = R(II) − R(I); <b>Interaction</b> = R(I) − R(II) − R(III) + R(IV).<br>Total contribution = R(IV) − R(I).	Topic17 17.3.3 Attribution
Why is <b>risk analysis</b> important in performance evaluation?	Two portfolios with the same return may have very different risk. Risk-adjusted measures reveal how much risk the manager took to achieve returns — investors need to know whether excess return adequately compensates for extra risk taken. Focus is on <b>ex-post</b> (historic) measures based on variability and volatility.	Topic17 17.4.1 RiskAdjusted
State the <b>Sharpe measure</b> formula. What does it measure and what is its limitation?	$$\text{Sharpe} = \frac{R_p - R_f}{SD_p}$$<br>Measures excess return per unit of <b>total risk</b> (standard deviation). Higher = better risk-adjusted performance.<br><b>Limitation</b>: misleading when both portfolios have <b>negative</b> excess returns — a higher SD then produces a less negative (seemingly better) ratio for the worse portfolio.	Topic17 17.4.2 Sharpe
State the <b>Treynor measure</b> formula. When is it preferred over Sharpe?	$$\text{Treynor} = \frac{R_p - R_f}{\beta_p}$$<br>Measures excess return per unit of <b>systematic risk</b> (beta). Higher = better.<br>Preferred for <b>well-diversified</b> portfolios where unsystematic risk has been diversified away (so only beta matters). If the portfolio is <b>not</b> well diversified, Sharpe (which captures total risk) may be more appropriate.	Topic17 17.4.2 Treynor
State the <b>information ratio</b> formula. How does it differ from Sharpe/Treynor?	$$IR = \frac{R_p - R_B}{SD_{surplus}}$$<br>where R_B = benchmark return and SD<sub>surplus</sub> = standard deviation of (R_p − R_B), known as <b>tracking error</b>.<br>Unlike Sharpe/Treynor, it measures excess return over the <b>benchmark</b> (not the risk-free rate) per unit of tracking error. A negative IR means the manager underperformed the benchmark.	Topic17 17.4.2 InformationRatio
State <b>Jensen's alpha</b> formula and interpret it.	$$\alpha = R_p - R_b$$<br>where R_b = R_f + β_p(R_m − R_f) is the CAPM expected return for the portfolio's beta.<br>Positive α = manager outperformed the risk-adjusted benchmark (skill). Negative α = underperformance.<br><b>Limitation</b>: may be inappropriate if the manager takes on substantial <b>specific (diversifiable) risk</b> beyond what beta captures.	Topic17 17.4.2 Jensen
How is bond portfolio performance measured on a <b>duration-adjusted</b> basis?	$$P = \frac{r_p - r_f}{D_p / D_m}$$<br>where D_p and D_m are the portfolio and market durations. Analogous to Treynor for equities but uses <b>relative duration</b> instead of beta as the risk measure. A higher value indicates better risk-adjusted performance.	Topic17 17.4.2 BondPerformance
How can <b>total return be decomposed</b> into risk-adjusted components?	Total return = <b>risk-free return</b> + <b>benchmark return</b> (from beta exposure) + <b>return due to market timing</b> + <b>return due to diversifiable risk</b> + <b>return due to selection</b> (alpha).<br>Jensen's alpha may overstate skill if the manager increased specific risk without adequate extra return. A more appropriate benchmark can be constructed matching the <b>total risk</b> (SD) of the managed fund to correctly assess whether extra risk was rewarded.	Topic17 17.4.3 Decomposition
What is <b>tracking error</b> and what causes it?	The <b>standard deviation of the differences</b> between a fund's returns and its benchmark index returns over time.<br>Causes: dealing costs and timing of purchases, taxation, reinvestment of income, rebalancing, and the method of index replication (full replication vs stratified sampling).	Topic17 17.4.3 TrackingError
What problem arises when assessing risk-adjusted returns in relation to <b>market timing</b>?	Risk measures (SD, beta) only capture the <b>average</b> risk over the period. If a manager varied the portfolio's risk profile to time the market (e.g. increasing beta before a rally), the average risk measure may be misleading. One solution is to calculate risk on a <b>rolling basis</b> and check if risk increases preceded market rises.	Topic17 17.4.3 Timing
