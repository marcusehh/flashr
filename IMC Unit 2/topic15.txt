What is meant by <b>dominance</b> between investment strategies?	A strategy <b>dominates</b> another if it has a higher expected return for the same or lower risk. A portfolio dominates if: (1) for a given risk, it has higher return; (2) for a given return, it has lower risk; or (3) it has both lower risk and higher return.	Topic15 15.1.1 RiskReturn
Why is it important to distinguish <b>ex-ante</b> from <b>ex-post</b> risk measures?	<b>Ex-ante</b> = before the event (forecast/expected). <b>Ex-post</b> = after the event (actual/realised). They may differ substantially — what was expected may not match what occurred, so both are needed for proper investment analysis.	Topic15 15.1.2 RiskMeasurement
What are the commonly used <b>measures of risk</b> in investment analysis?	(1) <b>Standard deviation</b> (most common); (2) <b>Semi-variance</b>; (3) <b>Probability of shortfall</b>; (4) <b>Expected shortfall</b>; (5) <b>Value at Risk (VaR)</b>; (6) <b>Drawdown</b>; (7) <b>Tracking error</b>; (8) <b>Active share</b>.	Topic15 15.1.3 RiskMeasurement
What are the <b>advantages and disadvantages</b> of standard deviation as a risk measure?	<b>Advantages</b>: intuitive, widely understood, captures dispersion of returns.<br><b>Disadvantages</b>: (1) based on past data — may not predict future; (2) captures <i>both</i> upside and downside (investors mainly care about downside); (3) assumes upside/downside equally likely; (4) volatility alone is incomplete (e.g. ignores inflation risk); (5) <b>autocorrelation</b> in returns (property, hedge funds) can understate risk.	Topic15 15.1.4 StdDev
What are <b>semi-variance</b>, <b>probability of shortfall</b> and <b>expected shortfall</b>?	<b>Semi-variance</b>: like variance but only measures returns <i>below</i> the mean (downside risk only).<br><b>Probability of shortfall</b>: chance of a return falling below a target level (does not give the <i>size</i> of loss).<br><b>Expected shortfall</b>: the expected loss in the worst x% of cases (an absolute measure, e.g. in the 5% worst years, the expected loss might be £5m).	Topic15 15.1.3 RiskMeasurement
What are the implications of assuming returns are <b>normally distributed</b>?	The normal distribution is symmetric and fully described by mean and standard deviation; ~95% of observations lie within ±2 SDs. However, real asset returns have <b>fat tails</b> — extreme events occur more often than predicted. Using a normal assumption may <b>underestimate</b> the probability of crashes and the capital needed to protect against losses.	Topic15 15.1.5 NormalDist
Define <b>Value at Risk (VaR)</b> and state its three key features.	VaR estimates the <b>maximum expected loss</b> on a portfolio over a given <b>time period</b> at a given <b>confidence level</b>. Three features: (1) a time period; (2) a confidence/probability level; (3) a loss amount/percentage.<br>E.g. a 99% daily VaR of £10m means on 99% of days the loss will not exceed £10m.	Topic15 15.1.6 VaR
Name the <b>three methods</b> for calculating VaR and a key limitation of VaR.	(1) <b>Historical return approach</b> (rank past returns, read off the percentile); (2) <b>Variance-covariance method</b> (assumes normal distribution — worst 1% lies beyond 2.58 SDs); (3) <b>Monte Carlo simulation</b> (model random trials of returns).<br><b>Key limitation</b>: fat tails mean extreme losses occur more often than VaR predicts. VaR is a probability statement, not a true maximum — losses can exceed VaR. Past may not guide the future.	Topic15 15.1.6 VaR
How do you <b>scale</b> standard deviation across time periods?	Multiply by the <b>square root of time</b>: σ(monthly) = σ(daily) × √T.<br>E.g. if daily SD = 2% and 20 trading days/month: monthly SD = 2% × √20 = 8.94%.	Topic15 15.1.6 VaR
Define <b>drawdown</b> and state its advantages and disadvantages.	Drawdown = the decline from a <b>historical peak</b> value. It measures the maximum loss an investor could have experienced.<br><b>Advantage</b>: refers to empirical reality, more intuitive than abstract volatility.<br><b>Disadvantages</b>: (1) longer time series → larger drawdown (survival bias); (2) maximum drawdown is a single number with large uncertainty — too little data to draw strong inferences.	Topic15 15.1.7 Drawdown
What is <b>relative drawdown</b>?	The maximum cumulative underperformance of a portfolio <b>relative to a benchmark</b>. Investors may use it to limit how far behind a manager can fall — too much cumulative underperformance may be too difficult to recover from.	Topic15 15.1.7 Drawdown
Define <b>tracking error</b> and give its formula.	Tracking error = the difference between a portfolio's return and its benchmark return. The standard way to calculate it is the <b>standard deviation</b> of the return differences over time:<br>$$TE = \sqrt{\frac{\sum_{i=1}^{N}(R_P - R_B)^2}{N-1}}$$<br>Low TE → closely follows benchmark; high TE → significant deviation.	Topic15 15.1.8 TrackingError
What factors determine a portfolio's <b>tracking error</b>?	(1) Common securities between portfolio and benchmark; (2) differences in capitalisation, timing, style; (3) differences in asset weightings; (4) management fees, custodial fees, brokerage costs; (5) pricing timing differences; (6) benchmark volatility; (7) portfolio beta.	Topic15 15.1.8 TrackingError
What are the advantages and disadvantages of <b>tracking error</b>?	<b>Advantages</b>: indicates how actively a fund is managed; can set acceptable performance ranges; useful for comparing managers.<br><b>Disadvantages</b>: outperformance also raises TE (may suggest excess risk when the manager was simply skilled); TE is also affected by changing market volatility — calibration from low-volatility periods may mislead in high-volatility periods.	Topic15 15.1.8 TrackingError
What is <b>volatility clustering</b> and how does it affect risk measures?	Periods of high volatility tend to follow each other, and likewise for low volatility (autocorrelation in volatility). This means risk measures like tracking error may be useful for prediction <i>only if</i> the level of price volatility is not expected to change.	Topic15 15.1.9 Volatility
Define <b>relative weight</b> and <b>active share</b>.	<b>Relative weight</b> = the value of a security as a proportion of the portfolio (or benchmark).<br><b>Active share</b> = ½ × Σ|weight_portfolio,i − weight_benchmark,i|. It measures how different a portfolio is from its benchmark. Ranges from 0% (index tracker) to 100% (no overlap). An active share of 20–60% suggests a <b>closet tracker</b>.	Topic15 15.1.10 ActiveShare
What are the advantages and disadvantages of <b>active share</b>?	<b>Advantages</b>: identifies closet trackers charging active fees; complements tracking error as a second measure of active management.<br><b>Disadvantages</b>: sensitive to choice of benchmark — an inappropriate benchmark or one with many constituents inflates active share; a small benchmark deflates it.	Topic15 15.1.10 ActiveShare
Explain <b>diversification</b> and its role in constructing efficient portfolios.	Diversification = adding assets whose returns do not perfectly follow the same pattern. It reduces <b>unsystematic (specific) risk</b> by holding many assets from different sectors/markets. As the number of holdings grows, individual weights shrink and specific risk variances (multiplied by weight²) become negligible.<br><b>Limitation</b>: in extreme market conditions, correlations converge towards +1, reducing diversification benefits.	Topic15 15.1.11 Diversification
Define <b>systematic</b> and <b>unsystematic</b> risk and state the additive rule.	<b>Unsystematic (specific)</b>: risk unique to one asset (e.g. director illness, factory fire) — diversifiable.<br><b>Systematic (factor/market)</b>: affects all assets (e.g. recession, interest rate changes) — <i>not</i> fully diversifiable.<br><b>Total risk</b> = systematic risk + unsystematic risk (variances are additive because specific risks are uncorrelated). Note: <b>standard deviations</b> are <i>not</i> additive.	Topic15 15.3.5 Risk
How do you calculate <b>systematic standard deviation</b> given total and specific risk?	Use: Total variance = Systematic variance + Specific variance.<br>E.g. total SD = 14%, specific SD = 10%: 14² = S² + 10² → S² = 96 → S = √96 = 9.8%.	Topic15 15.3.5 Risk
What is <b>offsetting</b> of systematic risk?	Holding two assets with opposite sensitivities to the same factor so the net effect is zero. E.g. a brewing company (hurt by hop price rise) and a hop-farming company (helped by hop price rise). Portfolio managers use this to neutralise specific factor exposures.	Topic15 15.1.11 Diversification
Explain the importance of <b>correlation</b> in portfolio construction and interpret correlation coefficients.	The <b>correlation coefficient (r)</b> measures the linear association between two assets' returns, ranging from −1 to +1.<br><b>r = −1</b>: perfect negative (move opposite) — risk can theoretically be eliminated.<br><b>r = +1</b>: perfect positive (move together) — no risk reduction.<br><b>0 < r < +1</b>: positive correlation (close to +1 = strong).<br><b>−1 < r < 0</b>: negative correlation.<br><b>r = 0</b>: no relationship.<br>The <b>lower</b> the correlation, the greater the diversification benefit and the more dominant the resulting portfolio.	Topic15 15.1.12 Correlation
Give the formula for the <b>correlation coefficient</b> from covariance and standard deviations.	$$r = \frac{\text{Cov}(x,y)}{\sigma_x \times \sigma_y}$$<br>E.g. Cov = −30, σ_x = 13%, σ_y = 10%: r = −30 / (13 × 10) = −30/130 = −0.23 (weak negative correlation — combining them improves risk/return).	Topic15 15.1.13 Correlation
What are the <b>shortfalls/limitations of correlations</b>?	(1) In extreme markets, correlations <b>converge to +1</b> — diversification fails when most needed.<br>(2) Correlations change over time, so designed offsets can break down.<br>(3) Correlation is <b>meaningless</b> if distributions are not approximately normal.<br>(4) Only captures <b>linear</b> association — many products have non-linear relationships.<br>(5) Correlation ≠ causation — high correlation may be transitory or driven by a common third factor.	Topic15 15.1.12 Correlation
List and briefly explain <b>other types of investment risk</b> (inflation, currency, interest rate, fraud, liquidity, counterparty).	<b>Inflation risk</b>: unanticipated price changes erode purchasing power. IL bonds, equities, property may hedge it.<br><b>Currency risk</b>: FX movements affect returns on overseas assets. Split into <b>transaction risk</b> (hedgeable with forwards) and <b>translation risk</b> (accounting).<br><b>Interest rate risk</b>: variability in asset values as rates change; measured by duration; hedged with shorter-duration bonds or swaps.<br><b>Fraud risk</b>: deception for gain — false advertising, embezzlement, identity theft.<br><b>Liquidity risk</b>: inability to convert an asset to cash without significant loss (e.g. MBS in 2008).<br><b>Counterparty risk</b>: risk that the other party defaults on obligations; assessed via credit ratings.	Topic15 15.1.14 InvestmentRisk
Describe the typical <b>correlation matrix</b> between major asset classes and its relevance for asset allocation.	Cash, gilts, UK equity, commercial property and hedge funds typically show <b>low or negative</b> correlations between equities and bonds, cash and property. Adding assets with low correlations to equities/bonds (e.g. property, hedge funds) can diversify a mainstream portfolio. However, correlations are <b>not constant</b> — they vary over time; e.g. US/UK stock correlation varied from +0.2 (1970) to +0.9 (2005) to +0.7 (2014–16). Equities/bonds can flip between positive and negative correlation.	Topic15 15.2.1 AssetCorrelation
What are the <b>limitations of correlation analysis in extreme market conditions</b>?	During financial stress, all asset classes tend to become <b>highly positively correlated</b> (risk aversion leads to selling all risky assets). Portfolios built on low historical correlations can suddenly find diversification benefits disappear. Also, <b>private assets</b> (property, infrastructure) may have unreliable correlation data due to infrequent/irregular pricing.	Topic15 15.2.2 ExtremeCorrelation
What methods are used to <b>forecast correlations</b>?	(1) <b>Extrapolate historical averages</b>; (2) Use factor models (CAPM or APT) to <b>derive implied correlations</b> from the factor betas driving returns. Forecasting correlations is difficult but crucial for constructing mean-variance efficient frontiers.	Topic15 15.2.2 ExtremeCorrelation
List the <b>assumptions of the CAPM</b> and identify other factors in common use.	CAPM assumptions: (1) markets are perfectly competitive, free of tax and transaction costs; (2) all investors agree on the same investment period and have homogeneous expectations about returns and SDs; (3) investors can borrow/lend at the same risk-free rate; (4) investors maximise return while minimising risk.<br><b>Other factors</b> beyond market beta: firm <b>size</b>, firm <b>value</b> (dividend yield, P/B ratio), <b>momentum</b> (winners vs losers).	Topic15 15.3.1 CAPM
State the <b>Security Market Line (SML)</b> equation and define each component.	$$E(R_p) = R_f + \beta_p \times (E(R_m) - R_f)$$<br><b>E(R_p)</b> = expected return on portfolio p.<br><b>R_f</b> = risk-free rate (e.g. T-bill yield).<br><b>E(R_m)</b> = expected return on the market portfolio.<br><b>β_p</b> = portfolio's CAPM beta.<br><b>E(R_m) − R_f</b> = market-risk premium.<br>Proxies: R_f = short-term gilt/T-bill; R_m = broad equity index (e.g. FTSE All Share).	Topic15 15.3.2 SML
What does <b>beta</b> measure, and how is it calculated? Interpret beta values of &lt;1, =1, and &gt;1.	Beta measures a security's <b>systematic risk</b> relative to the market.<br>$$\beta_p = \frac{\text{Cov}(R_p, R_m)}{\text{Var}(R_m)}$$<br><b>β = 1</b>: moves with the market (same risk as the market).<br><b>β < 1</b>: less risky/volatile than the market (e.g. utilities).<br><b>β > 1</b>: riskier/more volatile than the market (e.g. tech stocks).<br>In practice, beta is estimated by regressing security returns against market returns; the slope of the <b>characteristic line</b> is the beta.	Topic15 15.3.2 15.3.3 Beta
Work through a <b>CAPM calculation</b>: R_f = 3%, E(R_m) = 8%. Calculate expected returns for β = 0.72, 1.00, and 1.44.	Market-risk premium = 8% − 3% = 5%.<br>β = 0.72: E(R) = 3% + 0.72 × 5% = <b>6.6%</b>.<br>β = 1.00: E(R) = 3% + 1.00 × 5% = <b>8.0%</b> (= market return).<br>β = 1.44: E(R) = 3% + 1.44 × 5% = <b>10.2%</b>.<br>Higher beta → higher expected return (compensation for greater systematic risk).	Topic15 15.3.2 CAPM
How do you <b>calculate the beta of a portfolio</b>?	Portfolio beta = weighted average of constituent betas:<br>$$\beta_p = \sum w_i \beta_i$$<br>E.g. 25% in A (β=0.75), 50% in B (β=1.00), 25% in C (β=1.44): β_p = (0.25×0.75)+(0.50×1.00)+(0.25×1.44) = <b>1.05</b>.	Topic15 15.3.4 PortfolioBeta
How is <b>total risk</b> decomposed using the CAPM beta?	$$VS_p = \beta_p^2 \times VS_m + VS_e$$<br>VS_p = total variance of portfolio; VS_m = variance of market; VS_e = unsystematic (diversifiable) variance.<br>For a well-diversified portfolio, VS_e → 0, so: VS_p ≈ β_p² × VS_m.	Topic15 15.3.6 15.3.8 TotalRisk
How do you calculate beta from the variance of market returns and covariance of investment return with market return?	$$\beta = \frac{\text{Cov}(R_p, R_m)}{\text{Var}(R_m)}$$<br>E.g. Var(R_m) = 120%, covariances with market for X, Y, Z = 60%, 80%, 150%: β_X = 60/120 = 0.5; β_Y = 80/120 = 0.667; β_Z = 150/120 = 1.25.	Topic15 15.3.7 15.3.8 Beta
What are the <b>limitations of the CAPM</b>?	(1) Assumptions are unrealistic (no taxes, homogeneous expectations, borrowing at risk-free rate).<br>(2) Beta is <b>not a reliable predictor</b> of returns in practice — small firms earn higher returns than their beta predicts (the <b>size anomaly</b>).<br>(3) Led to extensions: three-factor CAPM (adds size and value factors). However, relaxing the theoretical assumptions tends <i>not</i> to change CAPM's main predictions — it is the <b>empirical</b> shortcomings that drive extensions.	Topic15 15.3.9 CAPM
Explain <b>Arbitrage Pricing Theory (APT)</b> and how it differs from CAPM.	APT models expected return as a <b>linear function of multiple factors</b>, each with its own beta. Factors can include oil prices, inflation, GNP surprises, yield curve shifts, default premiums.<br><b>Difference from CAPM</b>: CAPM is a <b>single-factor</b> model (market risk only); APT is <b>multi-factor</b>. APT does not require the restrictive CAPM assumptions. Factor sensitivities are asset-specific (e.g. BP is sensitive to oil price; IAG is sensitive to oil differently).	Topic15 15.3.10 APT
What macro-economic factors have been identified as important for explaining stock returns?	(1) Surprises in <b>inflation</b>; (2) surprises in <b>GNP</b> (via industrial production); (3) surprises in <b>investor confidence</b> (changes in default premiums on corporate bonds); (4) surprise shifts in the <b>yield curve</b>.<br>However, these are measured retrospectively with publication lags, so real-time factors like oil prices or futures indices may be more practical.	Topic15 15.3.10 APT
Define <b>factor investing</b> and <b>smart beta</b>. Name the most popular single factors.	A <b>factor</b> is a security attribute persistently associated with returns. <b>Factor investing</b> seeks to capture these return drivers.<br><b>Smart beta</b> = ETFs/portfolios using transparent, rules-based methodologies to exploit factors (as opposed to market-cap weighting).<br>Popular factors: (1) low volatility; (2) momentum; (3) value; (4) quality; (5) high dividend yield; (6) size.<br>Smart beta strategies weight stocks differently from market-cap (e.g. equal-weight, inverse-volatility). Factor performance is <b>cyclical</b> — each factor can underperform for long periods.	Topic15 15.3.11 SmartBeta
What is the <b>Efficient Markets Hypothesis (EMH)</b> and what are its three forms?	EMH = security prices <b>instantaneously reflect all available information</b>. If true, prices = fair values, and active trading cannot consistently outperform passive strategies.<br><b>Weak form</b>: prices reflect all <i>past market data</i> (prices/volume) → technical analysis is useless; only fundamental analysis or inside info can beat the market.<br><b>Semi-strong form</b>: prices reflect all <i>publicly available</i> information → fundamental analysis also useless; only inside info works.<br><b>Strong form</b>: prices reflect <i>all</i> information including inside info → no one can consistently beat the market. (Extreme and unlikely — implies insider trading is pointless.)	Topic15 15.4.1 EMH
What conditions are needed for an <b>efficient market</b> to exist?	(1) Many rational, profit-maximising investors actively participating; (2) securities are valued rationally; (3) if some investors are irrational, their behaviour is random and cancels out — or arbitrageurs eliminate the mispricing; (4) information is freely and widely available.	Topic15 15.4.1 EMH
What are the main <b>market anomalies</b> that challenge the EMH?	(1) <b>Momentum</b> and <b>mean reversion</b> (predictable patterns in returns); (2) <b>seasonal variations</b> (though tend to disappear once published); (3) <b>post-earnings announcement drift</b>; (4) <b>Size effect</b>: small-cap firms earn higher returns than predicted by CAPM beta; (5) <b>Value effect</b>: high dividend yield / low P/E firms outperform. Led to the <b>three-factor CAPM</b> (market + size + value). These are also known as returns in <b>excess of that predicted by beta</b>.	Topic15 15.4.2 Anomalies
Explain the <b>behavioural finance</b> school and how it differs from standard finance.	Standard finance assumes: (1) investors are rational; (2) markets are efficient; (3) mean-variance portfolio theory; (4) returns depend on risk alone.<br>Behavioural finance says: (1) investors are <i>normal</i> (not always rational); (2) markets are hard to beat but <i>not efficient</i>; (3) portfolios follow behavioural rules, not mean-variance; (4) returns are driven by factors <i>and</i> cognitive biases.	Topic15 15.4.3 BehaviouralFinance
Name and explain the key <b>behavioural biases</b> in investment decision-making.	<b>Information processing errors</b>: <b>Memory bias</b> (overweight recent experience); <b>Overconfidence</b> (overestimate precision of forecasts — active traders underperform); <b>Confirmation bias</b> (favour info confirming existing views); <b>Conservatism</b> (too slow to update beliefs → momentum); <b>Sample size neglect</b> (extrapolate from small samples).<br><b>Framing/emotional biases</b>: <b>Loss aversion / regret avoidance</b> (reluctance to realise losses); <b>Endowment effect</b> (overvalue what you own); <b>Prospect theory</b> (sell winners too soon, hold losers too long); <b>Anchoring</b> (fixate on irrelevant reference points); <b>Faulty framing</b> (fail to mark to market); <b>Hindsight bias</b> (think you "knew it all along"); <b>Herd behaviour</b> (follow the crowd); <b>Mental accounting</b> (segregate decisions, e.g. savings account + credit card debt simultaneously).	Topic15 15.4.4 BehaviouralBiases
What is <b>noise trader risk</b>?	The unpredictable actions of irrational investors who trade on incomplete information. Arbitrageurs try to correct mispricing, but noise traders create <b>additional risk</b> that can block the corrective forces, allowing mispricing to persist.	Topic15 15.4.4 BehaviouralFinance
Explain <b>financial amnesia</b> and <b>bubbles</b>.	<b>Financial amnesia</b>: market participants behave as if they have forgotten lessons of financial history. After prolonged stability, asset prices detach from fundamentals, driven by easy credit and complacent regulation.<br><b>Bubble</b>: extreme overvaluation of assets relative to fundamentals. Driven by: innovation (belief in a "new era"), herd behaviour, cognitive dissonance, overconfidence, narrow framing, recency bias. Discipline fails due to: poor corporate governance, moral hazard, originate-and-distribute model, regulatory capture/groupthink.	Topic15 15.4.5 Bubbles
What is <b>fair value</b> and what are the three levels under FASB 157?	Fair value = the price at which an asset could be willingly exchanged in a current transaction (excluding liquidation). Hard to determine for illiquid or non-traded assets.<br><b>Level 1</b>: quoted prices from <b>active markets</b> for identical assets (most reliable).<br><b>Level 2</b>: prices from <b>similar but not identical</b> assets.<br><b>Level 3</b>: <b>unobservable inputs</b> / assumptions by market participants ("mark to management").<br>Advantage of FASB 157: captures hard-to-value assets on balance sheet. Disadvantage: current depressed prices can force large write-downs that exaggerate true losses.	Topic15 15.5.1 FairValue
Explain the relationship between <b>liquidity and investment strategy capacity</b>.	As AUM grows, the cost of implementing a strategy <b>rises</b> (wider bid-offer spreads, market impact on illiquid securities), potentially eroding performance.<br><b>Threshold capacity</b>: AUM level that still allows the stated return objective.<br><b>Wealth-maximising capacity</b>: AUM that maximises alpha × AUM (net of costs).<br><b>Terminal capacity</b>: AUM at which alpha net of costs = 0.<br>Managers may <b>soft close</b> (stop new clients) or <b>hard close</b> (stop all inflows) to protect performance.	Topic15 15.5.2 Liquidity
Distinguish <b>top-down</b> from <b>bottom-up</b> fund management.	<b>Top-down</b>: start with broad asset class allocation (equities, bonds, cash), then tactical deviations, then individual security selection within classes.<br><b>Bottom-up</b>: select individual securities on their own merits; portfolio composition emerges from stock-level analysis. Becoming <b>less popular</b> as quantitative tools allow more precise style/liability matching.	Topic15 15.6.1 FundMgmt
Distinguish <b>strategic</b> from <b>tactical</b> asset allocation.	<b>Strategic</b>: long-term target weights across asset classes (held over 5–10 years). Determined by benchmark, liability profile, or actuarial advice.<br><b>Tactical</b>: short-term deviations from strategic weights based on the manager's near-term views. Ranges should be narrower if transaction costs are high or manager skill is limited.	Topic15 15.6.3 AssetAllocation
Distinguish <b>active</b> from <b>passive</b> fund management and explain costs/benefits.	<b>Passive (index tracking)</b>: mimic a benchmark index. Lower fees, lower turnover, low tracking error. Problem: no protection in bear markets; risk/return of tracked index may change.<br><b>Active</b>: seek to outperform via security selection, timing, or mispricing. Higher fees, higher specific risk. Challenge: more invested in mispriced assets → more risk; may yield higher or lower returns than passive.<br><b>Portfolio tilting</b> combines both: hold all index constituents but <b>tilt</b> weights (e.g. overweight favoured stocks by 0.1–2%).	Topic15 15.6.4 ActivePassive
What is meant by <b>management style</b>? Describe <b>value</b> and <b>growth</b> styles.	Style = portfolios grouped by similar characteristics and performance patterns. Two main styles:<br><b>Value</b>: seek undervalued shares — low P/E, low price-to-book, high dividend yields. Sub-styles: (i) low P/E, (ii) contrarian (low P/B, expecting turnaround), (iii) high yield. Focus on defensive/cyclical sectors out of favour.<br><b>Growth</b>: seek companies with above-average earnings growth — higher P/E, often in consumer, service, healthcare, tech. Smaller growth stocks tend to have high betas (above-average in bull markets, below in bear).	Topic15 15.6.5 Style
Describe the <b>IA mixed asset sector definitions</b> for fund classification.	<b>Mixed Investment 0–35% Shares</b>: equities 0–35%, ≥45% in fixed income/cash, ≥80% in established currencies.<br><b>Mixed Investment 20–60% Shares</b>: equities 20–60%, ≥30% in fixed income/cash, ≥60% in established currencies.<br><b>Mixed Investment 40–85% Shares</b>: equities 40–85%, no fixed income minimum, ≥60% in established currencies.<br><b>Flexible Investment</b>: no minimum for any component; no currency restriction.<br>Higher equity % → higher portfolio volatility/risk.	Topic15 15.6.5 PortfolioComposition
What is <b>risk budgeting</b>?	Allocating assets (i.e. constructing the portfolio) based on their <b>risk characteristics</b>, using measures like standard deviation, beta, or VaR. This forces the manager to explicitly consider risk trade-offs rather than just targeting returns.	Topic15 15.6.5 RiskBudgeting
What are the <b>two objectives</b> of bond portfolio management?	(1) <b>Match future liabilities</b> (pension fund/insurance company); (2) <b>Achieve/surpass a benchmark return</b> (bond index).<br>Techniques for liability matching: <b>cash matching (dedication)</b> and <b>immunisation</b>.	Topic15 15.7.1 FixedIncome
Explain <b>cash matching (dedication)</b> for bond portfolios.	Purchase bonds so that coupons + principal received each period <b>exactly match</b> each cash outflow. No reinvestment rate risk (income not reinvested) and no interest rate risk (bonds not sold before maturity). Called a <b>dedicated portfolio</b>. Very restrictive and hard to achieve perfectly in practice.	Topic15 15.7.1 CashMatching
Explain <b>immunisation</b> and how it works.	Choose a bond portfolio with the <b>same duration</b> as the liability it is intended to meet. This neutralises the opposing effects of interest rate changes: if rates rise, reinvested coupon income increases (<b>reinvestment risk</b>) but bond prices fall (<b>price risk</b>); with matching duration, these roughly <b>offset</b>.<br>E.g. a £1,000 liability in 2 years → buy a bond with duration = 2 years (its maturity may be longer, e.g. 4 years, but the duration matches).	Topic15 15.7.1 15.7.2 Immunisation
What are the <b>assumptions and challenges</b> of immunisation?	Assumes: (1) flat yield curve; (2) parallel shifts in the yield curve; (3) no defaults or early redemptions.<br>Challenges: duration changes as time passes and yields change → requires <b>periodic rebalancing</b> (frequent = precise but costly; infrequent = cheaper but increases duration mismatch).	Topic15 15.7.1 Immunisation
How is the <b>duration of a bond portfolio</b> calculated?	Weighted average of individual bond durations:<br>Portfolio duration = Σ(w_i × D_i).<br>E.g. 40% in bond A (duration 5y) + 60% in bond B (duration 10y) = (0.40 × 5) + (0.60 × 10) = <b>8 years</b>.	Topic15 15.7.2 Duration
What is <b>contingent immunisation</b>?	An active manager starts with discretion over duration and yield positions. Performance is regularly reviewed against the liability. If the portfolio performs poorly, the manager switches to a fully <b>immunised</b> (passive, duration-matched) position that is locked in and irrevocable until the liability date. A "safety net" strategy.	Topic15 15.7.1 ContingentImmunisation
Explain <b>credit risk management</b> for bond portfolios.	Managers trade bonds in anticipation of <b>credit rating changes</b> — sell bonds expected to be downgraded, buy bonds in sectors expected to outperform at certain stages of the economic cycle. This aims to enhance returns by exploiting anticipated shifts in credit quality.	Topic15 15.7.1 CreditRisk
Explain the <b>riding the yield curve</b> strategy and calculate a theoretical gain.	Buy a bond with a <b>longer maturity than the investment horizon</b> and sell it when it has "rolled down" the curve to a lower yield (higher price).<br>E.g. 1-year zero-coupon bond priced at £94.79 (return = 5.5%). A 2-year zero at £89.42 will, if the yield curve is unchanged, be priced at £94.79 in one year (when it becomes a 1-year bond). Return = (94.79 − 89.42)/89.42 = <b>6.0%</b>. Additional return = 6.0% − 5.5% = <b>0.5%</b>.<br>Assumes: yield curve shape <b>does not change</b> and the relative mispricing persists.	Topic15 15.7.3 YieldCurve
Explain <b>barbell</b> and <b>bullet</b> bond portfolio strategies.	Both target the same portfolio duration but differ in dispersion:<br><b>Bullet</b>: bonds with durations <b>close to</b> the target liability duration. Lower immunisation risk from non-parallel yield curve shifts.<br><b>Barbell</b>: bonds with durations <b>much shorter and much longer</b> than the target. Wider range of bond selection but greater immunisation risk if yield curve shifts are non-parallel.<br>E.g. target duration 8y: bullet = 50% in 7y + 50% in 9y bonds; barbell = 50% in 4y + 50% in 12y bonds.	Topic15 15.7.4 BarbellBullet
What is <b>Liability-Driven Investment (LDI)</b>?	An investment framework that seeks to <b>match assets to liabilities</b>. Associated mainly with DB pension funds and insurers. Key aim: manage <b>funding level risk</b> (variability of assets vs liabilities). Uses bonds, swaps, and derivatives to hedge inflation and interest rate risk. LDI evolved from simple cash-flow matching to a holistic framework placing liabilities at the centre of investment decisions.	Topic15 15.7.5 LDI
Describe the <b>four stages</b> of implementing an LDI strategy.	(1) <b>Forecast funding needs</b> and identify a portfolio with similar sensitivities to inflation/interest rates to at least approximately match liability cash flows (swaps can help).<br>(2) <b>Determine acceptable risk</b> — trustees specify overall risk constraints (liability matching is central).<br>(3) <b>Assess active management potential</b> given the asset allocation.<br>(4) <b>Implement</b> the strategy, possibly using new vehicles (e.g. hedge funds, bespoke swaps).	Topic15 15.7.6 LDI
What is the <b>swap spread</b> and what drives it in LDI?	Swap spread = difference between <b>government bond yields</b> and <b>swap rates</b>. LDI portfolios hold both, so movements in the spread create a mismatch between asset and liability valuations.<br>Drivers: (1) government fiscal position/credit risk; (2) financing risk and bank balance sheet shrinkage; (3) monetary/fiscal policy (QE reduces yields → lowers swap spread); (4) bank regulation (leverage ratios make repo financing less attractive).	Topic15 15.7.5 SwapSpread
What happened in the <b>UK LDI crisis of October 2022</b>?	The September 2022 "mini-budget" caused a sharp rise in gilt yields. ~60% of DB pension schemes held leveraged LDI products. Rising yields triggered <b>collateral calls</b> that many schemes couldn't meet quickly. Schemes were forced to sell gilts (further depressing prices/raising yields), eroding the LDI cushion. The Bank of England intervened on 28 September 2022 to stabilise the gilt market. The crisis highlighted the importance of <b>liquidity buffers</b> and stress testing in leveraged LDI strategies.	Topic15 15.7.5 LDICrisis
What risk measures are used in an <b>LDI context</b>?	Focus on the <b>plan surplus</b> (assets minus liabilities) and its <b>volatility</b>. Risk = <b>surplus tracking error</b> (shortfall between actual and target surplus). Duration plays a major role; a more sophisticated approach assesses the probability of the surplus becoming negative (a deficit) — analogous to VaR applied to the surplus.	Topic15 15.7.7 LDIRisk
What is <b>ESG investing</b> and how does it differ from <b>SRI</b>?	<b>ESG</b> = incorporating <b>environmental, social, and governance</b> factors into investment analysis to assess long-term risk/return. ESG is generally <b>ethics-agnostic</b> — concerned with sustainability and financial materiality rather than morals.<br><b>SRI (Socially Responsible Investment)</b> = applying ethical criteria as a <b>hurdle</b> for investment, often involving <b>negative screening</b> (excluding weapons, tobacco etc.). SRI introduces an ethical dimension; ESG integrates ESG data into mainstream financial analysis.<br>Two key ESG activities: (1) <b>Screening</b> (include/exclude based on ethical criteria); (2) <b>Shareholder advocacy & engagement</b> (encourage better corporate responsibility via proxy voting). Both are often used together.	Topic15 15.8.1 ESG
What ESG factors are considered?	<b>Environmental</b>: climate change, hazardous waste, nuclear energy, sustainability.<br><b>Social</b>: diversity, human rights, animal welfare, consumer protection, capital and labour.<br><b>Governance</b>: management structure, executive compensation, board independence, remuneration, decision-making quality.	Topic15 15.8.1 ESG
Describe the <b>Stewardship Code</b> and its key principles for asset owners/managers.	Published by the <b>Financial Reporting Council (FRC)</b>. The 2020 Code defines stewardship as responsible allocation, management, and oversight of capital for long-term sustainable value. Revised Code published 2025, effective 1 January 2026.<br>Key principles for asset owners/managers include: (1) integrate stewardship to deliver long-term value; (2) identify and respond to market-wide/systemic risks; (3) engage to maintain/enhance asset value; (4) exercise rights and responsibilities; (5) integrate stewardship into selection of external managers; (6) monitor and hold service providers to account.<br>Signatories should consider: UK Corporate Governance Code, directors' duties (s.172 Companies Act), capital structure, diversity, audit quality, environmental/social issues, covenant compliance.	Topic15 15.8.2 Stewardship
What is the <b>PRI</b> and what are its six principles?	<b>Principles for Responsible Investment</b> — an independent organisation (~2,370 signatories) supported by UNEP FI and UN Global Compact. Launched April 2006 at NYSE.<br>Six voluntary principles: (1) Incorporate ESG issues into investment analysis and decision-making; (2) Incorporate ESG into ownership policies and practices; (3) Seek appropriate disclosure on ESG from investee entities; (4) Promote acceptance of these principles in the industry; (5) Work together to enhance implementation effectiveness; (6) Report on activities and progress.	Topic15 15.8.2 PRI
Why might investors <b>include or exclude</b> ESG factors in investment decisions?	<b>Demand-side reasons to include</b>: raised awareness of climate change, social concerns, more data availability, empirical evidence that ESG does not hurt (and may help) returns, easy access to ESG vehicles.<br><b>Supply-side reasons</b>: increased disclosure requirements, accepted international standards, reputational risk management, financial risk mitigation, PRI advocacy.<br><b>Reasons to exclude/resist</b>: ESG data is harder and more costly to obtain than financial data; disclosure lags reality; different regulatory contexts make comparisons difficult; traditional valuation tools have shorter timeframes than ESG issues require.	Topic15 15.8.3 ESGSupplyDemand
Describe the evidence on whether <b>ESG investing leads to superior returns</b>.	Over 2,000 studies since the 1970s. ~90% of studies find a <b>non-negative</b> relationship between ESG and financial performance, with the majority reporting <b>stable, positive</b> findings. Governance factors are the strongest driver — poorly governed companies tend to underperform. Environmental/social factors are more industry-specific (e.g. environmental is critical for mining, less so for tech). Good governance does not guarantee outperformance, but poor governance is associated with underperformance — this holds across international regions.	Topic15 15.8.4 ESGReturns
What are the main methods and challenges of <b>incorporating ESG into investment decisions</b>?	<b>Method</b>: <b>Integrated analysis</b> — use ESG information in estimating a company's fundamental value alongside traditional financials (revenue, cost of capital, DCF etc.). ESG factors can be treated like any other input to valuation models. Also consider intangibles: labour force, supply chain, stakeholder relations, governance.<br><b>Challenges</b>: (1) ESG data is resource-intensive and hard to get consistently/comparably; (2) company disclosure often lags reality; (3) different regulatory regimes mean raw data needs context; (4) traditional valuation timeframes are shorter than ESG impact timeframes.	Topic15 15.8.5 15.8.6 ESGIntegration
What is <b>impact investing</b> and how does it differ from ESG?	<b>Impact investing</b> = intentional investment in a company specifically to have a <b>positive environmental/social impact</b> alongside financial return. Defined by the OECD as finance addressing social needs with expectation of financial <i>and</i> social return.<br>Key characteristics (Global Impact Investing Network): (1) <b>intentionality</b>; (2) financial <b>return expectations</b> (at worst, return of capital); (3) <b>impact measurement</b> and reporting.<br><b>Difference from ESG</b>: ESG considers E/S/G factors in valuation broadly; impact investing is specifically targeting measurable positive outcomes. Examples: affordable housing, healthcare, clean energy, sustainable agriculture.	Topic15 15.8.7 ImpactInvesting
