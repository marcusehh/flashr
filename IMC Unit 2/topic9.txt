Six applications of macro-economic theory (9.1.1)	(1) Stabilisation policy from understanding employment/growth/inflation/cycle; (2) growth, productivity & poverty policy (min wage, tax & spend); (3) international: exchange rates, balance of payments, transmission of shocks, tariffs; (4) firms anticipating policy & forecasting investment; (5) consumers planning finances & jobs; (6) government budgets, taxes, expenditure, policy.
Financial crisis: definition & causes (9.1.2)	Definition: sharp fall in value of financial/real assets; debtors can't repay; institutions face liquidity shortages as depositors flee (e.g. 2023 Silicon Valley Bank). Causes: over-valuation (PE 2× historical avg, USA 2000; house-price/income peaks, 2008) + a trigger → selling. Behavioural factors: risk incentives (bonuses), lax regulation, herding, exogenous shocks (COVID March 2020) — worse when debt is high.
Financial amnesia & failure of market discipline (9.1.2)	Amnesia: participants behave as if they've forgotten market history; after stable growth, asset prices divorce from fundamentals (very low dividend/earnings yields; house prices vs rental yields). Discipline fails via poor corporate governance + self-regulation → under-pricing of risk; drivers: management incentives, moral hazard, behavioural biases (cognitive dissonance, herding/groupthink, overconfidence, disposition effect), regulatory failure.
Guarding against amnesia/bubbles (9.1.2)	Education on market history; research measures of "excess"; better corporate governance; independent informed regulators (systemic risk, without blocking innovation); central bank independence + limits to curb excess debt; constraints on destabilising government deficits (avoid sovereign debt crises).
Long-term socio-economic trends (9.2.1)	Ageing population (health/pension demands, govt expenditure/revenue strain); labour supply & immigration; rising public expenditure (UK debt/GDP ~80% in 2018–19 → ~96% by March 2025); climate change & sustainable development (UN: low-carbon high-growth transformation; protect vulnerable communities).
Leading, lagging & coincident indicators (9.2.2)	Leading: move ahead of economy, aid prediction (stock market, surveys, money supply, credit growth; US examples: weekly hours, jobless claims, manufacturing/capital-goods orders, housing permits, yield-curve shifts, consumer sentiment, personal income). Lagging: change after economy (employment lags output 3–4 quarters). Coincident: move in step (industrial production, GDP); identify peaks/troughs after the event.
Relative importance of world economies (9.2.3)	USA ~26% global GDP (2024), ~8% exports, 4.5% population. EU ex-UK ~15%. Eurozone: Germany 4.2%, France 2.9%, Italy 2.1%. UK ~3.2% GDP, 2.1% exports, <1% population. Japan ~3%. Top 10 ≈ 66%, top 20 ≈ 79%. China 17%, India 3.6%. Emerging/developing ≈ 60% (up from 46% in 2009).
Business & economic cycles (9.2.4)	Economy-wide fluctuations in real GDP around trend; key feature = common fluctuations across a large area. Lengths: 3–5yr business cycle up to ~60yr Kondratiev wave. UK: ~2.5% GNP growth, ~5yr peak-to-peak; "output gap" = loss vs potential. Four phases: (1) expansion (low inflation/rates); (2) euphoric (overconfidence → falling stocks, rising rates, bankruptcies); (3) recession (output/inventories cut); (4) recovery (confidence, demand & output rise).
GDP vs GNP vs national income; real vs nominal (9.2.5 / 9.2.6)	GDP = output by factors located within the economy. GNP = GDP + net income from abroad. National income (net national product) = GNP at factor cost − capital depreciation. GDP at market prices includes indirect taxes; GDP at factor cost deducts them & adds subsidies. Nominal = current prices; real = inflation-adjusted.
Measuring activity; value added; final vs intermediate goods (9.2.5)	Three measures (identical results): expenditure by firms on inputs; output by firms; purchases by consumers = national income accounting. Avoid double-counting by summing value added (output − bought-in goods): car firm buys £100m steel, sells £200m cars → £100m value added. Final goods = bought by ultimate user (incl. capital goods); intermediate goods = inputs to further production.
National income identities & ≡ (9.2.7)	≡ = "identically equal to" (true by definition). Y ≡ C + S. With only consumption & investment goods, Y ≡ C + I, so I ≡ S (equality via stocks/inventories as investment; refers to actual not desired values). Closed with govt: GDP(market) ≡ C + I + G; at factor cost Y ≡ C + I + G − t. Open: GDP ≡ C + I + G + X − M. YD = Y + T − tᵧY.
Injections, leakages & sectoral balance (9.2.7)	Leakages (withdrawals): S, M, taxes tᵧ and t. Injections: I, X, G. Sectoral identity (holds always): (S − I) ≡ (G + T − t − tᵧ) + (X − M) — private S-over-I surplus ≡ govt deficit + foreign trade surplus. If govt runs deficit & country imports > exports, then I > S.
Consumption function, MPC/MPS/APC (9.2.8)	C = a + cYD. a = autonomous consumption (min when YD=0). c = MPC = proportion of extra income consumed (c=0.9 → £100 income raises C by £90). MPS = 1 − c. APC = total consumption/total income (Keynes). With govt: YD = Y(1 − t¹), so C = a + c(1 − t¹)Y — higher net tax flattens slope, cuts equilibrium output.
AD, equilibrium output & the multiplier (9.2.8)	AD = planned C + I (+ G + X − M); investment autonomous. Equilibrium (point F) = where AD cuts the 45° line (planned spending = output; planned I = planned S); may be below full employment. Adjustment via unplanned stocks. Multiplier = 1/(1 − c) = 1/MPS = ratio of change in equilibrium output to a shift in autonomous spending. c=0.9 → multiplier 10 (£100 I rise → £1,000 output). Open economy: 1/(1 − (c − e)).
Paradox of thrift (9.2.8)	If all try to save more, AD and income fall, so total savings may fall. Investment is autonomous (fixed), so reducing saving raises income; individual thrift is good but collective thrift can harm the whole economy.
Schools of thought overview (9.3.1)	Classical & Monetarist: prices/wages fully flexible. Keynesian: prices/wages fixed/slow. Austrian: methodological individualism — phenomena derive from individuals' knowledge, expectations, perceived consequences.
Classical economics & Say's law (9.3.1)	Late-18thC UK (Smith, Ricardo, Mill). Economy self-regulating, always reaching natural (full-employment) GDP via flexible rates/wages/prices. Requires Say's law: output generates the income to buy it (supply creates its own demand).
Monetarist & rational expectations (9.3.1)	Monetarism (late 1940s, Friedman): a modern Classical school; via Fisher's equation of exchange, inflation is solely from money supply (not AD); crowding out hobbles fiscal policy → focus on monetary policy; markets self-stabilise. Rational expectations / new Classical (1970s): agents use all information, markets competitive, prices adjust to AD; agents offset anticipated policy.
Keynesian economics (9.3.1)	Modern macro from Keynes, "General Theory" (1936), prompted by 1930s mass unemployment (inconsistent with Classical clearing). Structural rigidities let AD fall further; falling wages don't boost hiring when demand is weak. Advocated counter-cyclical fiscal policy (deficit spending) — contrary to then-orthodoxy.
Austrian economics & business cycle (9.3.1)	Late-19thC Vienna (Mises, Hayek). Subjective theory of value (vs Classical objective costs); contributions: subjective value, marginalism, Austrian business cycle theory, entrepreneur's dynamic role. Uses deduction, rejects empirical methods. Cycle: central banks setting rates below free-market level → over-investment & under-saving → bubble that bursts; intervention perpetuates capital misallocation.
Fiscal policy, balanced budget & full-employment surplus (9.3.2)	Fiscal policy = govt spending & taxation; budget surplus = revenue − spending. Balanced budget change = equal rise in G and net tax tᵧ; balanced-budget multiplier still raises output (G shifts AD fully up; equal tax rise cuts AD less, as some would've been saved). Full-employment budget surplus = surplus at current G/t¹ if Y at full employment — measures fiscal stance. UK deficit = PSNCR.
Automatic stabilisers & fiscal policy problems (9.3.2 / 9.3.3)	Stabilisers: when autonomous spending rises, tax revenue & surplus rise automatically, damping AD (progressive tax = "fiscal drag"); benefits sustain AD in recession — no specific policy needed (vs discretionary). Problems: (1) needs accurate read of cycle position (wrong → just inflation); (2) time-lags make changes mistimed/unnecessary; (3) crowding out — borrowing raises rates, crowds out private investment (disputed).
Money supply definitions, narrow → broad (9.3.4)	Notes/coins (narrowest). M0: notes/coins + banks' operational deposits + reserves (discontinued 2006). M1: + non-interest sight deposits. M2 (retail M4): + all retail deposits. M3: + all sight/time deposits with banks (excl. building societies). M4: + deposits, CDs, repos, securities <5yr. M4ex: M4 excl. international offshore deposits (headline broad measure since 2007).
Fractional reserve banking & money multiplier (9.3.5)	Fractional reserve: only a small fraction of liabilities held as cash; banks create money by expanding loans/deposits without first taking deposits (since 1971 Competition & Credit Control). Money multiplier = change in M1 per £1 change in monetary base; Money stock = multiplier × monetary base. Depends on cash/deposit ratios (lower ratios → larger multiplier). £1m base → £10m money = multiplier 10; 5% reserve ratio → deposits 20× reserves.
Quantity theory of money: MV = PT (9.3.5)	M = money, V = velocity, P = price level, T = transactions. Assumptions: V & T fixed wrt money supply; money supply exogenous; causation MV → PT. Therefore a money-supply increase → exactly proportionate price increase (only price inflation).
Money demand & monetary control (9.3.6)	Three motives to hold money: transactions, precautionary, portfolio/asset. As income rises balances rise; as rates rise balances fall (opportunity cost). Money demand (LL schedule) slopes down vs interest rate. A central bank cannot control both money supply and interest rates; to set r₁ it must accept M₁. Financial innovation shifts LL, making targeting hard.
Monetary transmission mechanism & crowding out (9.3.6)	Lower rates raise consumption (consumer credit for durables; wealth effect from higher asset/house prices) and firms' investment (more viable projects) → higher AD, income, output. Higher income raises money demand → upward pressure on rates → partial reversal. Crowding out: higher G raises rates (if money supply fixed) → lower I and C, partly/wholly offsetting G (extreme = full offset; most disagree).
Aggregate supply & the labour market (9.3 AS)	AS = output firms supply at each price level P. Firms hire while marginal product of labour > real wage (money wage/avg price); labour supply rises with real wages. Equilibrium at the natural rate of unemployment (unemployment when labour market clears). Real balance effect: changing real money balances (as P changes) alters consumption until labour-market equilibrium restored.
Classical vs Keynesian AS (9.3 AS)	Classical: no money illusion (respond to real wages/prices) → vertical AS, full wage flexibility, long-run; full-employment output raised only by supply-side policy (work, tax, innovation, education), not AD. Keynesian: "sticky" wages/prices → output changes, prices adjust slowly; short-run adjustment.
Unemployment: UK definition & types (9.3.7)	UK rate = registered available for work ÷ labour force; uses ILO definition (not working but willing/able or actively searching), via Labour Force Survey (ONS) — differs from benefit claimants. Types: frictional (between jobs/new entrants/returners); structural (skills mismatch from changing demand/technology); classical (real wage too high); Keynesian (demand deficiency, involuntary/cyclical).
Natural rate of unemployment (9.3.7)	Unemployment when the labour market clears — completely voluntary, includes frictional + structural. Involuntary = accepting a job at current real wage. Higher replacement ratio (benefits/wages) and union power may raise the natural rate (open to doubt).
Inflation: UK definition & measurement (9.3.8)	Inflation = rise in average prices; control = price stability. Main UK target CPI (since Dec 2003; before, RPI excl. mortgage interest); CPI = geometric mean of expenditure subgroups, weighted by relative expenditure. Since March 2018 focus shifted toward CPIH (includes owner-occupier housing costs). RPI reform: from Feb 2030 RPI uses CPIH methodology, likely ~1% pa lower — hits RPI-linked assets & DB pensions in RPI-linked gilts.
Fisher hypothesis & hyper-inflation (9.3.8)	Fisher hypothesis: inflation rises are matched by interest-rate rises, keeping the real rate (nominal − inflation) constant; high-inflation countries have high rates. Hyper-inflation: extreme inflation (e.g. Brazil, Russia) where velocity becomes very high as holding money gets too costly.
Phillips curve: short- and long-term (9.3.9)	Short-term: inverse trade-off between unemployment and inflation (UK data, early 20thC); 1960s belief AD could pick a point (e.g. 2.5% unemployment, 0% inflation). 1970s: both rose together → trade-off temporary. Long-term Phillips curve is vertical at the natural rate (UN): money growth has no real effect, constant money growth → constant inflation; AD changes only move along the short-term curve.
BoE: inflation target, structure & tools (9.3.10 / 9.3.11)	MPC sets rates to keep CPI within ±1% of 2%; if breached, Governor writes to the Chancellor (BoE autonomy since 1997). Founded 1694; Issue dept (open market operations) & Banking dept (banker to banks/govt). Tools: (1) required reserve ratio (5% → deposits 20× reserves); (2) open market operations (buy/sell securities → change monetary base); (3) repo rate.
Major central bank mandates (9.3.11)	ECB: eurozone inflation below but close to 2%, support EU policy, FX ops, payments; one rate for all members. US Fed: open market ops, discount rate, reserve requirements for price stability & growth; does banking supervision; NOT deposit insurance (FDIC) or govt debt (Treasury). Bank of Japan: sound development via price stability. PBC (China): increasingly globally important.
Unconventional tools: QE, forward guidance, helicopter money (9.3.12)	QE: when rates can't fall further, central bank buys assets (govt/corporate bonds) with electronically created money → raises securities prices (lowers rates), gives institutions cash to lend; "printing money," debatable beyond short term. Forward guidance: commit to a rate level into the future (date or until a variable like unemployment hits a target) → certainty; risk of redundancy if circumstances change. Helicopter money/monetary finance: central bank finances fiscal stimulus via money creation (direct govt transfer; perpetual zero-interest debt — permanence distinguishes from QE; direct payments to public).
Basel framework & capital requirements (9.3.13)	Banks hold minimum capital (shareholders' funds, absorbs losses) vs loans/investments. Basel I (1988, BIS): 8% of risk-adjusted assets — criticised for not discriminating risk, encouraged securitisation. Basel II (2010): risk-differentiated or internal models. Basel III (Jan 2023): micro-prudential (individual bank) + macro-prudential (system-wide); leverage ratio 4.5%, higher ratios for systemic banks (→ Basel IV). UK FPC (Financial Services Act 2012) monitors systemic risk.
Basel III liquidity rules (9.3.13)	Liquidity coverage ratio (from 2015, implemented 2019): hold high-quality liquid assets to survive a 30-calendar-day stress scenario. Net stable funding ratio (from 1 Jan 2018): maintain a stable funding profile. Dynamic provisioning (Spanish): extra reserves in boom-lending to meet recession defaults — macro-prudential smoothing of the cycle.
Debt and the business cycle (9.3.14)	Credit growth (often housing) causes booms; contraction causes depressions. Rising debt/GDP → over-indebtedness → defaults, recession, unemployment (credit cycle theory; Fisher debt deflation). Research: rising household debt/GDP predicts lower output growth & higher unemployment medium-term (many economists dispute).
Exchange rate: definition, FX size, supply & demand (9.4.1)	Exchange rate = price of one currency in another (e.g. $/£); FOREX > US$1,000bn/day, only a few % trade-related. FX market: downward-sloping demand, upward-sloping supply. Fall in €/£ → more £ demanded (UK goods cheaper in € terms). Higher attractiveness of UK goods → demand shifts up → €/£ rises = appreciation of sterling (fall = depreciation).
Fixed, floating, dirty floating; devaluation/revaluation (9.4.2)	Fixed: govt maintains target via FX intervention (using reserves); convertible at the rate. Floating (free): no intervention, market equilibrium. Dirty/managed: some intervention to coax/subdue volatility, no fixed target. Under fixed: devaluation = lower foreign price of domestic currency; revaluation = higher. Higher-inflation countries lose competitiveness; fixed rates aid certainty but real shocks (OPEC oil) threaten them.
Balance of payments components (9.4.3)	Current account: cross-border goods, services, net income from abroad. Capital account: flows of financial assets. Visible trade = goods; invisible trade = services; trade balance = visible + invisible (exports − imports). Current account surplus ≡ equal capital account deficit; overall BoP sums to zero (incl. govt intervention).
Determinants of current & capital accounts; real exchange rate (9.4.4)	Current account depends on UK competitiveness via real exchange rate = (£ price of UK goods / € price of goods) × (€/£). If constant despite price changes, rate offsets them = PPP rate; export demand rises as real rate falls. Capital account: capital flows to high risk-adjusted returns; tiny return changes → massive flows = perfect capital mobility. Total return = asset return ± foreign-currency capital gain/loss.
Policy under fixed vs floating rates; internal/external balance (9.4.5 / 9.4.6 / 9.4.8)	Internal balance = AD at full employment; external balance = current account zero. Fixed: BoP & money supply linked (surplus raises money supply; deficit cuts) unless sterilised; with perfect capital mobility monetary policy is ineffective (rates return to world level), fiscal policy more potent (inflows thwart crowding out). Floating: monetary policy useful short-term (rates shift, currency overshoots, competitiveness changes until wages/prices adjust); fiscal policy weak (expansion raises rates → inflows → currency up → exports less competitive, offsetting). Persistent imbalances: ongoing surplus/deficit = accumulating assets or external debt (e.g. USA's persistent deficits; dollar demand lowered US rates, encouraged leverage).
Purchasing power parity (PPP) & formula (9.4.7 / 9.4.12)	PPP (law of one price): nominal rates move to offset differential inflation, keeping the real rate/competitiveness constant → full internal & external balance. Higher-inflation country faces depreciating nominal rate; short-term deviations from rate/return differences. Formula: E₁/E₀ = (1 + iₓ)/(1 + iᵧ) — E = rate of X per Y at times 0,1; i = inflation rates.
PPP worked example (9.4.12)	£/US$ = 1.60, UK inflation 5%, US 2%: £/US$ = (1.02/1.05) × 1.60 = 1.55. Lower US inflation → US dollar expected to appreciate vs the pound.
Optimal currency area (OCA) & union conditions (9.4.9)	OCA = region maximising efficiency via a single currency (integrate capital markets, ease trade). Benefits: common currency area, lower transaction costs, scale economies. Cost: loss of independent fiscal/monetary policy. Success conditions: (1) full labour mobility (language/pension differences are barriers); (2) free capital movement; (3) if not mobile, automatic fiscal transfers to weaker regions; (4) synchronised business cycles so one rate suits all. Eurozone struggles; USA a better model.
Exchange rate (currency) risk & political risk (9.4.11)	Currency risk = uncertainty in foreign-investment return from the exchange rate at which domestic↔foreign currency converts; can boost or wipe out returns. Also: political risk (hostile regimes, confiscation/nationalisation), market-access limits, transaction costs, double tax (UK investor in US equity taxed on dividends in US and UK).
Currency risk worked example (9.4.11)	Buy US$100 bond at US$2=£1 (cost £50). Sterling falls to US$1.95: value = £1 ÷ 1.95 × 100 = £51.28 → gain (51.28−50)/50 = +2.56%. Sterling rises to US$2.05: value = £48.78 → loss = −2.44%. A US investor sees no dollar gain/loss.
FOREX, spot & forward markets (9.4.10)	FOREX: no physical presence; London ~300 participants (banks/brokers via computers); continuous dealers' market. Customers: corporations (input currency), financial institutions (foreign assets), individuals (holidays). Spot: immediate delivery, settled T+2; bid = lower (dealer buys sterling), ask = higher (dealer sells); difference = spread (profit). Forward: agreement to buy at a future date at a price fixed now — premium if forward > spot, discount if spot > forward. Forward price = spot − premium, or spot + discount.
Spot & forward worked examples (9.4.10)	Spot quote US$1.5244−1.5284, sell £500,000 (buy $): dealer wins → use lower 1.5244 → £500,000 × 1.5244 = US$762,200. Forward: spot 1.5020−1.5030, 3-month premium 1.2¢ & 1.0¢ (subtract premium): bid 1.5020−0.0120 = 1.4900; ask 1.5030−0.0100 = 1.4930.
International Fisher effect (IFE) & worked example (9.4.13)	Fisher: (1+R)=(1+r)(1+E(i)) [R nominal, r real, E(i) expected inflation]. IFE: real rate equal across currencies (arbitrage) → (1+R£)/(1+E(i£)) = (1+R$)/(1+E(i$)). Example (IFE holds): UK inflation 5%, US 2%, US rate 5% → (1+R£)/1.05 = 1.05/1.02 → 1+R£ = 1.05 × 1.0294 = 1.081 → R£ = 8.1%.
Covered & uncovered interest rate parity + worked examples (9.4.13)	Interest rate parity links a currency's forward premium to interest rates (via arbitrage). Covered: F/E = (1+Rₓ)/(1+Rᵧ) (forward vs interest differential). Uncovered: replace forward premium with expected spot change. Ex1: spot US$1.52, UK 8%, US 11% → F = 1.52 × (1.11/1.08) = US$1.5622 (pound at premium). Ex2: 1-yr forward £/US$ = 1.63, UK 6%, US 4% → spot E = 1.63 × (1.06/1.04) = US$1.66. Hedging: deposit £ at 10% & sell sterling forward = deposit $ at 6%; arbitrage sets F = $1,060,000 ÷ £550,000 = US$1.9272 (pound at discount).
