What are the six main applications of macro-economic theory? (9.1.1)	(1) Understanding employment/growth/inflation/business cycle to design stabilisation policy; (2) designing policies to encourage growth, boost productivity, reduce poverty (e.g. minimum wage, tax & spending implications); (3) the international aspect — exchange rates, balance of payments, transmission of shocks/flows between countries (e.g. tariffs); (4) businesses anticipating impact of government policy and forecasting investment needs; (5) consumers planning personal finances (cost of borrowing) and anticipating job opportunities; (6) governments planning budgets, raising taxes, quantifying expenditure and making policy decisions.
What is a financial crisis? (9.1.2)	A sharp fall in the value of financial and real (business) assets, where consumers and businesses cannot repay debts, leading financial institutions into liquidity shortages as depositors run away from banks. May be restricted to one institution or spread to related institutions/geographies. Example: 2023 US banks (Silicon Valley Bank).
What are the typical causes of financial crises? (9.1.2)	Assets or institutions become over-valued — e.g. trailing PE ratio twice the historical average (USA 2000), or house price to disposable income surpassing all previous values (2008). A sudden trigger (bad macro news) prompts selling. Behavioural/economic factors: incentives to take too much risk (bonus schemes); lax regulation encouraging excessive borrowing; herd-like behaviour; non-financial exogenous shocks (e.g. COVID-19 March 2020 crash), especially when debt is high.
What is "financial amnesia"? (9.1.2)	The phenomenon where financial market participants (individuals, institutions, regulators) behave as if they have forgotten the lessons of financial market history. After a prolonged period of stable growth, asset prices become divorced from fundamental drivers of value — e.g. equity prices so high that dividend/earning yields are very low, or house prices diverging from sensible rental yields.
Why does "failure to maintain market discipline" occur? (9.1.2)	A failure of corporate governance combined with over-reliance on firms to self-impose discipline leads to systematic under-pricing of risk. Drivers: incentive structures for senior management encouraging unsustainable activity; moral hazards (wider economy bears the cost of risk-taking); behavioural finance biases — cognitive dissonance, herding/groupthink, illusion of control/overconfidence, the disposition effect; plus regulatory failure.
How can individuals guard against financial amnesia and bubbles? (9.1.2)	Education on the history of financial markets; research into measures of "excess" to flag problems early; improved corporate governance; independent, well-informed regulators focused on systemic risk without inhibiting innovation; central bank independence and limits on activities to prevent excessive debt build-up; and constraints on destabilising government activities (e.g. sustained budget deficits leading to sovereign debt crises).
What are the main long-term UK and global socio-economic trends? (9.2.1)	An ageing population (extra demands on health service and pensions, implications for government expenditure/revenue); labour supply and immigration as key political influences; consistently growing public expenditure (UK public debt-to-GDP rose from ~80% in 2018–19 to ~96% by March 2025); and climate change / sustainable development as a global issue (UN favours a low-carbon, high-growth transformation; managing economic security for vulnerable communities).
What are leading, lagging and coincident indicators? (9.2.2)	Leading: signal movement in advance of the wider economy, useful for prediction (e.g. stock market trends, consumer/business surveys, money supply, credit growth). Lagging: change after the overall economy (e.g. employment may move 3–4 quarters after output). Coincident: move in step with the economy (e.g. industrial production, GDP) — useful for identifying peaks/troughs after the event, subject to revisions.
Give examples of US leading economic indicators. (9.2.2)	Average weekly hours of production workers; initial claims for unemployment insurance; new orders in manufacturing and non-defence capital goods; new private housing permits; yield curve slope shifts; money supply; consumer sentiment; and personal income. The stock market price index is itself a leading indicator.
Describe the relative importance of the main world economies. (9.2.3)	USA: ~26% of global GDP (2024), ~8% of world exports, 4.5% of population. EU ex-UK: ~15% GDP. Within eurozone — Germany 4.2%, France 2.9%, Italy 2.1%. UK: ~3.2% GDP, 2.1% of exports, <1% of population. Japan: ~3% GDP. Top 10 economies ≈ 66% of world economy; top 20 ≈ 79%. China 17% and India 3.6% of global GDP. Emerging/developing economies ≈ 60% of global GDP (up from 46% in 2009).
What are the four phases of the business cycle? (9.2.4)	(1) Expansionary phase — production rises, inflation and interest rates low; (2) euphoric phase — overconfidence, giving way to falling stock prices and rising interest rates and bankruptcies; (3) recession — output and inventories cut back; (4) recovery — consumers regain confidence, demand and output rise.
What are economic cycles, and how long do they last? (9.2.4)	Economy-wide fluctuations in economic activity (output/real GDP around a trend), with the key feature being common fluctuations across a large area. Cycles range from a 3–5 year business cycle to the almost 60-year Kondratiev wave. In the UK, activity fluctuates around a GNP growth rate of ~2.5%, with a full peak-to-peak cycle averaging ~5 years. The "output gap" is the loss of output from short-term fluctuations relative to potential.
Distinguish GDP, GNP and national income. (9.2.5)	GDP = value of output produced by factors of production located within the domestic economy. GNP = GDP plus net income from abroad (allows for domestic ownership of factors located abroad and foreign ownership of domestic factors). National income (net national product) = GNP at factor cost less capital depreciation (depreciation of buildings/machinery used in producing that output).
Distinguish real and nominal GDP. (9.2.6)	Nominal GDP is measured at current prices. Real GDP is adjusted for changes in price levels over time, i.e. removing the effect of inflation. Distinguish also GDP at market prices (includes indirect taxes) from GDP at factor cost (deducts indirect taxes, adds back subsidies).
What are the three ways of measuring economic activity? (9.2.5 / circular flow)	In the circular flow of income: (1) the value of expenditure by firms on inputs; (2) the value of output by firms; (3) the value of purchases by consumers. In a simplified model all three yield identical results. This is known as national income accounting.
How is double-counting avoided when measuring economic activity? (9.2.5)	By totalling the value added by each producer — the value of output over the cost of goods bought in (raw materials and intermediate goods). Example: a car producer buys £100m of steel and sells cars worth £200m → £100m value added. If the steelmaker bought £20m of iron ore, it added £80m. This makes the measure independent of the structure of production.
Define final goods and intermediate goods. (9.2.5)	Final goods are purchased by the ultimate user (a consumer, or a firm using capital equipment). Intermediate goods are inputs into another production process. The distinction matters to avoid double-counting aggregate economic activity.
What is the basic identity Y ≡ C + S, and what does ≡ mean? (9.2.7)	Y is GDP (household income), C is consumption (household spending), S is household saving. The symbol ≡ means "is identically equal to" — true by definition. Households can spend or save, so income equals consumption plus saving.
Why does S ≡ I (savings identically equal investment)? (9.2.7)	With only consumption and investment goods, Y ≡ C + I; since Y ≡ C + S, then I ≡ S. Equality holds by defining changes in stocks/inventories as part of investment I. If households don't spend all income, unsold output accumulates as unplanned stocks (investment). This refers to actual values over a period — not desired/planned values.
What is the closed-economy government identity for GDP? (9.2.7)	GDP at market prices ≡ C + I + G (the three types of final expenditure). G = government expenditure on goods/services (gives rise to factor earnings, so included). T = transfer payments (e.g. unemployment benefits — no economic activity, excluded). GDP at factor cost: Y ≡ C + I + G − t, where t = indirect tax net of subsidies. Personal disposable income YD = Y + T − tᵧY.
What is the full open-economy GDP identity? (9.2.7)	GDP ≡ C + I + G + X − M. Exports (X) add to factor incomes at home; imports (M) do not give rise to domestic factor incomes so are deducted. Y at factor cost requires further deduction of t (indirect tax net of subsidies).
What are injections and leakages in the circular flow? (9.2.7)	Leakages (withdrawals): savings (S), imports (M), taxes on income (tᵧ) and on expenditure (t). Injections: investment (I), exports (X) and government spending (G). Sectors: personal (C, S, T, Y); government (G, t, tᵧ); international (M, X).
State the sectoral balance identity. (9.2.7)	(S − I) ≡ (G + T − t − tᵧ) + (X − M): private sector surplus of savings over investment ≡ government deficit expenditure over income + foreign sector surplus of exports over imports. Holds at all times — a useful way of understanding imbalances between sectors. If government runs a large deficit and the country imports more than it exports, then I > S.
What is the consumption function C = a + cYD? (9.2.8)	a = autonomous consumption (minimum level of consumption when YD = 0). c = the proportion of additions to disposable income spent on consumption — the marginal propensity to consume (MPC). YD = disposable income. E.g. if c = 0.9, a £100 increase in disposable income raises consumption by £90.
Define MPC, MPS, APC. (9.2.8)	MPC (c) = marginal propensity to consume = proportion of additional income spent. MPS = marginal propensity to save = 1 − c = proportion of additional income saved. APC = average propensity to consume (Keynes), total consumption divided by total income.
What is aggregate demand (AD) in the simple model? (9.2.8)	AD = total planned expenditure by households and firms at each level of income = C + I. With consumption rising linearly with income and investment (I) fixed (autonomous), AD is the vertical sum of C + I. Changes in the slope (c) or autonomous expenditures (a or I) will shift AD.
What is the short-term equilibrium output (point F)? (9.2.8)	The point where the AD line cuts the 45° line — the only point where planned aggregate spending actually equals output produced. It may be below full-employment output. The adjustment mechanism: if AD > output, stocks run down and firms raise output; if AD < output, stocks rise and output is cut. Equilibrium requires planned I = planned S.
What is the multiplier and how is it calculated? (9.2.8)	The ratio of the change in equilibrium output to a parallel shift in AD (a change in autonomous spending, I or a). Multiplier = 1/(1 − c) = 1/MPS. The increase is the sum of a geometric progression: (£100 × 0.9) + (£100 × 0.9²) + … = £1,000, so a £100 increase in I with c = 0.9 gives a £1,000 increase in output, i.e. multiplier = 10.
What is the Paradox of Thrift? (9.2.8)	If everyone tries to save more, AD (and hence income) will fall, which in turn leads to lower savings — so total savings may fall even as people try to save more. Reducing savings raises income (resources not fully employed). With investment autonomous (fixed), individual thrift is generally good but collective thrift may be bad for the economy as a whole.
How is government included in the consumption function? (9.2.7)	Taxes are proportional to income; tᵧ is taxes net of transfers. YD = Y − tᵧ = Y(1 − t¹), where t¹ = proportion of income deducted in tax (net of transfers). So C = a + cYD = a + c(1 − t¹)Y. The slope of the consumption function shifts from c to c(1 − t¹). Higher net taxes reduce the slope and reduce equilibrium output.
How is foreign trade incorporated, and what is the open-economy multiplier? (9.2.7)	X is autonomous (determined by rest of world). Import demand M = d + eY, where e = marginal propensity to import. A higher e reduces equilibrium output (AD less steep). The open-economy multiplier = 1/(1 − (c − e)). MPC of imported goods + MPC of domestically produced goods + MPS = unity.
Summarise the four schools of economic thought. (9.3.1)	Classical & Monetarist: prices and wages are fully flexible. Keynesian: prices and wages are fixed or slow to adjust. Austrian: emphasises the role of individual/group actions (methodological individualism) — all economic phenomena derive from individuals' knowledge, expectations and perceived consequences of their actions.
Describe Classical economics. (9.3.1)	Began in the UK in the late 18th century (Adam Smith, David Ricardo, John Stuart Mill). The real economy is self-regulating, always capable of achieving the natural level of real GDP (full employment) via automatic self-adjustment — flexible interest rates, wages and goods' prices. Requires Say's law: producing output generates the income needed to purchase that output (supply creates its own demand).
Describe Monetarist economics. (9.3.1)	Began in the late 1940s with Milton Friedman. Embraced supply/demand equilibrium for the whole economy. Via Irving Fisher's equation of exchange, regards inflation as solely the result of money supply variations, not AD. Argued "crowding out" hobbles fiscal policy, so the focus should be monetary policy; governments should not stabilise — markets naturally lead to macro-economic stability. A modern form of Classical economics.
What is "rational expectations" / new Classical economics? (9.3.1)	A doctrine from the 1970s asserting: individuals behave rationally, collecting and analysing all relevant information; markets are competitive; and prices adjust to changes in AD. Individuals act on expectations about the future, so may act to offset anticipated government policies. Critics doubt how well-informed people really are and whether prices/wages are flexible downwards.
Describe Keynesian economics. (9.3.1)	Modern macro began with Keynes's "The General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money" (1936), prompted by mass unemployment of the 1930s Depression — inconsistent with Classical theory. Structural rigidities mean AD can fall further; falling wages don't lead to more hiring when demand is weak. Keynes advocated counter-cyclical fiscal policy (deficit spending), contrary to the then-orthodoxy of cutting welfare and raising taxes.
Describe Austrian economics and its key contributions. (9.3.1)	Originated in late-19th-century Vienna (Ludwig von Mises, Friedrich Hayek). Emphasises the subjective role of individual preferences in determining prices (vs Classical focus on objective production costs). Key contributions: the subjective theory of value; marginalism in pricing; the Austrian business cycle theory; and the dynamic role of the entrepreneur. Uses deduction over induction and rejects empirical methods, seeing itself as a philosophy of human action.
What is the Austrian business cycle theory? (9.3.1)	Central banks fixing interest rates below the free-market level leads to bad investments and excess consumption — entrepreneurs over-invest, consumers under-save — creating a bubble that bursts when it becomes clear there are insufficient resources to maintain consumption and investment simultaneously. Central banks are seen as the main cause of financial instability; government intervention only perpetuates capital misallocation.
What is fiscal policy and a balanced budget change? (9.3.2)	Fiscal policy = government decisions about spending and taxation. The budget surplus = difference between government revenue and spending. A balanced budget change = an increase in G accompanied by an equal increase in net tax revenue tᵧ. The balanced-budget multiplier still raises output: higher G shifts AD up fully, while the equal tax rise causes a smaller fall in AD (some of that income would have been saved).
What is the full-employment budget surplus and the PSNCR? (9.3.2)	Because tax revenue varies with the cycle, the actual budget surplus is not a reliable indicator of fiscal stance. The full-employment budget surplus = the surplus/deficit that would occur at current G and t¹ if Y were at full employment — a standard for measuring fiscal stance. The UK budget deficit is also known as the public sector net cash requirement (PSNCR).
How does fiscal policy act as an automatic stabiliser? (9.3.2 / 9.3.3)	If autonomous spending (a or I) rises suddenly, net tax revenue and the budget surplus rise automatically, reducing the growth in AD; the reverse holds for falls. Known as "fiscal drag" when progressive taxes slow rising incomes. In a recession, social security benefits kick in to sustain AD. This stabilisation happens without specific policy changes (vs discretionary policy).
What are the problems associated with fiscal policy? (9.3.3)	(1) Requires accurate understanding of where the economy is in the cycle — getting it wrong (e.g. boosting when no recession is imminent) may only raise inflation. (2) Time-lags: fiscal policy takes time to implement and have impact; circumstances may have changed, making changes unnecessary. (3) Crowding out: fiscal expansion funded by government borrowing may raise interest rates and crowd out private investment (disputed by many economists).
List the UK money supply definitions from narrow to broad. (9.3.4)	Notes & coins (narrowest). M0: notes/coins in circulation + banks' operational deposits + central bank reserves (discontinued 2006, reconstructable). M1: notes/coins + non-interest-bearing sight deposits. M2 (retail M4): notes/coins + all retail deposits. M3: notes/coins + all sight and time deposits with banks (excl. building societies). M4: notes/coins, deposits, CDs, repos, securities <5yr maturity. M4ex: M4 excluding international offshore deposits (current headline broad aggregate since 2007).
What is fractional reserve banking? (9.3.5)	A system where only a small fraction of bank liabilities is held as cash assets; other assets are held in liquid form realisable at short notice (bills, market loans). Banks create money by expanding loans (and deposits) without first accepting deposits — allowing the quantity of broad money to expand through commercial bank lending. A key feature since the 1971 Competition and Credit Control reforms.
What is the money multiplier? (9.3.5)	The change in M1 following a £1 change in the monetary base. Money stock = money multiplier × monetary base. It depends on the proportion of bank cash reserves to total deposits, and the private sector's desired ratio of cash to deposits. Lower ratios → larger multiplier. E.g. a £1m base change leading to £10m money stock change → multiplier = 10. If reserve ratio is 5%, banks create deposits 20× their cash reserves.
State the quantity theory of money (MV = PT). (9.3.5)	M = money, V = velocity of circulation, P = price level, T = level of transactions. Three assumptions: (1) V and T are fixed with respect to money supply; (2) money supply is exogenous; (3) causation runs left (MV) to right (PT). Therefore an increase in money supply leads to an exactly proportionate increase in the price level — money supply expansions only cause price inflation.
What are the three motives for holding money? (9.3.6)	(1) Transactions motive; (2) precautionary motive (meeting unforeseen events); (3) portfolio/asset motive (money as a low-risk, low-return financial asset). As income rises, individuals hold larger balances; as interest rates rise, they reduce balances (opportunity cost of holding money rises).
Explain the demand for money and monetary control. (9.3.6)	The demand for money slopes downward against the interest rate (LL schedule): at lower interest rates, demand for money is higher. A central bank cannot control both the money supply and interest rates simultaneously. If it wants interest rate r₁, money supply must be M₁. Financial innovation shifts the LL schedule, making it hard to confidently select M₁ for a target r₁.
Explain the monetary transmission mechanism. (9.3.6)	A fall in interest rates raises both consumer spending (via bank lending/consumer credit for durables, and the wealth effect from higher asset/house prices) and firms' investment (lower cost of financing projects → more viable projects). Combining the extended consumption and investment schedules, lower rates raise AD, equilibrium income and output. The initial rise in income raises money demand, putting upward pressure on rates, causing a partial fall back.
What is "crowding out" of government spending? (9.3.6 / 9.3.3)	An increase in G shifts AD upwards but puts upward pressure on interest rates (if money supply doesn't rise); higher rates reduce investment and consumption, partly offsetting the rise in G. The extreme view is that increases in G are completely offset — though most economists don't fully subscribe to this.
What determines aggregate supply (AS)? (9.3.x AS & inflation)	The AS schedule shows output firms wish to supply at different price levels P. The labour market is key: firms employ workers while the marginal product of labour exceeds the real wage (money wage / average price level). The labour supply rises as real wages rise. Equilibrium occurs at the natural rate of unemployment — the proportion of the labour force unemployed when the labour market is in equilibrium.
Why is the Classical AS curve vertical? (9.3.x)	If firms/workers are not subject to money illusion (they respond to real, not nominal, wages and prices), supply is constant at all price levels — a vertical AS curve. Complete wage flexibility means the labour market is always in equilibrium. Only real changes (technology, capital stock, education, population) shift it. The real balance effect: changing real money balances (as the price level changes) alters consumption until labour market equilibrium is restored.
Contrast the Classical and Keynesian models of AS. (9.3.x)	Classical (vertical AS): wages/prices fully flexible; describes the long run; full-employment output can only be raised by supply-side policies (work attitudes, taxation, innovation, education), not changes in AD. Keynesian: wages/prices are "sticky" / not fully flexible; output changes and prices adjust slowly; describes the short-term adjustment of the economy.
How is unemployment defined and measured in the UK? (9.3.7)	UK unemployment rate = number registered as available for work ÷ total UK labour force. The UK uses the ILO definition: those not working but willing and able to work, or available for and actively searching for work. Numbers are based on the Labour Force Survey (ONS) — different from those claiming unemployment benefits.
Distinguish the types of unemployment. (9.3.7)	Frictional: individuals between jobs or not easily employable (workers who left jobs, returners, new entrants). Structural: persistent unemployment from a mismatch between workforce skills and changing job-market requirements (technology, changing demand). Classical: too high a real wage so the labour market doesn't fully adjust. Keynesian: demand deficiency from lack of wage/price flexibility (involuntary, cyclical).
What is the natural rate of unemployment? (9.3.7)	The rate of unemployment when the labour market is in equilibrium — completely voluntary, including frictional and structural unemployment. Voluntary unemployment includes those unemployed at classical full employment; involuntary unemployment means accepting a job at the current real wage. Increases in the replacement ratio (benefits relative to wages) and trade union power may raise the natural rate (open to doubt).
How is inflation defined and measured in the UK? (9.3.8)	Inflation = the rise in average goods prices over time; its control is "price stability". The main UK target is the consumer prices index (CPI) — since December 2003 (previously RPI, excluding mortgage interest). CPI is the geometric mean of consumers' expenditure subgroups, weighted by relative expenditure. Since March 2018 focus shifted toward CPIH (includes owner-occupiers' housing costs).
What is happening to RPI methodology? (9.3.8)	HM Treasury (Sept 2019 letter from Chancellor Sajid Javid) proposed reforming RPI between 2025–2030. From February 2030, RPI will be calculated using the same methods/data as CPIH, likely lowering RPI by ~1% per annum. This affects RPI-linked assets/liabilities and DB pension schemes invested in RPI-linked gilts (negative funding impact).
State the Fisher hypothesis and define hyper-inflation. (9.3.8)	Fisher hypothesis: an increase in inflation is accompanied by a similar increase in interest rates, keeping the real interest rate (nominal less inflation) constant. So countries with high inflation also have high interest rates. Hyper-inflation = periods of extreme inflation (e.g. Brazil, Russia) where the velocity of circulation becomes very high as it becomes too expensive to hold money.
Explain the short-term Phillips curve. (9.3.9)	An inverse relationship (trade-off) between unemployment and inflation derived from UK data over the first half of the 20th century — higher inflation associated with lower unemployment. In the 1960s, governments thought AD could be manipulated to choose a desired point (e.g. 2.5% unemployment and zero inflation), using monetary/fiscal policy to shift AD.
What is the long-term Phillips curve? (9.3.9)	In the 1970s both higher unemployment AND higher inflation appeared, so the trade-off was found to be temporary, not permanent. Recognising the natural rate of unemployment, the long-term Phillips curve is vertical (UN). Individuals only care about real variables, so any shift in money supply has no real impact: a constant long-term money growth rate translates into a similar constant inflation rate. Changes in AD only move you along the short-term curve.
What is the BoE inflation target and what happens if it is breached? (9.3.10)	The Monetary Policy Committee (MPC) of the BoE sets interest rates to keep inflation within ±1% of the 2% CPI target. If the boundaries are breached, the Governor must write an open, explanatory letter to the Chancellor of the Exchequer. The BoE was given autonomy over interest rate setting in 1997.
Describe the Bank of England structure and tools. (9.3.11)	The BoE has existed since 1694 with two departments. Issue department: exchanges bank notes for government/other securities (open market operations). Banking department: banker to commercial banks and government. Three tools: (1) required reserve ratio (e.g. 5% ratio → deposits 20× cash reserves); (2) open market operations (buying/selling securities to change the monetary base); (3) the repo rate (interest charged for lending to commercial banks).
Distinguish the mandates of major central banks. (9.3.11)	ECB: keep eurozone inflation below but close to 2%, support EU policies, FX operations, smooth payment systems; sets one rate for all euro countries. US Federal Reserve: open market operations, discount rates, reserve requirements for long-term price stability and growth; also does banking supervision; not responsible for deposit insurance (FDIC) or government debt (US Treasury). Bank of Japan: sound development through price stability. PBC (China): increasingly important globally.
Explain quantitative easing (QE). (9.3.12)	When interest rates cannot be reduced further to stimulate the economy, the central bank purchases financial assets (government and corporate bonds) from financial institutions using electronically created money. This raises securities prices (lowers market rates) and gives selling institutions cash reserves to support new lending. Popularly described as "printing money," with historic associations to subsequent high inflation. Debatable whether it lowers rates beyond the short term.
Explain forward guidance. (9.3.12)	When a central bank commits to keeping interest rates at a certain level into the future — often to a specific date or until a key variable (e.g. unemployment) reaches a target. The intention is to give economic agents certainty about the future path of borrowing costs. A difficulty: circumstances change quickly, making guidance redundant and risking loss of credibility.
Explain helicopter money and monetary finance. (9.3.12)	Helicopter money = monetary finance whereby the central bank finances fiscal stimulus through money creation. Monetary finance = central bank finances fiscal expansion by increasing the monetary base ("printing money") to facilitate spending without increasing future debt burden. Mechanisms: direct transfer to the government's account; buying government debt paying no interest/principal or rolled over in perpetuity (permanence distinguishes it from QE); direct payment to the population.
Explain bank capital requirements and the Basel framework. (9.3.13)	Banks hold minimum capital relative to outstanding loans/investments. Basel I (1988, BIS): 8% of risk-adjusted assets — criticised for not discriminating between risky/less-risky activities and encouraging securitisation. Basel II (2010): calibrated capital by risk differentiation or internal models. Capital (shareholders' funds) absorbs losses from lending; capital levels influence profitability and lending volume, and could be a tool of monetary policy.
Explain Basel III and macro-prudential regulation. (9.3.13)	Basel III (developed for January 2023 implementation): comprehensive reforms to improve regulation, supervision and risk management. Two levels: (1) micro-prudential (individual bank resilience); (2) macro-prudential (system-wide issues). Includes a maximum leverage ratio of 4.5% (total assets to capital), enhanced capital ratios for systemically important institutions, and is evolving into Basel IV. The UK's Financial Policy Committee (FPC, created by the Financial Services Act 2012) monitors systemic threats.
Explain liquidity requirements under Basel III. (9.3.13)	Liquidity coverage rules (from 2015, implemented by 2019): a bank must hold adequate high-quality liquid assets convertible to cash to meet a 30-calendar-day liquidity stress scenario. The net stable funding ratio (from 1 January 2018) requires banks to maintain a stable funding profile. "Dynamic provisioning" (Spanish system) = setting aside more reserves during boom-lending to better meet defaults in recessions — a macro-prudential tool to smooth the cycle.
Identify the role of debt in the business cycle. (9.3.14)	Growth in credit (often housing) causes booms; contraction causes depressions. Changes in the debt-to-GDP ratio are associated with credit growth/contraction; if debt rises too much, society becomes over-indebted and cannot repay, causing defaults, recession and unemployment (credit cycle theory — Fisher's debt deflation). Research shows rising household debt-to-GDP predicts lower output growth and higher unemployment over the medium run (disputed by many economists).
What is the foreign exchange rate and the FOREX market size? (9.4.1)	The price of one currency in terms of another (e.g. dollars per pound). Transactions to buy/sell currencies for physical and financial goods take place in the foreign exchange market — a global screen-based market of over US$1,000 billion of transactions per day, only a few percent of which relate to trade in goods and services.
How do supply and demand affect a currency's value? (9.4.1)	The FX market has downward-sloping demand and upward-sloping supply curves. As euros per pound falls, quantity of pounds demanded rises (UK goods look cheaper in euro terms). If UK goods' attractiveness rises, demand shifts to D¹D¹, raising the equilibrium euros per pound — an appreciation of sterling (a fall in foreign value = depreciation).
Distinguish fixed, floating and dirty floating exchange rates. (9.4.2)	Fixed: government maintains a target rate; currency convertible at that rate via FX market intervention (adding to demand/supply using FX reserves). Floating (free): no intervention; price reaches free-market equilibrium. Dirty floating (managed): some intervention to coax the rate or subdue volatility, but with no fixed target in mind.
What are devaluation and revaluation? (9.4.2)	Under a fixed rate, if the government announces a change: devaluation = a reduction in the foreign price of domestic currency; revaluation = an increase. Countries with higher domestic inflation than competitors become less competitive and must use monetary/fiscal policy to realign inflation, or seek a devaluation. Fixed rates aid certainty for traders but real shocks (e.g. OPEC oil prices) can threaten them.
What are the key components of the balance of payments? (9.4.3)	Current account: cross-border flow of goods, services and other net income from abroad. Capital account: flows of transactions in financial assets. Visible trade: trade in goods. Invisible trade: flows of services. The trade balance = visible + invisible trade (exports less imports). A current account surplus must be matched by an equal capital account deficit. The overall balance of payments sums to zero (including government intervention).
What determines the current and capital accounts? (9.4.4)	Current account depends on UK competitiveness — the real exchange rate = (£ price of UK goods / € price of goods) × (€/£). The real exchange rate translates relative prices into a common currency; if constant despite price changes, the rate has adjusted to offset them (purchasing power parity rate). Capital account: capital moves toward high risk-adjusted returns; if small return changes induce massive flows = perfect capital mobility. Total return = asset price return ± foreign currency capital gain/loss.
Explain monetary and fiscal policy under fixed exchange rates. (9.4.5 / 9.4.8)	Under fixed rates, the balance of payments and money supply are linked: a BoP surplus raises money supply (intervention sells domestic currency); a deficit reduces it. This can be offset by sterilisation. With perfectly mobile capital, monetary policy is ineffective — any attempt to lower rates causes a capital-account outflow until money supply and rates return to world levels. Fiscal policy is more potent in the open economy (crowding out is thwarted by capital inflows).
Explain monetary and fiscal policy under floating exchange rates. (9.4.8)	Monetary policy is useful in the short term: changes in money supply become real changes (prices slow to adjust), rates shift, speculators rush in, the currency overshoots — a dramatic short-term change in competitiveness until wages/prices adjust. Fiscal policy is less effective: a fiscal expansion raises AD and rates, attracting fund inflows that bid up the currency, making exports less competitive and offsetting the expansion.
What are internal and external balance? (9.4.5)	Internal balance: AD at the full-employment level. External balance: the current account of the balance of payments being zero (in balance). When both hold, the economy is at long-term equilibrium.
Explain persistent global imbalances. (9.4.6)	When exports and imports are equal, the current account is in balance. A persistent surplus/deficit means a country is accumulating overseas assets or rising external indebtedness. E.g. the USA has imported more than it exported for years → persistent current account deficits, with claims on the USA accumulated by others. Willingness to hold dollar-denominated assets has lowered US interest rates and encouraged leverage — potentially destabilising if it leads to over-borrowing.
Explain purchasing power parity (PPP) as a forecasting tool. (9.4.7)	A theory determining long-term exchange rates under a free float: nominal exchange rates move to offset differential inflation rates between countries, so the real exchange rate (and competitiveness) is constant — giving full internal and external balance. Also known as the law of one price. Short term there may be deviations due to interest rate and expected return differences. Countries with higher domestic inflation face a depreciating nominal exchange rate.
State the PPP formula for exchange rate adjustment. (9.4.12)	E₁/E₀ = (1 + iₓ)/(1 + iᵧ), where E₀ and E₁ are exchange rates of currency X with respect to Y at times 0 and 1, and iₓ, iᵧ are the inflation rates over the period for X and Y. An economy with high (low) inflation relative to others will, on average, experience a depreciation (appreciation).
Worked PPP example: £/US$ is 1.60, UK inflation expected 5%, US 2%. Find the PPP rate. (9.4.12)	£/US$ = (1 + 0.02)/(1 + 0.05) × 1.60 = (1.02/1.05) × 1.60 = 1.55. The lower US inflation means the analyst expects the US dollar to appreciate against the pound over the period.
What is an optimal currency area (OCA)? (9.4.9)	A geographic region created to maximise economic efficiency through a single currency, allowing closer capital-market integration and facilitating trade. Benefits for closely-tied economies: a common currency area; reduced transaction costs for inter-country trade; enhanced scale of production (scale economies from a larger "domestic" market). Downside: loss of each country's ability to direct its own fiscal/monetary policy.
What conditions are essential for a successful currency union? (9.4.9)	(1) Labour should be fully mobile (few barriers — different languages/pensions are barriers); (2) capital should move freely; (3) if labour/capital not fully mobile, a risk-sharing mechanism (automatic fiscal transfer) to redistribute to less well-off regions; (4) countries should have similar/synchronised business cycles so one interest rate suits all. The eurozone struggles (different cycle stages, imperfect mobility); the USA is a better OCA model.
Explain exchange rate (currency) risk. (9.4.11)	The uncertainty in the return on a foreign investment due to uncertainty in the rate at which domestic currency can be exchanged for foreign currency. Movements can increase or eliminate returns. Investing abroad also involves political risk (hostile regimes, asset confiscation/nationalisation), foreign-market access restrictions, differing transaction costs, and tax (e.g. a UK investor in US equity is liable to tax on dividends in both the USA and UK).
Worked exchange risk example: UK investor buys US$100 bond at US$2 = £1; sterling falls to US$1.95. Calculate the gain. (9.4.11)	Cost = £50. Selling US$100 at the new rate: £1 ÷ US$1.95 × US$100 = £51.28. Percentage gain = (51.28 − 50.00)/50.00 × 100 = 2.56%. If instead sterling appreciated to US$2.05 = £1: value = £48.78, a loss of (48.78 − 50.00)/50.00 = −2.44%. A US investor holding the same bond sees no dollar gain/loss from the exchange rate.
Describe the FOREX market and its participants. (9.4.10)	The market for foreign exchange, with no physical presence. The London FOREX market has ~300 participants — major international banks and brokerage houses linked via computers, a continuous dealers' market where market makers are major banks trading on their own behalf; some act only as brokers. Final customers: corporations needing foreign currency for inputs; financial institutions buying foreign assets; private individuals (e.g. for holidays).
Describe the spot market. (9.4.10)	FOREX transactions for immediate delivery, settled within two working days (T+2). Market makers quote two spot prices: the lower rate is the bid price (price they buy sterling at); the higher rate is the ask price (price they sell sterling at). The difference is the market maker's spread (their profit). FT spot prices are the best buy/sell rates quoted toward the end of London trading the previous day.
Describe the forward market. (9.4.10)	A forward transaction is an agreement to buy currency at a future date at a fixed price agreed now. A currency trades at a premium if its forward price is higher than spot; at a discount if its spot price exceeds its forward rate. To calculate the forward price: subtract a forward premium from the current spot rate; add a forward discount to the current spot rate.
Worked spot example: quote US$1.5244 − 1.5284; how many dollars selling £500,000? (9.4.10)	The dealer always wins; the customer gets the worse rate. Selling pounds (buying dollars), the manager gets the lower rate US$1.5244: £500,000 × 1.5244 = US$762,200.
Worked forward example: spot US$1.5020 − 1.5030, 3-month premium 1.2¢ and 1.0¢. Find forward rates. (9.4.10)	A premium is subtracted from spot. Bid: 1.5020 − 0.0120 = 1.4900. Ask: 1.5030 − 0.0100 = 1.4930. So the 3-month forward sterling bid-and-ask prices are US$1.4900 and US$1.4930.
State the international Fisher effect (IFE). (9.4.13)	The Fisher effect: (1 + R) = (1 + r)(1 + E(i)), where R = nominal rate, r = real rate, E(i) = expected inflation. The IFE states there is a real interest rate for each currency, and if investors can invest in any currency without restriction, the real rate will be the same everywhere (arbitrage eliminates differences). So: (1 + R£)/(1 + E(i£)) = (1 + R$)/(1 + E(i$)).
Worked IFE example: IFE holds, UK inflation 5%, US inflation 2%, US 1-yr rate 5%. Find UK 1-yr rate R£. (9.4.13)	(1 + R£)/(1 + 0.05) = (1 + 0.05)/(1 + 0.02). So (1 + R£) = 1.05 × (1.05/1.02) = 1.05 × 1.0294 = 1.081. R£ = 0.081 or 8.1%.
Distinguish covered and uncovered interest rate parity. (9.4.13)	Interest rate parity: the special relationship between a currency's forward premium and interest rates (ensured by money/forward markets and arbitrage). Covered interest parity: the relationship between forward rates and the interest differential — F/E = (1 + Rₓ)/(1 + Rᵧ), where F and E are forward and spot rates. Uncovered interest parity: replace the forward premium with the expected change in the spot exchange rate — the expected change reflects current interest differentials.
Worked covered parity example: spot US$1.52 = £1, UK rate 8%, US rate 11%. Find the 1-yr forward rate. (9.4.13)	F/E = (1 + Rₓ)/(1 + Rᵧ). With pound as X: F/1.52 = (1 + 0.11)/(1 + 0.08) = 1.11/1.08. F = (1.11/1.08) × 1.52 = US$1.5622. The pound is trading at a premium with respect to the US dollar.
Worked interest parity (spot from forward) example: 1-yr forward £/US$ = US$1.63, UK rate 6%, US rate 4%. Find the spot rate. (9.4.13)	(E/F) = (1 + UK rate)/(1 + US rate). Spot E = F × (1 + UK)/(1 + US) = 1.63 × (1.06/1.04) = US$1.66 per £.
How does an investor use the forward market to eliminate exchange risk (covered)? (9.4.13)	Example: US$1m deposited in the US at 6% → US$1,060,000. Alternatively swap to £0.5m at £1 = US$2, deposit at 10% UK → £0.55m, then sell £0.55m forward for dollars at a rate fixed now (no risk). Arbitrage forces both to yield the same: the 1-yr forward rate F = US$1,060,000 ÷ £550,000 = US$1.9272. The pound trades at a discount — extra UK interest is offset by selling sterling forward at a worse rate.
