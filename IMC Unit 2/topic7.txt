What is the difference between primary and secondary data?	<b>Primary data</b> is collected or generated with a specific project/task in mind (e.g. a lab experiment, a commissioned survey).<br><b>Secondary data</b> is gathered and distributed by others (government agencies, banks, international bodies) for general use.	Topic7 7.1.1 SourcesOfData
Distinguish cross-section data from time-series data.	<b>Cross-section</b>: many subjects observed at a <i>single</i> point in time (e.g. GDP of 10 countries in 2021).<br><b>Time-series</b>: one variable observed over <i>successive, usually equally-spaced</i> time intervals (e.g. one country's GDP year by year).	Topic7 7.1.1 SourcesOfData
Define a population, a sample, a parameter and a statistic.	<b>Population</b>: all members of a well-defined set (finite or infinite).<br><b>Sample</b>: a subset of a population.<br><b>Parameter</b>: a number describing the <i>whole population</i> (e.g. population mean).<br><b>Statistic</b>: a number describing a <i>sample</i> (e.g. sample mean).	Topic7 7.1.2 Sampling
Name the three probability sampling methods and the key idea of each.	<b>Random</b>: every member has an equal, known chance of selection (purest form).<br><b>Systematic</b>: select every nth record from a list (works if list has no hidden order).<br><b>Stratified</b>: split population by known characteristics in correct proportions, then sample randomly within each — reduces sampling error.	Topic7 7.1.3 Sampling
Name the non-probability sampling methods.	<b>Convenience</b>: chosen for ease/low cost (exploratory).<br><b>Judgment</b>: chosen on the researcher's judgment as 'representative'.<br><b>Quota</b>: identify characteristics & proportions, then fill quotas via convenience/judgment (non-probability version of stratified).<br><b>Snowball</b>: referrals from initial subjects, used for very rare characteristics (introduces bias).	Topic7 7.1.3 Sampling
What makes a sample unbiased?	It must be <b>representative</b> of the population — i.e. randomly selected and sufficiently large, with members drawn without exclusions.	Topic7 7.1.3 Sampling
Distinguish continuous from discrete data.	<b>Continuous</b>: can take any value in an interval of the real line (e.g. height, weight) — limited only by measurement precision.<br><b>Discrete</b>: can take only a finite number of values; arises from counting (e.g. number of companies in an index).	Topic7 7.1.4 DataTypes
What is categorical data and how is it converted to ordinal data?	<b>Categorical</b>: items coded into categories (e.g. yes=1, no=0); descriptive statistics generally can't be applied.<br><b>Ordinal</b>: categories ranked in an order/scale (e.g. degree classes 1–5). The order conveys information but the actual numbers are arbitrary, so only order statistics like the median apply.	Topic7 7.1.5 DataTypes
What does a frequency distribution show, and what is a relative frequency distribution?	<b>Frequency distribution</b>: lists each value/interval alongside its number of occurrences (frequency).<br><b>Relative frequency</b>: each frequency divided by the total, giving the proportion (or %) in each category.	Topic7 7.1.6 DataPresentation
What does a cumulative frequency distribution tell you?	The proportion of a sample/population taking a value <b>less than or equal to</b> a given number. The final cumulative value is always 100%.	Topic7 7.1.6 DataPresentation
Which charts suit discrete vs continuous data, and how is a pie-chart angle found?	<b>Discrete</b>: pie charts and bar charts.<br><b>Continuous</b>: histogram (area of bar = frequency); group into intervals.<br><b>Pie angle</b> = relative frequency × 360°. E.g. 25% → 0.25 × 360° = 90°.	Topic7 7.1.7 DataPresentation
What is a histogram and why is the bar's area (not height) the frequency?	A histogram groups continuous data into intervals; <b>the area of each bar represents the frequency</b>. If an interval is twice the standard width, its height is halved so the area still correctly represents frequency.	Topic7 7.1.7 DataPresentation
What does a scatter diagram show, and which axis carries which variable?	It plots paired values of two variables to reveal a relationship. By convention the <b>independent (causal) variable</b> goes on the horizontal axis and the <b>dependent variable</b> on the vertical axis. (A trend does not prove causation.)	Topic7 7.1.7 DataPresentation
State the formula for the arithmetic mean of raw data.	$$\bar{x}=\frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i}{n}$$<br>Sum all observations and divide by the number of observations.	Topic7 7.2.1 CentralTendency
How do you calculate the mean from frequency (tabular) data?	$$\bar{x}=\frac{\sum_{i=1}^{m} x_i f_i}{n}$$<br>Multiply each value by its frequency, sum, then divide by total number of observations n. For <b>grouped/interval</b> data use the interval midpoint $m_i$ in place of $x_i$.	Topic7 7.2.1 CentralTendency
Define the median and how it is found for an even-numbered dataset.	The <b>median</b> is the middle-ranked value when data are ordered. With an even number of items, average the two middle values. Interpolation formula for grouped data: $$\text{Median}=L+\frac{n-F}{f}\,W$$ (L = lower boundary of median group, W = group width, F = cumulative frequency before that group, f = frequency in it).	Topic7 7.2.1 CentralTendency
Define the mode.	The <b>most frequently occurring value</b> in a dataset. It need not be unique (a dataset can have two or more modes), and may not exist usefully if all values are distinct.	Topic7 7.2.1 CentralTendency
Define the geometric mean and give its formula.	The <b>nth root of the product of n numbers</b>: $$\left(x_1 \times x_2 \times \dots \times x_n\right)^{1/n}$$ It is always ≤ the arithmetic mean; the gap widens with greater volatility.	Topic7 7.2.1 CentralTendency
How do you calculate the geometric mean return from a series of percentage returns?	Express each return as (1 + r), multiply them, take the nth root, then subtract 1.<br>E.g. returns 10%,2%,8%,1%,4%: $$(1.10\times1.02\times1.08\times1.01\times1.04)^{1/5}=1.0494 \Rightarrow 4.94\%$$	Topic7 7.2.1 CentralTendency
State the relationship between mean, median and mode for symmetric and skewed data.	<b>Symmetric</b>: mean = median = mode.<br><b>Positive (right) skew</b>: mode &lt; median &lt; mean (mean dragged up by the long right tail).<br><b>Negative (left) skew</b>: mean &lt; median &lt; mode.	Topic7 7.2.2 CentralTendency
List four measures of dispersion.	Standard deviation, variance, range, interquartile range (and percentiles/quartiles).	Topic7 7.2.3 Dispersion
State the steps / formula for the (population) standard deviation.	$$\text{SD}=\sqrt{\frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_i-\bar{x})^2}{n}}$$<br>1) find the mean; 2) subtract mean from each value; 3) square and sum; 4) divide by n; 5) square root. The <b>variance</b> is this without the final square root.	Topic7 7.2.3 Dispersion
When do you divide by n versus n−1 for standard deviation?	Use <b>n</b> for a <i>population</i>; use <b>n−1</b> for a <i>sample</i> (because the sample mean is itself an estimate, consuming a degree of freedom).	Topic7 7.2.3 Dispersion
Define range, interquartile range and quartiles.	<b>Range</b> = highest − lowest value (sensitive to extremes).<br><b>Quartiles</b>: Q1 = 25th percentile, Q2 = median, Q3 = 75th percentile.<br><b>Interquartile range</b> = Q3 − Q1 (the middle 50%, excluding extreme values).	Topic7 7.2.3 Dispersion
What is a probability distribution?	A list of all possible outcomes of a variable (or experiment) alongside the relative frequency (probability) of each. All probabilities sum to unity (1), since at least one outcome must occur.	Topic7 7.2.4 Probability
State the key properties of the normal distribution.	Bell-shaped and perfectly <b>symmetrical</b>; mean = median = mode; defined by mean (location) and SD (spread).<br>≈68% of observations within 1 SD of the mean, ≈95% within 2 SD, ≈99% within 3 SD.	Topic7 7.2.4 Probability
In a normal distribution, what proportion lies more than two standard deviations below the mean?	About <b>2.5%</b> (since ~95% lies within ±2 SD, leaving ~5% split between the two tails). 'Fat tails' means extreme events occur more often than the normal distribution predicts.	Topic7 7.2.4 Probability
Define the null and alternative hypotheses.	<b>Null hypothesis (H₀)</b>: the statement being tested (e.g. ERP = 5%, or a coin p = 0.5).<br><b>Alternative hypothesis (H₁ or Hₐ)</b>: the competing claim (a specific value, or simply 'not equal' to the null).	Topic7 7.2.5 Significance
How is a test statistic constructed for a hypothesised mean, and how is it used?	$$\text{Test stat}=\frac{\text{sample estimate}-\text{hypothesised value}}{\text{standard error}}$$ where standard error = sample SD ÷ √n.<br>A large value (e.g. far beyond ±3) means the sample is inconsistent with the null, so we <b>reject H₀</b> at the relevant significance level.	Topic7 7.2.5 Significance
Why does statistical significance matter in investment decisions?	Investment data (e.g. returns) are a sample from a 'true' population. Significance tells us how confident we can be that a sample estimate (e.g. an 8% equity risk premium) is close to the true value — a smaller sample SD gives more confidence.	Topic7 7.2.5 Significance
What are the two measures of correlation covered, and what does each measure?	<b>Rank correlation coefficient</b> ($r_s$ or ρ): strength of association between two <i>ranked</i> variables (monotonic relationship).<br><b>Simple correlation coefficient</b> (ρ or r): co-movement between the <i>actual values</i> of two variables.	Topic7 7.3.1 Correlation
State Spearman's rank correlation formula and its range.	$$\rho=1-\frac{6\sum_{i=1}^{n} d_i^2}{n(n^2-1)}$$ where $d_i$ = difference in paired ranks, n = number of items.<br>Ranges from +1 (perfect positive) through 0 (no association) to −1 (perfect negative).	Topic7 7.3.1 Correlation
What is the least-squares regression line and what does it minimise?	A line of best fit $Y=a+bX$ that <b>minimises the sum of the squared errors</b> (residuals), $e_i=Y_i-\hat{Y}_i$. Squaring gives extra weight to points far from the line.	Topic7 7.3.2 Regression
Give the formulae for the regression coefficients a and b.	$$b=\frac{\text{Cov}(X,Y)}{\text{Var}(X)} \qquad a=\bar{Y}-b\bar{X}$$<br>The regression line always passes through $(\bar{X},\bar{Y})$.	Topic7 7.3.2 Regression
Define R² (coefficient of determination) and interpret it.	$$R^2=1-\frac{SS_e}{SS_y}$$ It is the fraction of variation in Y <b>explained</b> by the regression. R² = r² (square of the correlation coefficient). E.g. r = 0.6 → R² = 0.36, so 36% of variability is explained and 64% unexplained.	Topic7 7.3.2 Regression
What is adjusted R² and why is it used?	As predictor variables are added, ordinary R² always rises. <b>Adjusted R²</b> scales R² to account for the number of predictors, preventing the goodness-of-fit from being overstated by simply adding variables.	Topic7 7.3.2 Regression
How do you forecast a value of Y from a regression equation?	Substitute the known X value into the fitted line: $$\hat{Y}_0=a+bX_0$$<br>You should also check the statistical significance (t-tests) and adjusted R² of the coefficients before relying on the forecast.	Topic7 7.3.3 Regression
Why does correlation not imply causation, and what is data-mining?	A high correlation/regression fit may be coincidental (e.g. BBQ grills & sunglasses both driven by weather). <b>Data-mining</b> is finding empirical relationships between variables with no real underlying causal link — leading to spurious conclusions and unreliable forecasts.	Topic7 7.3.4 Regression
What are the shortfalls of using linear regression for forecasting?	Coefficients (a, b) may change over time ('parameter instability'); the linear form may break down outside the sampled range (extrapolation risk); and the relationship may not be causal or constant. Models that fit one period often 'break down' out of sample.	Topic7 7.3.4 Regression
Describe the impact of extreme events on measures of correlation.	'Outlier' observations (e.g. the 1987 crash) can <b>induce apparent correlation</b> between variables where little exists, or strengthen/weaken a relationship. Including extreme historical events can make relationships look significant when they are not.	Topic7 7.3.5 Regression
What does BLUE stand for and what does each term mean?	<b>Best Linear Unbiased Estimator</b> (Gauss-Markov).<br><b>Unbiased</b>: the mean of the sampling distribution equals the true parameter.<br><b>Best</b>: lowest variance among linear unbiased estimators.	Topic7 7.3.5 Regression
What is the role of financial market indices?	An index measures change in the value of a portfolio of securities. Uses: compare a fund manager's performance to the market or a sector; benchmark positions and managers; time investment decisions; and serve as the underlying for derivative contracts.	Topic7 7.4.1 IndexNumbers
Define a price relative and how to calculate an index level from base/current data.	<b>Price relative</b> = $P_t / P_0$ (current price ÷ base price, adjusted for capital changes).<br><b>Index level</b> = base value (usually 100) × current value ÷ base value. E.g. GDP rising from 10 to 11 → index = 100 × 11/10 = 110.	Topic7 7.4.2 IndexNumbers
How do you rebase an index series to a new base year?	Divide each index value by the index value in the chosen new base year, then multiply by 100: $$\text{Rebased}=\frac{I_t}{I_{RB}}\times 100$$ Percentage changes between years are unaffected by rebasing.	Topic7 7.4.3 IndexNumbers
Give the formula for a simple arithmetic (price-weighted) index.	$$I_t=B\times\frac{\sum_{j=1}^{n} P_{jt}}{\sum_{j=1}^{n} P_{j0}}$$<br>Sum constituent prices now ÷ sum at base date, × base value B (usually 100). This is price-weighted.	Topic7 7.4.4 IndexNumbers
Give the formula for a geometric index (GI) and one key property.	$$I_t=B\times\left[\frac{P_{1t}}{P_{10}}\times\frac{P_{2t}}{P_{20}}\times\dots\times\frac{P_{nt}}{P_{n0}}\right]^{1/n}$$<br>Property: <b>if any one constituent's price falls to zero, the whole GI becomes zero</b>. A GI is always ≤ the equivalent equally-weighted (SAI) value and underestimates portfolio performance.	Topic7 7.4.4 IndexNumbers
Give the formula for a market value-weighted index.	$$I_t=B\times\frac{\sum_{j=1}^{n} N_{jt}P_{jt}}{\sum_{j=1}^{n} N_{j0}P_{j0}}$$<br>where $N_{jt}$ = number of shares of constituent j at time t. Weights by market capitalisation.	Topic7 7.4.4 IndexNumbers
Compare equally-weighted and market value-weighted indices.	<b>Equally-weighted</b>: a 1% move in a tiny firm counts the same as a 1% move in a giant — gives too much weight to small caps.<br><b>Market value-weighted</b>: weights by capitalisation, so larger firms move the index more, better reflecting overall market value changes.	Topic7 7.4.4 IndexNumbers
What is free-float adjustment and what impact does it have versus full market capitalisation?	<b>Free float</b> counts only shares available to trade (excludes insider/government/promoter holdings); a float factor (e.g. 0.80 if 20% closely held) scales the share count.<br>Impact: it more rationally reflects investable market value, reduces concentration in firms with low free float, and is the method used by S&P, MSCI and FTSE.	Topic7 7.4.5 IndexNumbers
How are the FT30, FTSE All-Share, FTSE 100 and S&P 500 constructed?	<b>FT30</b>: equally-weighted geometric mean of 30 UK stocks (no longer widely used).<br><b>FTSE All-Share</b>: market-cap-weighted arithmetic (~98% of UK market).<br><b>FTSE 100</b>: market-cap-weighted arithmetic, ~70% of UK listed value, base 1,000 (end-1983).<br><b>S&P 500</b>: market-cap-weighted arithmetic average.	Topic7 7.4.6 IndexNumbers
How are the Dow Jones Industrial Average, CAC 40, DAX and Nikkei 225 constructed?	<b>DJIA</b>: simple aggregation of unweighted share prices of 30 large US stocks.<br><b>CAC 40 / DAX</b>: arithmetic market-weighted indices.<br><b>Nikkei 225</b>: unweighted (price-based) arithmetic index.	Topic7 7.4.6 IndexNumbers
How do bond market indices differ from most equity indices?	Equity indices usually track price changes only; <b>bond indices often reflect total returns</b> — coupons, price changes and reinvested coupons. A total-return index assumes all distributions are reinvested and is a more accurate performance measure.	Topic7 7.4.6 IndexNumbers
Distinguish simple from compound interest, with the basic formulae.	<b>Simple</b>: interest only on the original principal. After T years: $D_0(1+T\times r)$.<br><b>Compound</b>: interest earned on previous interest too. After T years: $D_T=D_0(1+r)^T$.	Topic7 7.5.1 Interest
What is the compound formula with m compounding periods per year?	$$D_T=D_0\left(1+\frac{r}{m}\right)^{T\times m}$$<br>where r = nominal annual rate, m = compounding frequency, T = years.	Topic7 7.5.2 Interest
Calculate the effective annual compound rate (i) from a nominal rate r compounded m times.	$$1+i=\left(1+\frac{r}{m}\right)^{m}$$<br>E.g. 10% nominal compounded quarterly: $(1+0.10/4)^4-1 = 10.38\%$ effective.	Topic7 7.5.2 Interest
Given an effective annual rate i and frequency m, find the nominal (simple) rate r.	$$r=m\times\left((1+i)^{1/m}-1\right)$$	Topic7 7.5.3 Interest
State the continuous-compounding (effective annual rate) formula.	$$D_T=D_0\,e^{rT}$$<br>where e ≈ 2.718. E.g. £1,000 at 8% continuously for 25 years: $1000\,e^{(0.08)(25)}=1000e^2=£7,389.06$.	Topic7 7.5.4 Interest
State the future value formula for a single sum.	$$D_T=D_0(1+r)^T$$<br>The future value of a present sum compounded at rate r over T periods.	Topic7 7.6.1 TimeValue
State the present value formula and the PV factor for a single sum.	$$PV=\frac{D_T}{(1+r)^T} \qquad \text{PV factor}=\frac{1}{(1+r)^T}$$<br>r is the discount rate. PV translates a future sum to today's money for comparison.	Topic7 7.6.2 TimeValue
What is the present value of a perpetuity paying C per year at discount rate r?	$$PV=\frac{C}{r}$$<br>E.g. £100 per annum forever at 5%: 100 ÷ 0.05 = £2,000.	Topic7 7.6.2 TimeValue
State the general formula linking an initial sum, regular equal payments and a future value.	$$D_T=D_0(1+r)^T+\frac{d\left[(1+r)^T-1\right]}{r}$$<br>where d = the equal annual payment/contribution (negative for withdrawals).	Topic7 7.6.2 TimeValue
How do you find the price (PV) of an annuity?	Set the future value $D_T=0$ and solve for the initial value $D_0$:<br>$$D_0=\frac{-d(1+r)^T+d}{r}\times\frac{1}{(1+r)^T}$$<br>E.g. £2,000/yr for 5 years at 10% → annuity price ≈ £7,582.	Topic7 7.6.2 TimeValue
How do you calculate the equal instalment on a repayment mortgage?	Set $D_T=0$ with $D_0$ = −loan, and solve for d in $$0=D_0(1+r)^T+\frac{d[(1+r)^T-1]}{r}.$$<br>E.g. £50,000 over 25 years at 8% → d ≈ £4,683.94 per year.	Topic7 7.6.3 TimeValue
Define net present value (NPV).	$$NPV=\text{PV of future cash flows}-\text{cost of investment}$$<br>If costs occur over time they are also discounted. A positive NPV adds wealth.	Topic7 7.7.1 NPV_IRR
Define the internal rate of return (IRR).	The discount rate that makes the <b>NPV equal to zero</b> — i.e. the rate equating the PV of an investment's cost with the PV of its cash inflows. For a bond, the IRR is its redemption yield.	Topic7 7.7.1 NPV_IRR
How is the IRR estimated by linear interpolation between two rates?	$$IRR=r_{low}+\left[\frac{NPV_{low}}{NPV_{low}+NPV_{high}}\times(r_{high}-r_{low})\right]$$<br>Pick a rate giving positive NPV and one giving negative NPV, then interpolate.	Topic7 7.7.1 NPV_IRR
When do NPV and IRR give the same accept/reject decision, and when can they conflict?	With <b>unlimited funding</b> and independent projects they agree. They can <b>rank differently</b> when projects are mutually exclusive with different cash-flow timing or different scales.	Topic7 7.7.2 NPV_IRR
Why is NPV preferred to IRR when they conflict, and what are IRR's limitations?	NPV always <b>maximises investor wealth</b> and uses the cost of capital as the reinvestment rate.<br>IRR limitations: assumes reinvestment at the IRR (unrealistic); may be non-unique with alternating cash flows; ignores project scale; cannot handle variable discount rates.	Topic7 7.7.2 NPV_IRR
