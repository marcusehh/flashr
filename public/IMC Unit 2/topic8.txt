What are the three main ways micro-economics assists investment professionals?	(1) Analysing how consumers/firms/markets interact to value securities, sectors and firms; (2) generating forward-looking expectations for asset selection; (3) framing analysis of a firm/industry's current state before projecting forward.	Topic8 8.1.1 Introduction
What four levels of assessment can economic analysis support?	(i) Forecasts of financial market behaviour; (ii) outlook for industries/sectors; (iii) outlook for individual securities; (iv) forecasts of the wider macro-economy and its impact on (i)–(iii).	Topic8 8.1.1 Introduction
What is the major <b>strength</b> of economic theory for analysing market behaviour?	Its rigorous statement of relationships between economic and financial variables, making all underlying <b>assumptions explicit</b> (so weaknesses are transparent).	Topic8 8.1.2 StrengthsWeaknesses
What are the two key <b>assumptions</b> behind much economic analysis, and their weaknesses?	<b>Ceteris paribus</b> — 'all else unchanged' (unlikely given real-world complexity).<br><b>Rationality</b> — agents maximise well-being without systematic error (challenged by <b>behavioural finance</b>, e.g. bubbles).	Topic8 8.1.2 StrengthsWeaknesses
What does the <b>efficient markets</b> idea imply about forecasting errors?	Errors must be <b>random</b>; agents don't systematically repeat them, since a pattern would be spotted and traded away for profit.	Topic8 8.1.2 StrengthsWeaknesses
What are the main applications of micro-economic theory?	Modelling consumer/firm decisions; understanding market structure (competitive vs monopoly); forecasting industry developments; interpreting economic news; coordinating research via common assumptions.	Topic8 8.1.3 Applications
What is the <b>production possibility frontier</b> (PPF), and what do points inside/on/outside it mean?	Max output achievable from fully-employed resources.<br><b>Inside</b> = feasible but inefficient (idle resources); <b>on</b> = efficient; <b>outside</b> = unattainable.	Topic8 8.2.1 DemandSupply
Define <b>opportunity cost</b> via the PPF.	The amount of one good given up to produce one more unit of another (e.g. machines forgone per extra tonne of apples).	Topic8 8.2.1 DemandSupply
Distinguish <b>demand</b> from <b>quantity demanded</b> (and supply likewise).	<b>Demand/supply</b> = the full schedule of quantities at <i>every</i> price.<br><b>Quantity demanded/supplied</b> = the amount at <i>one particular</i> price.	Topic8 8.2.1 DemandSupply
What is the <b>equilibrium price</b>?	The price where quantity demanded = quantity supplied. Above it → excess supply; below it → excess demand.	Topic8 8.2.1 DemandSupply
Distinguish a <b>movement along</b> from a <b>shift in</b> a demand curve.	<b>Movement along</b> = caused by the good's <i>own price</i> (Δ quantity demanded).<br><b>Shift</b> = caused by a <i>non-price factor</i> (Δ demand).	Topic8 8.2.2 DemandSupply
What factors <b>shift</b> the demand curve? The supply curve?	<b>Demand</b>: prices of substitutes/complements, income, tastes, advertising.<br><b>Supply</b>: input prices, technology, taxes/subsidies, production conditions.	Topic8 8.2.3 DemandSupply
Define <b>normal</b>, <b>inferior</b> and <b>Veblen</b> goods.	<b>Normal</b>: demand rises as income rises.<br><b>Inferior</b>: demand falls as income rises (e.g. cheap cuts of meat).<br><b>Veblen</b>: higher price increases appeal ('snob' goods, e.g. luxury watches).	Topic8 8.2.3 DemandSupply
State the <b>own price elasticity of demand</b> formula and the elastic/inelastic split.	$$\text{PED}=\frac{\%\Delta \text{ quantity demanded}}{\%\Delta \text{ price}}$$<br><b>Elastic</b>: |PED| &gt; 1; <b>inelastic</b>: |PED| &lt; 1; <b>unit-elastic</b> = −1.	Topic8 8.2.4 Elasticity
How does <b>cutting price</b> affect total revenue for elastic, inelastic and unit-elastic goods?	<b>Elastic</b> → revenue <b>rises</b>.<br><b>Inelastic</b> → revenue <b>falls</b>.<br><b>Unit-elastic</b> → revenue <b>unchanged</b>.	Topic8 8.2.4 Elasticity
What factors determine own price elasticity, and how does <b>time</b> affect it?	Availability of substitutes, addictiveness, narrowness of definition, share of income, time.<br><b>Time</b>: less elastic short-run (habits persist), more elastic long-run (alternatives found).	Topic8 8.2.4 Elasticity
Define <b>cross-price elasticity</b> and interpret its sign.	$$\text{XED}=\frac{\%\Delta \text{ Q demanded of A}}{\%\Delta \text{ price of B}}$$<br><b>Negative</b> → complements; <b>positive</b> → substitutes.	Topic8 8.2.5 Elasticity
Define <b>income elasticity</b> and classify goods by it.	$$\text{YED}=\frac{\%\Delta \text{ Q demanded}}{\%\Delta \text{ income}}$$<br>Positive = normal, negative = inferior; YED &gt; 1 = luxury, &lt; 1 = necessity. All inferior goods are necessities.	Topic8 8.2.6 Elasticity
What is a <b>Giffen good</b>?	A staple where a price rise <i>raises</i> quantity demanded (upward-sloping demand) because the negative income effect outweighs substitution. Inferior, but not all inferior goods are Giffen.	Topic8 8.2.6 Elasticity
Distinguish <b>explicit (accounting)</b> from <b>opportunity (economic)</b> costs.	<b>Explicit</b>: actual cash disbursements.<br><b>Opportunity</b>: what a resource would earn in its next best use (incl. owner's time/capital).	Topic8 8.3.1 Costs
Define <b>normal</b> and <b>supernormal</b> profit.	<b>Normal</b>: minimum profit needed to keep resources in the industry.<br><b>Supernormal</b>: excess above <i>all</i> costs including all opportunity costs.	Topic8 8.3.2 Costs
Define <b>fixed</b>, <b>variable</b>, <b>marginal</b>, <b>total</b> and <b>average</b> costs.	<b>Fixed</b>: don't vary with output (exist at zero output).<br><b>Variable</b>: vary with output.<br><b>Marginal</b>: cost of one extra unit.<br><b>Total</b> = fixed + variable.<br><b>Average</b> = total ÷ output.	Topic8 8.3.3 Costs
What is the relationship between the <b>MC</b> curve and the <b>AC</b> curve?	If MC &lt; AC, AC is <b>falling</b>; if MC &gt; AC, AC is <b>rising</b>. MC crosses AC at AC's <b>minimum</b>.	Topic8 8.3.4 Costs
Why is the short-run <b>AVC</b> curve U-shaped?	It falls, levels, then rises as efficient use of the fixed factor gives way to <b>diminishing returns</b>.	Topic8 8.3.4 Costs
State the <b>law of diminishing marginal returns</b>.	Adding units of a variable factor (labour) to a fixed factor (capital) eventually lowers marginal product, so MC per unit rises.	Topic8 8.3.5 Costs
Explain <b>MR</b> vs <b>AR</b> for a normal downward-sloping demand curve.	AR = price = the demand curve. <b>MR is always below AR</b> (to sell one more, price falls on <i>all</i> units), and falls twice as steeply for a straight-line demand curve.	Topic8 8.3.6 Costs
State the <b>profit-maximising rule</b>.	Produce where <b>MC = MR</b>. Profit grows while MR &gt; MC and falls once MC &gt; MR.	Topic8 8.3.7 Costs
State the <b>short-run</b> shut-down vs continue decision.	Produce where MR = SRMC provided price (AR) covers <b>average variable cost</b>; shut down if price &lt; SRAVC. Ignore fixed/sunk costs.	Topic8 8.3.8 Costs
State the <b>long-run</b> condition for producing at all.	Produce where MR = LRMC only if <b>LRAC &lt; price (AR)</b>, so average total costs are covered; otherwise not viable.	Topic8 8.3.8 Costs
Define the <b>short run</b>, the <b>long run</b> and a <b>production function</b>.	<b>Short run</b>: some inputs fixed.<br><b>Long run</b>: all inputs variable (no fixed costs).<br><b>Production function</b>: max output achievable from a given set of inputs.	Topic8 8.3.9 Costs
Define <b>economies of scale</b>, <b>MES</b> and <b>diseconomies of scale</b>.	<b>Economies</b>: falling LRAC (increasing returns).<br><b>MES</b>: lowest output at which LRAC is minimised.<br><b>Diseconomies</b>: rising LRAC (decreasing returns).	Topic8 8.3.10 Costs
Why is the <b>LRAC</b> curve U-shaped?	Economies of scale (spreading minimum-size costs, cheaper large machinery, specialisation) lower it; beyond MES, diseconomies (bureaucracy, extra management) raise it.	Topic8 8.3.10 Costs
Explain the <b>LRMC–LRAC</b> relationship and its link to productive efficiency.	LRMC crosses LRAC at LRAC's lowest point (the <b>MES</b>) — the lowest-cost, productively efficient output level.	Topic8 8.3.11 Costs
List the conditions that characterise a <b>perfectly competitive (price-taker)</b> market.	Many small firms; homogeneous product; good product information; free entry/exit. No buyer or seller can influence price.	Topic8 8.4.1 PerfectComp
What does the demand curve facing a <b>single price-taker</b> look like, and what does it imply for MR/AR?	<b>Horizontal</b> (perfectly elastic) at market price. Therefore <b>MR = AR = price</b>, so profit-max output is where MC = price.	Topic8 8.4.1 PerfectComp
Explain <b>long-run equilibrium</b> for a price-taker and the mechanism enforcing it.	Price = minimum LRAC, earning only <b>normal profit</b>. Supernormal profit attracts entrants (↑ supply, ↓ price); losses cause exit (↓ supply, ↑ price) — free entry/exit drives price to min LRAC.	Topic8 8.4.2 PerfectComp
What is a price-taker's <b>short-run supply curve</b>?	Its <b>SRMC curve above the SRAVC curve</b>.	Topic8 8.4.4 PerfectComp
Describe the <b>long-run supply curve</b> for a perfectly competitive industry.	The LRMC curve above LRATC — <b>flatter</b> than short-run supply (all factors vary, firms can enter); the extreme case (identical-cost firms) is horizontal.	Topic8 8.4.5 PerfectComp
State the <b>shut-down decision</b> for a price-taker facing losses.	<b>Short run</b>: continue if price covers AVC (fixed costs paid anyway); shut if price &lt; SRAVC.<br><b>Long run</b>: produce only if price covers ATC; else exit.	Topic8 8.4.6 PerfectComp
Define <b>elasticity of supply</b> and the two extreme cases.	$$\text{PES}=\frac{\%\Delta \text{ Q supplied}}{\%\Delta \text{ price}}$$<br>Horizontal = infinite (perfectly elastic); vertical = zero (perfectly inelastic).	Topic8 8.4.7 PerfectComp
On what does supply elasticity depend, and why is it higher long-run?	On the <b>flexibility of factors of production</b>. Higher in the long run as techniques and organisation can be adjusted more cost-effectively.	Topic8 8.4.7 PerfectComp
List the conditions of a <b>pure monopoly</b>.	One supplier (industry = firm), facing the whole downward-sloping market demand. Both AR and MR fall, and <b>MR is always below AR</b>.	Topic8 8.4.8 Monopoly
Why does a monopolist always produce on the <b>elastic</b> part of demand, and why is there <b>no supply curve</b>?	MR = MC and MC &gt; 0, so MR must be positive → demand elastic.<br><b>No supply curve</b>: output depends on the demand curve faced, so one price can map to different quantities.	Topic8 8.4.8 Monopoly
Compare <b>monopoly vs perfect competition</b> on price, output and efficiency.	Monopoly restricts output and charges a <b>higher price</b>, earning supernormal profit = (price − AC) × quantity. It does <b>not</b> produce at minimum AC, so is productively inefficient (welfare loss).	Topic8 8.4.9 Monopoly
What is a <b>natural monopoly</b>?	A firm with falling LRACs over a large output range (e.g. gas, water, electricity) — costly to build the network, cheap to add each user, so competing networks are wasteful.	Topic8 8.4.9 Monopoly
Define <b>price discrimination</b> and its conditions.	Charging different customers different prices for the same good. Needs market power, separable groups with different elasticities, and no resale (e.g. airline business vs tourist fares).	Topic8 8.4.10 Monopoly
Describe <b>monopolistic competition</b>.	Many fairly small firms; easy entry/exit; differentiated products giving limited pricing power. Long-run profit = zero (ATC tangent to AR) but <b>excess capacity</b> remains.	Topic8 8.4.11 Oligopoly
Describe <b>oligopoly</b> and name some models of it.	Few firms, significant entry barriers, interdependent behaviour. Models: cartel (explicit collusion), kinked demand, price leadership, game theory (prisoner's dilemma), contestable markets.	Topic8 8.4.11 Oligopoly
What is the <b>kinked demand</b> model and what is <b>predatory pricing</b>?	<b>Kinked demand</b>: rivals follow price <i>cuts</i> but not <i>rises</i> → price rigidity.<br><b>Predatory pricing</b>: a large firm temporarily selling below cost to drive out rivals (illegal).	Topic8 8.4.11 Oligopoly
What is a <b>contestable market</b>?	One with near-costless entry/exit, so even large firms behave competitively — prices pushed to LRAC and profits cannot persist.	Topic8 8.4.11 Oligopoly
How do business cycles affect <b>relative industry performance</b> (bull-market sequence)?	Upturn: (1) transport &amp; energy lead; (2) credit cyclicals &amp; technology; (3) consumer growth/cyclicals; (4) capital goods &amp; financials peak. At the top → basic industries, then defensives (consumer staples, utilities).	Topic8 8.4.12 BusinessCycles
Why is the business-cycle/stock-market link hard to exploit?	No two cycles are alike; lead/lag relationships (GDP, rates, returns) vary; sector composition evolves; and forecasts may not be accurate enough to be worthwhile.	Topic8 8.4.12 BusinessCycles
List <b>Porter's five forces</b>.	(1) Bargaining power of suppliers; (2) bargaining power of buyers; (3) threat of new entrants; (4) threat of substitutes; (5) rivalry between competitors.	Topic8 8.4.13 Porter
How do Porter's five forces influence <b>industry profitability</b>?	Buyer power &amp; substitutes affect <b>prices</b>; supplier power affects <b>input costs</b>; rivalry affects prices and costs (advertising/R&amp;D); threat of entry <b>limits pricing power</b> and influences investment.	Topic8 8.4.13 Porter
Name the phases of the <b>product life cycle</b>.	(1) Introduction (slow sales, low/negative profit); (2) Growth (rapid sales, rising profit, attracts rivals); (3) Maturity (established, economies of scale, falling promotion); (4) Decline (lost share/profit); (5) Obsolescence (profits disappear).	Topic8 8.4.14 ProductLifeCycle
What are the main <b>limitations</b> of the product life cycle model?	Assumes no reversion to old preferences (ignores revivals); assumes no relaunch; ignores multi-product balancing; weak for prediction (can't tell temporary dip from real decline).	Topic8 8.4.14 ProductLifeCycle
What does <b>SWOT</b> stand for and how are elements categorised?	<b>S</b>trengths, <b>W</b>eaknesses (internal/firm-specific); <b>O</b>pportunities, <b>T</b>hreats (external environment).	Topic8 8.4.15 SWOT
What is the <b>role</b> of SWOT analysis?	To raise awareness of all internal/external factors affecting strategic planning and decision-making; usable beyond firms (government, education, personal).	Topic8 8.4.15 SWOT
List the <b>four Ps</b> of the marketing mix.	<b>Product</b> (fit for purpose), <b>Place</b> (available where customers shop), <b>Promotion</b> (appropriate communication), <b>Price</b> (good value).	Topic8 8.4.16 MarketingMix
What three extra Ps make the <b>seven P</b> model, and why?	<b>People</b>, <b>Process</b>, <b>Physical evidence</b> — added to reflect the importance of <b>services</b> (e.g. waiters, meal delivery, the food itself).	Topic8 8.4.16 MarketingMix
